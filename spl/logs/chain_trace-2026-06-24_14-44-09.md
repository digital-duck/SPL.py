### 子 (16.8s)

## 子

**Everyday example.**
Think of a toddler with arms stretched wide, eager to be picked up. That little figure — head, body, and two reaching arms — is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew **子** (pronounced *zǐ*). It means **child** or **son**, and it is one of the most common building blocks in the Chinese writing system.

**Picture this.**
Look at the character 子 and imagine a simple stick figure of a baby: the top horizontal stroke is the head, the vertical stroke is the body, and the curving bottom strokes suggest tiny legs wrapped in a swaddling cloth. In its oldest form — carved on oracle bones over 3,000 years ago — the resemblance to a small child was even clearer, with a round head and outstretched arms.

**Simple rule.**
Whenever you see 子 tucked inside a larger character, it usually adds a meaning connected to *children*, *smallness*, *birth*, or *learning*. For example: **好** (hǎo, "good") pairs 女 (woman) with 子 (child) — a mother and child together convey the idea of goodness. In everyday Mandarin, 子 also works as a common noun suffix, turning words like 桌 (table) into 桌子 (zhuōzi, "a table").

**Try it yourself.**
Spot the 子 component hiding inside these characters: 学 (xué, "to learn"), 孙 (sūn, "grandchild"), and 存 (cún, "to exist"). For each one, ask: how does the idea of "child" or "seed" connect to the larger meaning?

### 女 (16.5s)

## 女

**Everyday example.** Think about how a stick figure sits cross-legged on the floor — arms gently crossed, one leg tucked beneath the other. That graceful, kneeling posture is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character 女 (*nǚ*), meaning **woman** or **female**.

**Picture this.** Look at 女 carefully. The long diagonal stroke sweeping left is like one arm reaching across the body. The second stroke curves from upper right down to lower left — the other arm crossing over. The flat horizontal stroke at the bottom is the ground she kneels on, or a lap seen from the side. Early forms of this character, carved on oracle bones over 3,000 years ago, showed a person kneeling with hands clasped in front — a posture of composure and dignity.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 女 tucked into the left side of a larger character, it usually signals that the word's meaning connects to women, mothers, or family relationships. For example: 妈 (mā, mother), 姐 (jiě, older sister), and 好 (hǎo, good — a woman 女 beside a child 子). This left-side role is so common that 女 is called a **radical** — a recurring building block that hints at a character's meaning.

**Try it yourself.** Write 女 three times slowly. Notice the crossover point where the first two strokes meet. Then spot 女 hiding inside these characters: 她, 妹, 安. Can you guess which ones relate to family?

### 宀 (17.2s)

## 宀

**Everyday example.** Think about what a roof does — it covers and protects everything underneath it. When it rains, you step inside and the roof keeps you dry. That simple idea of "shelter from above" is exactly what the Chinese component 宀 captures.

**Picture this.** Look at the shape: 宀. It resembles a small peaked roof seen from the front — a slanted line on each side meeting at a dot on top, like a tiny house outline. Now imagine sliding different things underneath that roof. Put a person under it and you get 安 (ān, "peace") — a woman safe at home. Put a pig under it and you get 家 (jiā, "family, home") — because in ancient China, a household with livestock meant prosperity. The roof stays the same; what lives beneath it changes the meaning.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 宀 sitting on top of a character, think **"shelter, roof, dwelling."** It almost always signals that the word relates to houses, rooms, protection, or the idea of being enclosed and safe. Characters like 室 (room), 宝 (treasure — something precious kept under a roof), and 字 (character — a child nurtured under a roof) all follow this pattern.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 守 (shǒu, "to guard"). Can you spot the 宀 on top? What might "guarding" have to do with a roof? Think of a watchman protecting a home — that's the story this character tells.

### 好 (18.0s)

## 好

**Everyday example.**
When a friend asks "How are you?", the simplest reply in Chinese is just one syllable: *hǎo* — "good." You hear it in greetings (*nǐ hǎo* 你好, "hello"), in approval (*hǎo de* 好的, "okay"), and whenever something is pleasant or well-done. It is one of the most frequently used characters in the language.

**Picture this.**
Look at the character 好 and split it down the middle. On the left stands 女 (*nǚ*, "woman"). On the right stands 子 (*zǐ*, "child"). A woman beside her child — an image ancient writers associated with happiness, harmony, and goodness. Two familiar elements, side by side, create a new meaning greater than either part alone.

**Simple rule.**
好 is a *compound ideograph*: a character built by combining two simpler characters whose meanings together suggest the new word. Whenever you see an unfamiliar character, ask: *Can I spot smaller pieces I already know?* Here, recognizing 女 and 子 inside 好 gives you both a memory hook and a window into how Chinese writing thinks in pictures.

**Try it yourself.**
Cover the right half of 好 — can you name the radical? Now cover the left half. Practice writing the full character stroke by stroke: 女 first (three strokes), then 子 (three strokes). Say *hǎo* aloud each time you finish. Tomorrow, greet someone with 你好 and notice how naturally 好 flows off the pen.

### 字 (17.9s)

## 字

**Everyday example.** Think about how a family grows: a child is born under a roof, and the household gains a new member. In ancient China, people saw something similar happening with writing — new symbols were "born" by combining simpler pieces together, and each one carried meaning that could be passed down for generations.

**Picture this.** The character 字 is built from two parts stacked vertically. On top sits 宀, a small pictograph of a roof or shelter. Underneath is 子, meaning "child." Put them together and the original image is vivid: *a child under a roof* — something new being nurtured in a protected space. Over time, this idea of "giving birth to meaning" shifted to refer to the written symbols themselves. Today, 字 simply means **character** — the basic unit of Chinese writing.

**Simple rule.** When you see 字, remember the formula:

$$\text{字} = \text{宀 (roof)} + \text{子 (child)}$$

A character is a small, self-contained unit of meaning — like a child that has grown up and can stand on its own. Every Chinese character, no matter how complex, is ultimately assembled from a handful of simpler building blocks, just as 字 itself is assembled from two.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 学 (xué, "to learn"). Can you spot 子 hiding at the bottom? What does that suggest about the connection between children, learning, and written characters?

### vocabulary_compounds (49.6s)

## Vocabulary Compounds

**Everyday example.** Think about how English builds longer words from shorter ones: *sun* + *flower* = *sunflower*, *book* + *shelf* = *bookshelf*. Each piece carries meaning, and together they create something new yet guessable. Chinese does exactly the same thing — but because every character already represents a meaning, compound-building is the *primary* way the language grows its vocabulary.

**Picture this.** You already know the character 火 (*huǒ*, fire). Now meet one new building block: 山 (*shān*, mountain). Place them side by side — 火山 (*huǒshān*) — and you get *volcano*: a mountain of fire. Neither character changed its shape or pronunciation; they simply joined forces. The logic is transparent once you recognise the parts.

**Simple rule.** A *vocabulary compound* is a word made of two or more characters whose individual meanings combine to express a new concept. The pattern is often modifier + head: in 火山, the modifier 火 tells you *what kind* of mountain, and the head 山 tells you *what it is*. Knowing the building blocks lets you *predict* meanings you have never formally studied.

**Try it yourself.** Using 山 and a character you already know — 水 (*shuǐ*, water) — what might 山水 (*shānshuǐ*) mean? Mountain + water. Hint: it names a famous style of Chinese landscape painting. Guess first, then look it up!
