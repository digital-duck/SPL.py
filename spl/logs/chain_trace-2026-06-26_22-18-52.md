## Position

When you use a GPS device, you are calculating your position—a precise location relative to an established global origin. This concept is fundamental to describing motion and change in physics. If we ignore vertical movement for simplicity, the simplest model of position treats an object's location as a single coordinate value on a number line. Mathematically, the position ($\vec{x}$) is defined by this coordinate. It answers the question: "Where are you right now?" Understanding position requires establishing a reference point (the origin) and a directionality (a positive axis).

**Definition:** Position $\vec{x}$ is a vector quantity that specifies an object's location relative to a chosen origin within a defined coordinate system. In one dimension, it is represented by the coordinate $x$. If we move from an initial position $\vec{x}_i$ to a final position $\vec{x}_f$, we define the change in position ($\Delta \vec{x}$) as the difference between these two endpoints:
$$\Delta \vec{x} = \vec{x}_f - \vec{x}_i$$

**Worked Example:** A particle starts at $x_i = 2.0$ meters on a track and moves to a final position of $x_f = 7.5$ meters. We calculate the displacement (change in position):
$$\Delta x = 7.5 \text{ m} - 2.0 \text{ m} = 5.5 \text{ m}$$
The particle's change in position is $5.5$ meters, regardless of how complex its actual path was between these two points.

**Why it’s true:** The linearity of the coordinate system allows us to use simple subtraction. Position is inherently defined relative to a fixed origin (often set at $x=0$). By subtracting the initial location from the final location, we are mathematically isolating only the net change in coordinates—the displacement. This definition holds because all motion can be resolved along these perpendicular axes.

### time (16.4s)

## Time

We experience time constantly, whether we are timing cooking ingredients or tracking planetary orbits. It seems like an invisible dimension that always moves forward. But what exactly *is* time in a mathematical sense? We often treat it as a simple counter—a number that increases uniformly—but formal physics and mathematics require us to define its structure precisely enough to model change itself. Understanding this definition is the crucial first step toward mastering temporal dynamics, allowing you to move beyond mere elapsed duration into the structured analysis of causality.

**Definition:** Mathematically, time ($t$) is typically modeled as a one-dimensional real parameter that orders events and specifies their sequence. It is usually represented by an increasing function $t: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, where positive values represent movement forward from a defined origin point ($t=0$). The fundamental property of time is its irreversible flow, meaning processes generally move only in the direction of increasing $t$.

**Worked Example:** Consider monitoring the temperature change ($\Delta T$) of coffee over time. We can model this relationship as a function $T(t)$, where $t$ is measured in minutes and $T$ is measured in degrees Celsius. If we know the initial temperature $T_0$ at $t=0$, and the cooling rate follows Newton's Law of Cooling, the change over time must be solved using differential equations: $\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_{ambient})$. By integrating this equation, we define how the dependent variable $T$ changes as an explicit function of the independent parameter $t$.

**Why it's true:** Time acts as a fundamental coordinate system because physical laws (like conservation of energy) are formulated by describing how state variables change *with respect to* time. Our mathematical model must therefore treat time not just as measurement, but as the scaffolding that connects cause and effect. The directionality of the arrow of time ensures that our solutions only proceed forward in $t$.

**Practice Problem:** If a particle's position is described by $x(t) = 3t^2 + t$, what is its velocity function? Using differentiation, calculate $\frac{dx}{dt}$ and determine the instant when the particle's velocity reaches $15$ units per second.

### vector (15.8s)

## Vector

Imagine you are giving directions to a friend across town. Simply saying "go east" is vague; they need to know how far *and* in what direction to go. In physics and mathematics, many quantities—like speed or temperature—are fine with just magnitude (a number). However, quantities like force or displacement require both magnitude and direction information. These are the job of **vectors**. A vector is a mathematical object that possesses both size (magnitude) and orientation (direction). We typically represent vectors graphically using arrows: the length of the arrow represents the magnitude, and the way it points represents the direction. Mathematically, we usually write a vector $\mathbf{v}$ in component form, such as $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_x, v_y \rangle$ in two dimensions, where $v_x$ is the change in the horizontal direction and $v_y$ is the change in the vertical direction.

To calculate the magnitude (or length) of a vector $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_x, v_y \rangle$, we use the Pythagorean theorem:
$$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{v_x^2 + v_y^2}$$

Consider an airplane flying from Point A to Point B. If the plane travels 300 km east (the $x$-component) and then 400 km north (the $y$-component), its total displacement vector $\mathbf{d}$ is $\langle 300, 400 \rangle$. The magnitude of this vector represents the straight-line distance from A to B. Calculating this requires finding:
$$\|\mathbf{d}\| = \sqrt{(300)^2 + (400)^2} = \sqrt{90000 + 160000} = \sqrt{250000} = 500 \text{ km}$$

The magnitude is the hypotenuse of the right triangle formed by its components. This calculation is fundamental because it allows us to quantify the "size" of a vector regardless of how many dimensions we are working in, establishing a rigorous link between geometric measurement and algebraic computation.

### displacement (19.0s)

## Displacement

Imagine you are taking a scenic hike through a valley. You walk three kilometers due east, then turn north and walk another four kilometers. If someone asked you how far you traveled, you would add $3\text{ km} + 4\text{ km}$ to get $7\text{ km}$. However, if they ask for your *displacement*, they are only asking for the straight-line distance from where you started (your origin) to where you finished. Displacement is a vector quantity that encapsulates this net change in position, regardless of the path taken.

Formally, displacement ($\Delta \mathbf{x}$) is defined as the difference between the final position ($\mathbf{r}_f$) and the initial position ($\mathbf{r}_i$). Since position vectors are measured from a fixed origin (the reference point), we can write this relationship using vector subtraction:
$$\Delta \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{r}_f - \mathbf{r}_i$$
Displacement is inherently directional; it must be described both by its magnitude and its direction. If the initial position is $\mathbf{r}_i = (x_i, y_i)$ and the final position is $\mathbf{r}_f = (x_f, y_f)$, then the displacement vector is found by analyzing the net change in each coordinate:
$$\Delta \mathbf{x} = (\Delta x, \Delta y) = (x_f - x_i)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + (y_f - y_i)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}$$

**Worked Example:** A drone starts at coordinates $(0, 5)$ meters and is programmed to fly to the point $(12, 3)$ meters. What is its displacement?
We identify the initial position $\mathbf{r}_i = (0, 5)\,\text{m}$ and the final position $\mathbf{r}_f = (12, 3)\,\text{m}$. The displacement vector is:
$$\Delta \mathbf{x} = (12 - 0)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + (3 - 5)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} = 12\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} - 2\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}\,\text{m}$$
The magnitude of the displacement is found using the Pythagorean theorem: $|\Delta \mathbf{x}| = \sqrt{(12)^2 + (-2)^2} = \sqrt{148}\,\text{m}$.

**Why it's true:** The definition $\Delta \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{r}_f - \mathbf{r}_i$ works because vector addition and subtraction are linear operations. By subtracting the initial position coordinates from the final position coordinates, we isolate only the net change components ($\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$), which by geometric definition constitutes the shortest straight-line path (the hypotenuse) between the two points in the coordinate system.

### elapsed_time (13.7s)

## Elapsed Time

Have you ever been late because you underestimated how long a task would take? Calculating elapsed time is one of the most practical skills in mathematics, allowing us to accurately measure the duration between two points in time. It helps us manage schedules, estimate travel times, and even plan cooking meals! If we only know the start time and the end time, determining *how long* that process took requires understanding the concept of elapsed time.

**Definition:** Elapsed time is the total amount of time that passes between a specific starting point (the initial time) and an ending point (the final time). Mathematically, if $T_{start}$ is the start time and $T_{end}$ is the end time, the elapsed duration $\Delta T$ must be calculated by finding the difference:
$$\Delta T = T_{end} - T_{start}$$

**Worked Example:** A movie started at 4:15 PM and finished at 6:00 PM. What was the elapsed time? We can break this down into segments. From 4:15 PM to 5:00 PM is $45$ minutes. From 5:00 PM to 6:00 PM is $1$ hour. Therefore, the total elapsed time is $1 \text{ hour} + 45 \text{ minutes}$, or $\mathbf{1 \text{ hour and } 45 \text{ minutes}}$.

**Why It's True:** While simple subtraction works for consistent units (e.g., finding the difference between 8:00 and 9:00 is $1$ hour), elapsed time often crosses boundaries, such as crossing an hour mark or a day boundary. To ensure accuracy, we must first convert all times into a standard unit (like minutes since midnight) before performing subtraction if standard clock arithmetic fails. The principle remains the same: duration is always measured by subtracting the initial value from the final value.

**Practice Problem:** A train departed at 7:30 AM and arrived at its destination at 11:50 AM. Calculate the total elapsed time in hours and minutes.

Imagine driving across town. You might speed up and slow down constantly, stopping at traffic lights and accelerating onto highways. If someone asks you what your average speed was for the entire journey, they aren't interested in how fast you were going at any single moment; they want a single number that represents the overall rate of change of your position from start to finish. This concept introduces **average velocity**, which tells us exactly how quickly and in what direction your net displacement occurred over a time interval. It is fundamentally a measure of total movement, not instantaneous speed.

Mathematically, average velocity ($\vec{v}_{avg}$) is defined as the total displacement ($\Delta \vec{x}$) divided by the total elapsed time $(\Delta t)$. Since displacement is inherently a vector (meaning it has both magnitude and direction), average velocity must also be a vector quantity. The formal relationship is:
$$\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{x}_f - \vec{x}_i}{t_f - t_i}$$

## Kinematic Equations Constant Acceleration

Imagine driving a car that is braking smoothly, bringing it to a perfect stop over a measured distance. To understand how far you travel and how quickly your speed changes during this process, we rely on kinematic equations. These tools are fundamental for analyzing motion when an object experiences constant acceleration—meaning the rate of change of its velocity remains steady.

**Definition:** Kinematic equations relate displacement ($\Delta x$), initial velocity ($u$), final velocity ($v$), time ($t$), and constant acceleration ($a$). They assume continuous, uniform changes in speed. The primary equations are:
$$v = u + at$$
$$\Delta x = \frac{1}{2}(u + v)t$$
$$\Delta x = ut + \frac{1}{2}at^2$$

## Problem Solving Kinematics

Have you ever watched a baseball pitcher throw a ball or seen a car brake suddenly? These movements involve predictable changes in position over time—they are governed by kinematics. While often taught as simple equations, mastering problem-solving kinematics is about developing a systematic approach to translating physical scenarios into solvable mathematical models. It moves beyond simply plugging numbers into formulas; it requires structured reasoning to select the correct tools for the job.

**Definition:** Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of points, objects, and systems without considering the forces that cause the motion (that is the domain of dynamics). When solving kinematic problems, we focus on relating position ($\Delta x$), velocity ($\Delta v$), acceleration ($\Delta a$), and time ($\Delta t$). The core tools are the constant-acceleration equations.

**Worked Example:** A runner starts from rest at the starting line and accelerates uniformly to a final velocity of $10 \text{ m/s}$ over a distance of $50 \text{ meters}$. What is the time taken for this sprint?
*   Given: Initial velocity ($v_i$) = $0 \text{ m/s}$; Final velocity ($v_f$) = $10 \text{ m/s}$; Displacement ($\Delta x$) = $50 \text{ m}$. Find: Time ($\Delta t$).
*   We use the equation: $v_f^2 = v_i^2 + 2a\Delta x$ (to find acceleration first, as it's required for time).
*   $(10 \text{ m/s})^2 = (0)^2 + 2(a)(50 \text{ m})$
*   $100 = 100a \implies a = 1.0 \text{ m/s}^2$.
*   Now use: $v_f = v_i + a\Delta t$:
*   $10 \text{ m/s} = 0 + (1.0 \text{ m/s}^2)\Delta t \implies \Delta t = 10 \text{ s}$.

**Why it's true:** The structured approach requires identifying known variables and the unknown variable first, which dictates the necessary equation(s). By systematically eliminating unnecessary variables through intermediate steps (like finding acceleration before time), we ensure all physical constraints are met using the appropriate mathematical relationships derived from calculus.
