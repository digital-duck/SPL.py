### function (0.1s)

## Function

**Definition.** A *function* $f$ from a set $A$ to a set $B$, written $f: A \to B$, is a rule that assigns to each element $x \in A$ exactly one element $f(x) \in B$. The set $A$ is called the *domain* of $f$, and $B$ is called the *codomain*. The *range* (or image) of $f$ is the subset $\{f(x) \mid x \in A\} \subseteq B$. The defining property is uniqueness: if $x = y$ then $f(x) = f(y)$. A relation that assigns more than one output to some input is not a function.

**Worked Example.** Let $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined by $f(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2$. To evaluate $f$ at $x = 4$:

$$f(4) = 4^2 - 3(4) + 2 = 16 - 12 + 2 = 6.$$

To find the range, complete the square: $f(x) = \left(x - \tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \tfrac{1}{4}$. Since a square is always non-negative, $f(x) \geq -\tfrac{1}{4}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$, and every value $y \geq -\tfrac{1}{4}$ is achieved. Thus the range is $\left[-\tfrac{1}{4}, \infty\right)$, which is strictly smaller than the codomain $\mathbb{R}$.

**Key Theorem (Composition and Injectivity).** Let $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$. Their *composition* $g \circ f: A \to C$ is defined by $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$. A function $f$ is *injective* (one-to-one) if $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$ implies $x_1 = x_2$, and *surjective* (onto) if every element of $B$ is in the range of $f$. A function that is both injective and surjective is *bijective*, and bijections admit an inverse function $f^{-1}: B \to A$ satisfying $f^{-1}(f(x)) = x$ for all $x \in A$. Proof that injectivity is preserved under composition follows directly: if $g(f(x_1)) = g(f(x_2))$, injectivity of $g$ gives $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$, then injectivity of $f$ gives $x_1 = x_2$.

### limit (0.2s)

## Limit

**Definition.** Let $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function defined on a set $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, and let $a$ be an accumulation point of $D$. We say that the **limit of $f$ as $x$ approaches $a$** equals $L \in \mathbb{R}$, written

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L,$$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x \in D$ satisfying $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$. This is the classical **$\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition** due to Weierstrass. Crucially, $f$ need not be defined at $a$ itself; the limit describes the function's *tendency*, not its value.

**Worked Example.** We verify that $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2}(3x - 1) = 5$.

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, we need $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$ implies $|(3x-1) - 5| < \varepsilon$. Simplifying the target inequality:

$$|(3x - 1) - 5| = |3x - 6| = 3|x - 2|.$$

We require $3|x - 2| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $|x - 2| < \varepsilon/3$. Choosing $\delta = \varepsilon/3$, we have: if $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$, then $|f(x) - 5| = 3|x - 2| < 3\delta = \varepsilon$. $\square$

**Key Theorem (Uniqueness of Limits).** *If $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = M$, then $L = M$.*

*Proof sketch.* Suppose $L \neq M$. Set $\varepsilon = |L - M|/2 > 0$. By hypothesis, there exist $\delta_1, \delta_2 > 0$ such that $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ and $|f(x) - M| < \varepsilon$ simultaneously for $x$ within $\min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$ of $a$. The triangle inequality then gives $|L - M| \leq |f(x) - L| + |f(x) - M| < 2\varepsilon = |L - M|$, a contradiction.

### derivative (0.2s)

## Derivative

**Definition.** Let $f : (a, b) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function and let $x_0 \in (a, b)$. The *derivative* of $f$ at $x_0$ is defined as the limit

$$f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h},$$

provided this limit exists and is finite. When $f'(x_0)$ exists, $f$ is said to be *differentiable* at $x_0$. Geometrically, $f'(x_0)$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(x_0, f(x_0))$. Analytically, it measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ with respect to its argument.

**Worked Example.** Let $f(x) = x^3$. We compute $f'(2)$ directly from the definition:

$$f'(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(2+h)^3 - 2^3}{h}.$$

Expanding $(2+h)^3 = 8 + 12h + 6h^2 + h^3$ and subtracting $8$ gives

$$f'(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{12h + 6h^2 + h^3}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \left(12 + 6h + h^2\right) = 12.$$

Thus the tangent line to $y = x^3$ at $x = 2$ has slope $12$, with equation $y - 8 = 12(x - 2)$.

**Key Theorem (Differentiability Implies Continuity).** *If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.*

*Proof sketch.* Write $f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0) = \dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h} \cdot h$. As $h \to 0$, the first factor converges to $f'(x_0)$ and the second factor converges to $0$, so the product converges to $0$, establishing continuity. The converse fails: $f(x) = |x|$ is continuous at $0$ but not differentiable there, since the left- and right-hand limits of the difference quotient are $-1$ and $1$, respectively.

### continuity (46.7s)

## Continuity

**Definition.** Let $f : A \subseteq \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $c \in A$. We say $f$ is *continuous at $c$* if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that

$$|x - c| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(c)| < \varepsilon.$$

Equivalently, $\lim_{x \to c} f(x) = f(c)$, which bundles three simultaneous requirements: the limit exists, $f(c)$ is defined, and the two values agree. A function is *continuous on $A$* if it is continuous at every point of $A$.

---

**Worked Example.** Show that $f(x) = 3x - 1$ is continuous at $c = 2$.

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, we need $|f(x) - f(2)| < \varepsilon$. Compute:

$$|f(x) - f(2)| = |(3x-1) - 5| = 3|x - 2|.$$

Set $\delta = \varepsilon / 3$. Then whenever $|x - 2| < \delta$,

$$|f(x) - f(2)| = 3|x - 2| < 3\delta = \varepsilon.$$

Because $c = 2$ was arbitrary, the same argument applies at every real number, so $f$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$. Linear functions are always continuous; no holes or jumps can occur in a straight line.

---

**Key Theorem (Intermediate Value Theorem).** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $k$ is any value strictly between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ such that $f(c) = k$.

*Proof sketch.* Define $S = \{x \in [a,b] : f(x) \leq k\}$. Since $a \in S$, the set is non-empty and bounded above by $b$, so $c = \sup S$ exists. Using continuity of $f$ at $c$ and a squeeze argument on neighborhoods of $c$, one shows $f(c) = k$. The completeness of $\mathbb{R}$ is essential; the theorem fails over $\mathbb{Q}$.

---

### partial_derivative (13.6s)

## Partial Derivative

**Definition.** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function of $n$ variables $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n$. The *partial derivative* of $f$ with respect to $x_i$ at a point $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ is defined as the limit

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a_1, \ldots, a_i + h, \ldots, a_n) - f(a_1, \ldots, a_n)}{h},$$

provided the limit exists. Geometrically, $\partial f / \partial x_i$ measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ as only $x_i$ varies while all other variables are held fixed. This is equivalent to restricting $f$ to the line parallel to the $x_i$-axis through $\mathbf{a}$ and computing the ordinary derivative along that line.

**Worked Example.** Let $f(x, y) = x^3 y^2 + \sin(xy)$. To compute $\partial f / \partial x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 3x^2 y^2 + y\cos(xy).$$

Similarly, treating $x$ as a constant:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 2x^3 y + x\cos(xy).$$

Evaluating at the point $(1, \pi)$ gives $\partial f / \partial x \big|_{(1,\pi)} = 3\pi^2 + \pi\cos(\pi) = 3\pi^2 - \pi$.

**Key Theorem (Clairaut–Schwarz).** If $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ has second-order partial derivatives $\partial^2 f / \partial x \, \partial y$ and $\partial^2 f / \partial y \, \partial x$ that are both continuous on an open set $U$, then they are equal on $U$:

$$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \, \partial y} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \, \partial x}.$$

This symmetry of mixed partials is foundational for the study of the Hessian matrix and second-order optimality conditions.

### gradient (12.9s)

## Gradient

**Definition.** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable scalar-valued function. The *gradient* of $f$ at a point $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the vector of all first-order partial derivatives:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \left( \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},\, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},\, \ldots,\, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right) \in \mathbb{R}^n.$$

The gradient generalizes the notion of a derivative to multivariate functions. Geometrically, $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ points in the direction of steepest ascent of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}$, and its magnitude $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\|$ equals the rate of increase in that direction.

**Worked Example.** Consider $f(x, y) = x^2 + 3xy - y^2$. Computing partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2x + 3y, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = 3x - 2y.$$

Thus $\nabla f(x, y) = (2x + 3y,\; 3x - 2y)$. At the point $(1, 2)$:

$$\nabla f(1, 2) = (2(1) + 3(2),\; 3(1) - 2(2)) = (8,\; -1).$$

This means that, starting from $(1, 2)$, moving in the direction $(8, -1)/\sqrt{65}$ increases $f$ most rapidly, at a rate of $\sqrt{65} \approx 8.06$ per unit step.

**Key Theorem** *(Gradient and Directional Derivative).* For a unit vector $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the directional derivative of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}$ in direction $\mathbf{u}$ satisfies:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{u}.$$

*Proof sketch.* By the chain rule applied to $g(t) = f(\mathbf{x} + t\mathbf{u})$, one obtains $g'(0) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} u_i = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}) \cdot \mathbf{u}$. The Cauchy–Schwarz inequality then confirms that this inner product is maximized when $\mathbf{u}$ is parallel to $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$, establishing the steepest-ascent interpretation. $\square$

### optimization (15.7s)

## Optimization

**Definition.** Given a function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, an *optimization problem* seeks a point $\mathbf{x}^* \in \mathcal{D} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ such that $f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ is minimized (or maximized) over the feasible domain $\mathcal{D}$. Formally, we write

$$\mathbf{x}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{x} \in \mathcal{D}}\; f(\mathbf{x}).$$

When $\mathcal{D} = \mathbb{R}^n$ and $f$ is differentiable, a *necessary* condition for a local minimum is that the gradient vanishes: $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$. Such a point is called a *critical point* or *stationary point*. To distinguish minima from maxima and saddle points, one examines the *Hessian matrix* $H = \nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x}^*)$: if $H$ is positive definite, $\mathbf{x}^*$ is a strict local minimum.

**Worked Example.** Minimize $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 2$ over $\mathbb{R}$.

Set $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0$, giving $x = \pm 1$. Compute the second derivative $f''(x) = 6x$. At $x = 1$: $f''(1) = 6 > 0$, so $x = 1$ is a local minimum with $f(1) = 0$. At $x = -1$: $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$, so $x = -1$ is a local maximum with $f(-1) = 4$. Since $f(x) \to -\infty$ as $x \to -\infty$, no global minimum exists on $\mathbb{R}$; the global minimum on a closed interval $[-2, 2]$ is $f(1) = 0$.

**Key Theorem (Second-Order Sufficient Conditions).** Let $f \in C^2(\mathcal{D})$ and suppose $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$. If $\nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ is positive definite, then $\mathbf{x}^*$ is a strict local minimizer of $f$. Conversely, if $\nabla^2 f(\mathbf{x}^*)$ is negative definite, then $\mathbf{x}^*$ is a strict local maximizer. If the Hessian is indefinite, $\mathbf{x}^*$ is a saddle point.

For *constrained* optimization — minimize $f(\mathbf{x})$ subject to $g_i(\mathbf{x}) = 0$ — the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions generalize the stationarity requirement via Lagrange multipliers $\lambda_i$: $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}^*) + \sum_i \lambda_i \nabla g_i(\mathbf{x}^*) = \mathbf{0}$.

### gradient_descent (16.2s)

## Gradient Descent

**Definition.** Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a differentiable function. Gradient descent is an iterative first-order optimization algorithm that seeks a local minimum of $f$ by repeatedly moving in the direction of the negative gradient. Starting from an initial point $\mathbf{x}^{(0)} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the update rule at iteration $t$ is

$$\mathbf{x}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{x}^{(t)} - \eta \, \nabla f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(t)}\right),$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the **learning rate** (step size) and $\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ denotes the gradient vector whose $i$-th component is $\partial f / \partial x_i$.

The negative gradient $-\nabla f(\mathbf{x})$ is the direction of steepest descent at $\mathbf{x}$: any directional derivative $D_{\mathbf{v}} f(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x})^\top \mathbf{v}$ is minimized over unit vectors $\mathbf{v}$ precisely when $\mathbf{v} = -\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) / \|\nabla f(\mathbf{x})\|$, a consequence of the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality.

**Worked Example.** Consider $f(x) = x^2 + 4x + 5$ with learning rate $\eta = 0.3$ and starting point $x^{(0)} = 4$. The gradient is $f'(x) = 2x + 4$. The first three iterations proceed as follows:

| $t$ | $x^{(t)}$ | $f'(x^{(t)})$ | $x^{(t+1)}$ |
|-----|-----------|----------------|--------------|
| 0   | 4.000     | 12.000         | 0.400        |
| 1   | 0.400     | 4.800          | −1.040       |
| 2   | −1.040    | 1.920          | −1.616       |

The iterates converge toward the true minimum $x^* = -2$, where $f'(x^*) = 0$.

**Key Theorem (Convergence for Smooth Convex Functions).** If $f$ is convex and $L$-smooth — meaning $\|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) - \nabla f(\mathbf{y})\| \le L \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\|$ for all $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$ — then gradient descent with step size $\eta = 1/L$ satisfies

$$f\!\left(\mathbf{x}^{(T)}\right) - f(\mathbf{x}^*) \;\le\; \frac{L \,\|\mathbf{x}^{(0)} - \mathbf{x}^*\|^2}{2T},$$

giving an $O(1/T)$ convergence rate. Under the additional assumption of $\mu$-strong convexity, the rate improves to $O\!\left((1 - \mu/L)^T\right)$, i.e., linear (exponential) convergence.
