### physical_quantity (24.1s)

## Physical Quantity

When a doctor says your temperature is 38.5°C or a pilot announces cruising altitude at 35,000 feet, they are doing something mathematically precise: they are attaching a number to a measurable property of the world. This pairing of measurement and unit is the foundation of all quantitative science.

**Definition.** A *physical quantity* is an ordered pair $(n, [u])$, where $n \in \mathbb{R}$ is the *numerical value* and $[u]$ denotes a *unit of measurement* drawn from an agreed system (e.g., SI). Two physical quantities are equal if and only if they represent the same magnitude under any consistent unit conversion:

$$Q = n \cdot [u]$$

A quantity without a unit is called *dimensionless* (e.g., the refractive index $n = 1.5$); all other quantities belong to a *dimension* such as length $\mathsf{L}$, mass $\mathsf{M}$, or time $\mathsf{T}$.

**Worked example.** Suppose a car travels $d = 90 \text{ km}$ in $t = 1.5 \text{ h}$. Its average speed is:

$$v = \frac{d}{t} = \frac{90 \text{ km}}{1.5 \text{ h}} = 60 \; \frac{\text{km}}{\text{h}}$$

Converting to SI units ($1 \text{ km} = 1000 \text{ m}$, $1 \text{ h} = 3600 \text{ s}$):

$$v = 60 \times \frac{1000}{3600} \; \frac{\text{m}}{\text{s}} \approx 16.7 \; \text{m s}^{-1}$$

The numerical value changed, but the physical quantity — the speed of the car — did not.

**Why this matters.** The rule $Q = n \cdot [u]$ guarantees that equations involving physical quantities are *dimensionally homogeneous*: every term on both sides must share the same dimension. This constraint eliminates entire classes of errors before any arithmetic begins, and underpins dimensional analysis as a lightweight proof technique.

**Practice problem.** A 5 kg block accelerates at $2 \; \text{m s}^{-2}$. Using Newton's second law $F = ma$, express the force in Newtons and verify that $1 \; \text{N} = 1 \; \text{kg} \cdot \text{m} \cdot \text{s}^{-2}$ by expanding the dimensions on both sides.

### units (14.2s)

## Units

When a pharmacist reads a prescription that says "500" without specifying milligrams or micrograms, the ambiguity is not just inconvenient — it can be life-threatening. Units are what transform a bare number into a meaningful quantity. Mathematics and science depend on them to make statements that are true regardless of who reads them.

**Definition.** A *unit* is a fixed, agreed-upon reference quantity used to express measurements. A *measurement* is a pair $(n, u)$ where $n$ is a dimensionless number (the *magnitude*) and $u$ is the unit. Two measurements are equal when their magnitudes agree after conversion to a common unit.

Units organize into *dimensions* — the fundamental physical categories. The SI system recognizes seven base dimensions; every derived unit is a product of powers of these:

$$[u] = \mathsf{L}^{a}\,\mathsf{M}^{b}\,\mathsf{T}^{c}\cdots$$

**Worked example.** Speed has dimension $\mathsf{L}\,\mathsf{T}^{-1}$. Suppose a cyclist travels $36\,\mathrm{km}$ in $1\,\mathrm{h}$. Converting:

$$36\,\mathrm{km/h} \times \frac{1000\,\mathrm{m}}{1\,\mathrm{km}} \times \frac{1\,\mathrm{h}}{3600\,\mathrm{s}} = 10\,\mathrm{m/s}$$

The conversion factors are *dimensionless ratios* equal to $1$, so multiplying by them changes the representation without changing the quantity.

**Why it's true.** The key invariant is *dimensional homogeneity*: in any valid equation, every term must share the same dimension. This is not a convention — it follows from the fact that physical laws must hold independently of the measurement system chosen. Adding a length to a time is undefined because no choice of units can make them commensurable.

**Practice problem.** A recipe calls for $2.5\,\mathrm{cups}$ of flour. Given that $1\,\mathrm{cup} = 236.6\,\mathrm{mL}$ and $1\,\mathrm{L} = 1000\,\mathrm{mL}$, express the amount in liters. What is the dimension of "volume of flour," and why does it not depend on whether you use cups or liters?

### fundamental_units (15.4s)

## Fundamental Units

When you buy carpet, the salesperson quotes a price per **square meter**. When you check your car's fuel efficiency, you see **kilometers per liter**. These composite quantities feel different from a raw length or a raw time — and that intuition points to something deep: there exists a small set of irreducible building blocks from which every other measurable quantity is constructed.

**Definition.** A *fundamental unit* (or *base unit*) is a unit of measurement that is defined independently, not derived from any combination of other units. In the International System (SI), there are exactly seven:

$$\text{meter (m)},\quad \text{kilogram (kg)},\quad \text{second (s)},\quad \text{ampere (A)},\quad \text{kelvin (K)},\quad \text{mole (mol)},\quad \text{candela (cd)}$$

Every other physical quantity — *derived units* — is expressed as a product of powers of these seven:

$$[Q] = \mathrm{m}^{a}\,\mathrm{kg}^{b}\,\mathrm{s}^{c}\,\mathrm{A}^{d}\,\mathrm{K}^{e}\,\mathrm{mol}^{f}\,\mathrm{cd}^{g}, \qquad a,b,\ldots,g \in \mathbb{Z}.$$

**Worked example.** What is the unit of force? Newton's second law states $F = ma$. Mass has dimension $\mathrm{kg}$ and acceleration has dimension $\mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$, so:

$$[F] = \mathrm{kg} \cdot \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}} = \mathrm{kg\,m\,s^{-2}} =: \mathrm{N}.$$

The newton is not fundamental — it collapses into three base units with specific exponents $(b=1,\,a=1,\,c=-2)$.

**Why it's true.** Dimensional analysis is essentially linear algebra over the integers. Each physical quantity is a vector in $\mathbb{Z}^{7}$, and the seven fundamental units form a **basis** for that space. Because they are linearly independent over $\mathbb{Z}$, every derived quantity has a *unique* dimensional signature — which is why a dimensional check can catch errors in any formula.

**Practice problem.** Express the SI unit of pressure (force per area) entirely in fundamental units. What is the exponent of $\mathrm{s}$?
