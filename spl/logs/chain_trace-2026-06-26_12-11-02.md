### 木 (15.7s)

## 木

**Everyday example.** Step outside and look at any tree. You see a straight trunk rising from the ground, branches reaching upward, and roots anchoring it below. The ancient Chinese looked at the same sight thousands of years ago and drew what they saw.

**Picture this.** The character 木 is a simple sketch of a tree. The vertical stroke (丨) is the trunk. The horizontal stroke (一) crossing it represents the main branches spreading left and right. The two downward strokes at the bottom (丿 and ㇏) are the roots digging into the earth. Turn your head sideways and you can almost see the silhouette of a real tree.

**Simple rule.** 木 means *tree* or *wood* — both the living plant and the material it gives us. Whenever you see 木 sitting inside a more complex character, it is usually telling you "this word has something to do with trees, wood, or things made from wood." For example: 林 (two trees = *forest*), 森 (three trees = *dense forest*), 板 (*board*, wood split flat), and 桌 (*table*, furniture made of wood). One tree gives you the idea; stacking trees multiplies it.

**Try it yourself.** Write 木 stroke by stroke: horizontal first, then vertical, then the left-falling root, then the right-falling root. Now look around the room — how many objects near you came from 木?

### 女 (17.2s)

## 女

**Everyday example.** Think about the word "she" in English — one small word that tells you someone is female. In Chinese, a single character does that job and much more: **女** (nǚ) means *woman* or *female*. It is one of the oldest characters still in use, and it appears inside dozens of other characters as a building block.

**Picture this.** The ancient form of 女 showed a person kneeling with arms crossed — a posture of grace in early Chinese culture. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the shape still carries a gentle, curving energy. Notice the three strokes: a sweeping left stroke, a turning horizontal stroke, and a downward-right stroke that crosses through. The whole character leans slightly, as if mid-bow.

**Simple rule.** When 女 appears on the *left side* of another character, it acts as a **radical** — a meaning clue that tells you the word relates to women, femininity, or family relationships. For example: 妈 (mā, *mother*), 姐 (jiě, *older sister*), and 好 (hǎo, *good* — literally *woman* + *child*). Whenever you spot 女 tucked into the left of an unfamiliar character, ask yourself: "Does this word involve a person or a relationship?"

**Try it yourself.** Look at these characters and guess which ones contain the 女 radical: 她, 安, 姓, 男. Which one does *not* include 女?

### 人 (15.9s)

## 人

**Everyday example.** Stand up and take a relaxed step forward. Notice how your legs form an upside-down "V" shape — one leg forward, one leg back, your weight balanced between them. That simple silhouette of a walking human is exactly what ancient Chinese scribes carved into bone and bronze thousands of years ago.

**Picture this.** The character 人 is just two strokes: one falls to the left (撇, *piě*), one falls to the right (捺, *nà*). They lean against each other like two sticks propped up in a tent shape. Imagine a stick figure with no head, no arms — only two legs mid-stride. That's 人. It means **person** or **people**, pronounced *rén* (rhymes with "run" said with a rising tone).

**Simple rule.** When you see 人 standing alone, it means "person." When it appears *inside* other characters, it often shrinks into a shorter form called 亻(the "person radical," always on the left side). Think of 亻 as 人 squeezed into a narrow column to make room for the rest of the character. Whenever you spot either shape, the character's meaning usually involves people or human activity.

**Try it yourself.** Grab a pencil. Draw the left-falling stroke first, then the right-falling stroke, meeting at the top. Now write it five times, getting smaller each time. Can you see the walking person in every one?

### 子 (17.9s)

## 子

**Everyday example.** Think of a small child reaching up to hold a parent's hand. That image — tiny, dependent, full of potential — is exactly what the character **子** (pronounced *zǐ*) captures. It means **child** or **son**, and it is one of the oldest and most common building blocks in Chinese.

**Picture this.** In its ancient form, 子 looked like a baby with outstretched arms and a large head, wrapped in a swaddling cloth. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the idea stayed the same: something small that grows. Today 子 is written in just three strokes — a horizontal hook on top, a curving body, and a crossing vertical line.

**Simple rule.** When you see 子 sitting inside another character, it usually adds the meaning of "small," "offspring," or "seed." For example, **好** (hǎo, "good") combines **女** (woman) with **子** (child) — a mother with her child, the picture of goodness. On its own, 子 also works as a common noun suffix: 桌子 (zhuōzi, "table"), 杯子 (bēizi, "cup"). In this suffix role, it is pronounced with a neutral tone (*zi*) and simply turns a root word into an everyday object.

**Try it yourself.** Look at these characters and spot where 子 hides: **孩** (child), **学** (learn), **字** (written character). What shared idea might 子 contribute to each one?

### 宀 (34.8s)

## 宀

**Everyday example.**
Think about what makes a house feel like a house. Before the walls, before the furniture, the very first thing you notice from the street is the **roof** — that angled covering that keeps rain and snow out. A roof turns empty ground into a sheltered space.

**Picture this.**
The character 宀 (pronounced *mián*) is a small picture of exactly that: a roof. Look at its three strokes — a dot on top like the peak, then two sweeping lines sloping down on each side, forming an open shelter. Ancient Chinese writers drew it as a simple canopy over empty space, meaning "cover" or "dwelling."

**Simple rule.**
Whenever you see 宀 sitting on top of another character, it tells you the meaning is connected to **shelter, home, or enclosure**. You don't need to know the bottom parts yet — just spotting the roof is enough:

- **家** (jiā, "family/home") — a roof over a household
- **安** (ān, "peace") — safety under a roof
- **宝** (bǎo, "treasure") — precious things kept under a roof

The roof radical is your visual clue: *something is being sheltered or contained*.

**Try it yourself.**
Find the character 字 (zì, "character/word"). Can you spot 宀 on top? The bottom part will be introduced in a later section — for now, just practice recognizing the roof shape. Look at 家, 安, and 宝 again. How quickly can you find 宀 in each one?

### 马 (15.6s)

## 马

**Everyday example.** Horses have been humanity's partners for thousands of years — pulling carts, carrying riders, racing across open plains. In Chinese, the word for horse is **马 (mǎ)**, and the character itself began as a tiny picture of one.

**Picture this.** Ancient writers drew a horse from the side: a head on top, a body in the middle, and four legs at the bottom. Over centuries, the strokes simplified, but you can still feel the shape. The top horizontal stroke suggests the head, the vertical stroke is the spine, and the lower strokes hint at galloping legs. Think of 马 as a horse caught mid-stride, frozen into just three quick strokes.

**Simple rule.** 马 is one of the most common *building-block characters* in Chinese. Whenever you see it tucked inside a larger character, it usually contributes either the **meaning** (something related to horses or movement) or the **sound** (the *mǎ* syllable). For example:

- 妈 (mā) — mother (the sound comes from 马)
- 骑 (qí) — to ride (the meaning connects to horses)

Recognizing 马 inside other characters is like spotting a familiar face in a crowd — suddenly the complex character feels less intimidating.

**Try it yourself.** Write 马 three times, paying attention to stroke order: first the top horizontal, then the vertical fold, then the bottom horizontal with its upward hook. Now look at 吗, 码, and 蚂 — can you find 马 hiding inside each one?

### 从 (16.4s)

## 从

**Everyday example.** Imagine walking behind a friend through a crowded market. You follow their lead, stepping where they step. That simple act — one person trailing another — is exactly what the character 从 captures.

**Picture this.** Look closely at 从 and you will see two copies of 人 (rén, "person") standing side by side. The one on the left leans slightly into the one on the right, as though walking just behind. Two people, one following the other — that is the entire picture. Chinese characters often build meaning by combining simpler pieces, and 从 is one of the clearest examples: person + person = to follow.

**Simple rule.** When you double a basic element, the new character usually describes a *relationship* between the two. Here, two people together give us the core meaning **"to follow."** From that idea of following, 从 extended over time to also mean **"from"** (the starting point you follow a path from) and appears in words like 从前 (cóngqián, "once upon a time" — literally "from before") and 服从 (fúcóng, "to obey" — literally "submit and follow").

**Try it yourself.** Write 从 stroke by stroke: first the left 人 (撇 piě, then 捺 nà), then the right 人 the same way. As you write, picture one person stepping behind another. Can you spot 从 hiding inside the more complex character 众 (zhòng, "crowd")? How many 人 do you count there?

### 口 (17.0s)

## 口

**Everyday example.** Think about the shape your lips make when you say "oh" — a small, rounded opening. Now imagine drawing that opening as a simple square. That's exactly what the Chinese character 口 captures: a **mouth**, an opening, a hole.

**Picture this.** Look at the character: 口. It's one of the simplest shapes you'll ever write — just a small box. Ancient people drew it as a literal picture of an open mouth seen from the front. Over thousands of years the round shape squared off, but the meaning stayed the same. Whenever you see 口 sitting inside a more complex character, it usually signals something related to speaking, eating, or openings.

**Simple rule.** 口 (pronounced *kǒu*) means **mouth**. As a building block — called a *radical* — it appears in dozens of characters connected to the mouth's two jobs: **making sounds** and **taking things in**. For example, 吃 (*chī*, to eat) and 叫 (*jiào*, to shout) both contain 口. Spotting the little square inside a new character is your first clue to its meaning family.

**Try it yourself.** Write 口 three times — it's just three strokes forming a box (left side down, bottom across, then the right side and top in one stroke). Now look at these characters and find the 口 hiding in each: 吗, 唱, 品. How many mouths does 品 contain?

### 日 (15.6s)

## 日

**Everyday example.** Every morning you see it — the sun rising over the horizon, marking the start of a new day. The Chinese character **日** (pronounced *rì*) captures exactly this idea: it means both **sun** and **day**. One symbol, two tightly connected meanings — because the sun is what defines a day.

**Picture this.** Imagine looking at the sun and drawing a quick sketch. Ancient writers started with a circle, then added a horizontal line through the middle to show the sun isn't empty — it radiates light. Over centuries, that rounded shape squared off into the neat rectangle you see today: 日. The outer frame is the sun's edge. The inner bar is its glowing core.

**Simple rule.** 日 is one of the most common building blocks in Chinese writing. Whenever you spot it tucked inside a larger character, there's usually a connection to sunlight, time, or brightness. Think of it as a tag that says *"something about the sun or a day lives here."*

**Try it yourself.** Write 日 a few times — four strokes, starting with the left vertical line, then the top horizontal, then the inner bar, and finally closing the bottom. Now look at these characters and guess which part is 日: 明, 早, 晴. Each one borrows the sun to build a richer meaning.

### 月 (16.7s)

## 月

**Everyday example.** Look up at the night sky and find the brightest object after the sun — that curved, glowing shape is the moon. In ancient China, people watched the moon grow from a sliver to a full circle and back again, and they used that cycle to count time. One full cycle became one *month*.

**Picture this.** The character 月 started as a simple sketch of a crescent moon — two curved lines enclosing a hollow center. Over centuries the curves straightened into the rectangular shape you see today, but the meaning stayed: **moon**, and by extension, **month**.

**Simple rule.** 月 does double duty. On its own it means "moon" or "month." Inside other characters, it often appears as a *radical* — a building block that hints at meaning. When you spot 月 tucked into the left side of a character, it frequently signals something related to the **body** (because the "flesh" radical 肉 was simplified to look just like 月). So 月 can point toward the sky *or* toward the human body, depending on context.

Common words to know:

- **月亮** (yuè liang) — the moon
- **一月** (yī yuè) — January (literally "month one")

**Try it yourself.** Find three characters that contain 月 as a component. For each one, ask: does the 月 here relate to *time*, *the moon*, or *the body*? Start noticing — the pattern will surprise you.

### 林 (15.8s)

## 林

**Everyday example.** Walk to the edge of a city park and look out at a cluster of trees standing together. That's not just "a tree" — it's a *grove* or a small *woods*. In Chinese, a single tree is written 木 (mù). When you want to say "woods," you simply place two trees side by side: 林 (lín).

**Picture this.** Imagine two stick-figure trees standing next to each other: 木 + 木 = 林. The character literally *looks* like a pair of trees. This is one of the most intuitive combinations in the entire writing system — more trees means more forest.

**Simple rule.** 林 is built by *doubling* the component 木. This doubling technique is a common pattern in Chinese characters: repeating an element intensifies or multiplies its meaning. One tree → wood; two trees → woods. (Three trees, 森 (sēn), means a dense forest — but that's a story for another section.)

The character 林 is pronounced *lín* (second tone, rising pitch) and appears in everyday words like 森林 (sēnlín, "forest") and as one of the most common Chinese surnames.

**Try it yourself.** Write 木 twice, side by side, keeping both halves roughly equal in width. Notice how the left 木 squeezes slightly narrower to make room for its neighbor — this is how balanced characters share space.

### 水 (15.5s)

## 水

**Everyday example**
Turn on a tap and watch what happens. Water flows downward, splits around obstacles, and always finds the lowest path. The Chinese character **水** (shuǐ) captures exactly this image — a stream of liquid tumbling downhill.

**Picture this**
Look at the character 水. The vertical stroke in the center is the main current of a river. The shorter strokes on either side are splashes and tributaries branching off as the water rushes past rocks. Imagine standing on a bridge, looking down at a creek — the central flow with little ripples curling away on both sides. That is what the ancient writers were sketching.

**Simple rule**
水 means *water* in all its forms — drinking water, rivers, rain, oceans. When 水 appears inside other characters, it often shrinks into a three-dot pattern on the left side, called the "water radical" (氵). Whenever you spot those three dots, the character probably relates to liquid or flowing: 河 (river), 湖 (lake), 泪 (tears). Think of 氵 as a shorthand signature that says, "water was here."

**Try it yourself**
Write 水 stroke by stroke: start with the left-falling stroke, then the vertical center, then the remaining strokes on either side. Now scan these characters — 海, 洗, 汤 — and find the hidden 氵. What water-related meaning might each one carry?

### 休 (15.0s)

## 休

**Everyday example.** After a long walk, you spot a shady tree and lean against it to catch your breath. That moment of relief — stopping, relaxing, letting your body recover — is exactly what 休 means: *to rest*.

**Picture this.** Look at the character 休 and you'll see two familiar pieces side by side. On the left stands 亻, the slim form of 人 (person). On the right grows 木 (tree). Put them together and the picture writes itself: a person leaning against a tree. Thousands of years ago, someone watched a tired traveler pause beneath the branches and turned that scene into a single character.

**Simple rule.** When you combine a *person* (亻) with a *tree* (木), you get *rest* (休). The meaning flows directly from the image: people rest against trees. This is a beautiful example of how Chinese characters often work — two simple pictures merge to tell a small story, and that story *is* the meaning.

**Try it yourself.** Cover the right half of 休 — can you recognize the person radical 亻? Now cover the left half — do you see 木, the tree with its trunk and spreading roots? Put the scene back together in your mind: a person, a tree, a moment of rest. Next time you need a break, you'll know exactly which character to reach for.

### 众 (16.1s)

## 众

**Everyday example.** Think about standing in a busy park. One person is just *someone*. Two people are a pair. But once you see three or more gathered together, you instinctively think: *a crowd*.

**Picture this.** The character 众 is built from three copies of 人 (person). One 人 sits on top, and two more stand side by side beneath it. Imagine a little triangle of stick figures — one leading, two following. That simple picture *is* the character. Three people together mean "many," "the masses," or "a crowd."

**Simple rule.** In Chinese, repeating a building block often intensifies its meaning. One 人 means *person*. Stack three of them into 众, and the meaning jumps from a single individual to *everybody* — the public, the collective, the many. You don't need to count to a thousand; three is already "a lot" in the logic of character design. This principle — *repetition signals abundance* — appears in other characters too (木 "tree" → 森 "forest"), but 众 is one of the clearest examples.

**Try it yourself.** Write 人 three times on a scrap of paper: one centered above, two below. Now merge them into a single compact shape. Notice how the top 人 is slightly smaller, like a person standing farther back in a crowd. Can you spot the three individual figures inside the finished character?

### 吗 (15.3s)

## 吗

**Everyday example.** In English, turning "You are hungry" into a question requires rearranging words: "Are you hungry?" Chinese takes a simpler path — just stick one tiny word at the end and you're done.

**Picture this.** Imagine you have a statement like a finished sentence sitting on a table. Now place a small flag at the very end of it. That flag signals to everyone: "This is now a question — please answer yes or no." The character **吗** (pronounced *ma*, with a neutral tone) is exactly that flag.

Take the statement **你好** (*nǐ hǎo* — "you are well"). Add 吗 to get **你好吗？** (*nǐ hǎo ma?* — "Are you well? / How are you?"). The word order stays identical; only the 吗 at the end changes the meaning from a statement to a question.

**Simple rule.** To form a yes/no question in Mandarin, keep the statement exactly as it is and append 吗 at the end. Nothing else moves. Statement + 吗 = question.

- 他是老师。(*He is a teacher.*) → 他是老师吗？(*Is he a teacher?*)
- 你喜欢咖啡。(*You like coffee.*) → 你喜欢咖啡吗？(*Do you like coffee?*)

**Try it yourself.** Convert this statement into a question: **她会说中文。** (*She can speak Chinese.*) What does the question version look like, and what does it mean?
