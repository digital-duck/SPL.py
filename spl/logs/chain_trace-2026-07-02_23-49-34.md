### position (18.4s)

## Position

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, the *position* of a particle is a vector quantity that specifies the location of that particle relative to a chosen reference point called the *origin*. Given an inertial reference frame with a fixed origin $O$ and a right-handed Cartesian coordinate system, the position of a particle $P$ at time $t$ is the vector

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{k}},$$

where $x(t)$, $y(t)$, and $z(t)$ are the signed scalar projections of $\mathbf{r}$ onto the coordinate axes, and $\hat{\mathbf{i}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{j}}$, $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$ are the standard orthonormal basis vectors. The magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(t)|$ gives the straight-line distance from the origin to the particle. Position is a *state variable*: its specification at a given instant, together with velocity, completely determines the future evolution of an isolated Newtonian particle.

**Worked Example.** A ball is launched from the point $(2,\,0,\,0)\,\text{m}$ with initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0 = 3\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + 4\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m\,s}^{-1}$ under constant gravitational acceleration $\mathbf{g} = -9.8\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m\,s}^{-2}$. Integrating $\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{g}$ twice with the given initial conditions yields

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = (2 + 3t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + \left(4t - \tfrac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2\right)\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m}.$$

At $t = 0.5\,\text{s}$ the position is $\mathbf{r}(0.5) = 3.5\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + 0.775\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}\;\text{m}$, with magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(0.5)| = \sqrt{3.5^2 + 0.775^2} \approx 3.585\,\text{m}$.

**Key Theorem (Frame Transformation).** If two inertial frames $S$ and $S'$ share the same orientation and $S'$ moves at constant velocity $\mathbf{V}$ relative to $S$, with origins coinciding at $t = 0$, then the position of a particle as measured in each frame satisfies the *Galilean transformation*

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{V}t.$$

This relation preserves Newton's second law because the acceleration $\ddot{\mathbf{r}}' = \ddot{\mathbf{r}}$ is frame-independent under Galilean relativity, confirming that position is frame-dependent while the equations of motion retain the same form across all inertial frames.

**Lab Exercise.** Set up a two-dimensional coordinate system on a large sheet of graph paper. Place a marble at a measured initial position $\mathbf{r}_0$ and roll it across a level surface. Record its position $\mathbf{r}(t)$ at equal time intervals using stroboscopic photography or video frame analysis. Plot $x(t)$ and $y(t)$ separately and verify that each component varies linearly with time, consistent with constant-velocity motion. Compute $|\mathbf{r}(t) - \mathbf{r}_0|$ and compare with the measured path length.

### time (15.8s)

## Time

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, *time* is treated as an absolute, universal parameter $t \in \mathbb{R}$, independent of the observer or the physical system under study. Following Newton's *Principia Mathematica*, absolute time "flows equably without relation to anything external." Formally, the state of a mechanical system is described by a trajectory $\mathbf{q}(t)$ in configuration space, where $t$ parametrizes the sequence of states. The instantaneous rate of change of any observable $f(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}}, t)$ with respect to $t$ defines the dynamical evolution of the system. Time is measured in SI units of seconds (s), defined operationally by atomic transition frequencies.

**Worked Example.** Consider a particle undergoing uniform acceleration from rest. Its position is given by

$$x(t) = \frac{1}{2}a t^2,$$

with $a = 2\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-2}}$. To find the time at which the particle has traveled $x = 9\ \mathrm{m}$, solve

$$9 = \frac{1}{2}(2)t^2 \implies t^2 = 9 \implies t = 3\ \mathrm{s}.$$

Note that only the positive root is retained, since $t \geq 0$ by convention when measuring elapsed time from an initial event. This illustrates how $t$ serves as a monotonically increasing index of physical states.

**Key Theorem (Time-Translation Symmetry).** If the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{q}, \dot{\mathbf{q}})$ of a closed system has no explicit dependence on $t$, then by Noether's theorem the total energy $E$ is conserved:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial t} = 0 \implies \frac{dE}{dt} = 0.$$

*Proof sketch.* Compute the total time derivative of $\mathcal{L}$ along a solution trajectory, apply the Euler–Lagrange equations to eliminate $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \mathbf{q}$, and identify the conserved Hamiltonian $H = \dot{\mathbf{q}}\cdot\nabla_{\dot{\mathbf{q}}}\mathcal{L} - \mathcal{L} = E$.

**Lab Exercise.** Using a motion sensor and a cart on a frictionless air track, record position $x$ at uniform time intervals $\Delta t = 0.05\ \mathrm{s}$. Compute the finite-difference approximation to velocity,

$$v_i \approx \frac{x_{i+1} - x_{i-1}}{2\Delta t},$$

and plot $v$ versus $t$. Determine whether the slope $dv/dt$ is constant, and identify whether energy is conserved over the interval. Discuss how measurement precision in $t$ propagates into uncertainty in $v$.

### mass (32.9s)

## Mass

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, *mass* is an intrinsic scalar property of a body that quantifies its resistance to acceleration. Newton's second law encodes this precisely:

$$\mathbf{F} = m\,\mathbf{a}.$$

A larger mass requires a proportionally larger net force to produce the same acceleration. The SI unit of mass is the kilogram (kg). It is worth noting that mass also governs a body's gravitational coupling—the force on a body near Earth's surface is $\mathbf{F}_g = m\mathbf{g}$—and experiment confirms to one part in $10^{12}$ that the same scalar $m$ governs both roles, a result elevated to a postulate in general relativity.

**Worked Example.** A block of mass $m = 4.0\ \text{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. A net horizontal force $F = 12\ \text{N}$ is applied. Find the acceleration.

Applying Newton's second law:

$$a = \frac{F}{m} = \frac{12\ \text{N}}{4.0\ \text{kg}} = 3.0\ \text{m s}^{-2}.$$

The direction of $\mathbf{a}$ is parallel to $\mathbf{F}$. Note that doubling the mass while holding $F$ constant would halve the acceleration, illustrating the inverse proportionality $a \propto m^{-1}$.

**Key Theorem (Conservation of Mass in Newtonian Mechanics).** For a closed system of $n$ particles with no relativistic effects, the total mass

$$M = \sum_{k=1}^{n} m_k$$

is constant in time: $\dfrac{dM}{dt} = 0$. This follows from the assumption that classical particles neither appear nor disappear and that mass is additive. Combined with Newton's third law, this guarantees conservation of linear momentum $\mathbf{p} = M\mathbf{v}_{\text{cm}}$ in the absence of external forces.

**Lab Exercise.** Using a dynamics cart, track, and calibrated weights, design an experiment to verify the relationship $a = F/m$:

### velocity (14.1s)

## Velocity

**Definition.** The *velocity* of a particle is the time derivative of its position vector. Let the position of a particle in three-dimensional space be described by $\mathbf{r}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^3$. The instantaneous velocity is defined as

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{r}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{r}(t)}{\Delta t}.$$

Velocity is a vector quantity: it possesses both magnitude and direction. The magnitude $|\mathbf{v}(t)|$ is called the *speed* of the particle. In Cartesian coordinates, if $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + y(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + z(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{k}}$, then

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = \dot{x}(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + \dot{y}(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}} + \dot{z}(t)\,\hat{\mathbf{k}},$$

where dot notation denotes differentiation with respect to time.

**Worked Example.** A particle moves along a curved path described by $\mathbf{r}(t) = (3t^2)\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + (2t^3 - t)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}$, with $t$ in seconds and position in meters. Differentiating component-wise:

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = 6t\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + (6t^2 - 1)\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}.$$

At $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$, the velocity is $\mathbf{v}(1) = 6\,\hat{\mathbf{i}} + 5\,\hat{\mathbf{j}}\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$, with speed

$$|\mathbf{v}(1)| = \sqrt{6^2 + 5^2} = \sqrt{61} \approx 7.81\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}.$$

**Key Theorem (Mean Value Theorem for Motion).** If $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is continuously differentiable on $[t_1, t_2]$, there exists at least one instant $t^* \in (t_1, t_2)$ such that the instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity:

$$\mathbf{v}(t^*) = \frac{\mathbf{r}(t_2) - \mathbf{r}(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}.$$

This result, a direct consequence of the vector form of the Mean Value Theorem, justifies the use of $\Delta \mathbf{r} / \Delta t$ as an approximation to $\mathbf{v}$ for sufficiently small time intervals and underlies the kinematics of finite-difference methods.

**Lab Exercise.** A projectile is launched from the origin with initial speed $v_0 = 20\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$ at angle $\theta = 30°$ above the horizontal. Neglecting air resistance, write $\mathbf{r}(t)$ explicitly, differentiate to obtain $\mathbf{v}(t)$, and determine: (a) the speed at $t = 1\,\mathrm{s}$; (b) the angle $\mathbf{v}$ makes with the horizontal at that instant; (c) the time at which the vertical component of velocity vanishes.

### force (23.1s)

## Force

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, *force* is a vector quantity that describes the interaction between objects, capable of altering an object's state of motion or causing deformation. Formally, force $\mathbf{F}$ is defined through Newton's Second Law:

$$\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$$

where $m$ is the mass of the object and $\mathbf{a} = d\mathbf{v}/dt$ is its acceleration. The SI unit of force is the newton ($\mathrm{N} = \mathrm{kg \cdot m \cdot s^{-2}}$). Force is a vector, possessing both magnitude and direction, and multiple forces on a single body superpose according to the **principle of superposition**:

$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = \sum_{i} \mathbf{F}_i$$

**Worked Example.** A block of mass $m = 5\,\mathrm{kg}$ rests on a frictionless horizontal surface. Two horizontal forces act on it: $\mathbf{F}_1 = 12\,\hat{\mathbf{x}}\,\mathrm{N}$ and $\mathbf{F}_2 = -4\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 3\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\,\mathrm{N}$. Find the acceleration.

Applying superposition:
$$\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = (12 - 4)\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 3\,\hat{\mathbf{y}} = 8\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 3\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\;\mathrm{N}$$

The magnitude is $|\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}| = \sqrt{8^2 + 3^2} = \sqrt{73} \approx 8.54\,\mathrm{N}$. By Newton's Second Law:
$$\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}}{m} = \frac{8\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 3\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}}{5} = 1.6\,\hat{\mathbf{x}} + 0.6\,\hat{\mathbf{y}}\;\mathrm{m\cdot s^{-2}}$$

**Key Theorem (Newton's Third Law).** For every force $\mathbf{F}_{AB}$ exerted by body $A$ on body $B$, there exists a reaction force $\mathbf{F}_{BA}$ exerted by $B$ on $A$ satisfying:
$$\mathbf{F}_{AB} = -\mathbf{F}_{BA}$$

Action-reaction pairs are equal in magnitude, opposite in direction, and act on *different* bodies. This symmetry underlies conservation of momentum: summing Newton's Third Law pairs over an isolated system yields $d\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}/dt = 0$, where $\mathbf{p}_{\text{total}}$ is the total linear momentum of the system.

### work (50.0s)

## Work

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, the *work* done by a constant force $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle undergoing displacement $\mathbf{d}$ is defined as the scalar quantity

$$W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{d} = |\mathbf{F}||\mathbf{d}|\cos\theta,$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the force vector and the displacement vector. The SI unit of work is the joule ($\mathrm{J} = \mathrm{N \cdot m}$). Work is a scalar; only the component of force parallel to the displacement contributes. A force perpendicular to the motion ($\theta = 90°$) does zero work, as with the normal force on a horizontally sliding block.

**Worked Example.** A block of mass $m = 5\,\mathrm{kg}$ is pushed along a horizontal frictionless surface by a force $F = 20\,\mathrm{N}$ directed at $\theta = 30°$ below the horizontal. Find the work done over a displacement of $d = 4\,\mathrm{m}$.

Only the horizontal component of $\mathbf{F}$ is parallel to the displacement:

$$W = Fd\cos\theta = (20)(4)\cos 30° = 80 \times \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \approx 69.3\,\mathrm{J}.$$

The vertical component of $\mathbf{F}$ contributes no work because it is perpendicular to the motion.

**Work–Energy Theorem.** For a particle of mass $m$ subject to a net force $\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{net}}$, the net work done equals the change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{\mathrm{net}} = \Delta K = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_i^2.$$

*Proof sketch.* Applying Newton's second law, $\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{net}} = m\mathbf{a}$, and using the kinematic identity $v\,dv = a\,ds$, one integrates along the path to obtain $W_{\mathrm{net}} = m\int_{v_i}^{v_f} v\,dv = \Delta K$. This result holds even when the force varies in magnitude, provided all forces acting on the particle are included in $W_{\mathrm{net}}$.

**Lab Exercise.** A $2\,\mathrm{kg}$ crate starts from rest on a frictionless horizontal floor. A rope exerts a constant horizontal force of $F = 10\,\mathrm{N}$ on the crate over a displacement of $d = 5\,\mathrm{m}$.

1. Compute the work done by the rope force using the definition above.
2. Using the work–energy theorem, determine the speed of the crate at the end of the displacement.

### kinetic_energy (29.4s)

## Kinetic Energy

**Definition.** The *kinetic energy* of a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ is the scalar quantity

$$K = \frac{1}{2}m\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \frac{1}{2}m v^2,$$

where $v = \|\mathbf{v}\|$ denotes the speed. Kinetic energy is measured in joules ($\text{J} = \text{kg}\cdot\text{m}^2\cdot\text{s}^{-2}$) and is always non-negative.

**Worked Example.** A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\ \text{kg}$ slides across frictionless ice with speed $v = 12.0\ \text{m/s}$. Its kinetic energy is

$$K = \frac{1}{2}(0.170\ \text{kg})(12.0\ \text{m/s})^2 = \frac{1}{2}(0.170)(144) = 12.24\ \text{J}.$$

If the puck decelerates uniformly to rest over a distance $d = 3.00\ \text{m}$ due to a resistive force $f$, the work–energy theorem gives $-f\,d = \Delta K = 0 - 12.24\ \text{J}$, so $f = 4.08\ \text{N}$.

**Key Theorem (Work–Energy Theorem).** For a particle subject to a net force $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}$, the net work done as the particle moves from position $\mathbf{r}_1$ to $\mathbf{r}_2$ equals the change in kinetic energy:

$$W_{\text{net}} = \int_{\mathbf{r}_1}^{\mathbf{r}_2} \mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \Delta K = K_2 - K_1.$$

*Proof sketch.* Apply Newton's second law $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}} = m\dot{\mathbf{v}}$ and use the identity $\mathbf{F}_{\text{net}}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = m\dot{\mathbf{v}}\cdot\mathbf{v}\,dt = \frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\right)dt$. Integrating both sides over the time interval yields $W_{\text{net}} = K_2 - K_1$. $\square$

This theorem is coordinate-independent and holds whether or not the forces are conservative, making it one of the most broadly applicable results in mechanics.

### momentum (39.5s)

## Momentum

**Definition.** The linear momentum of a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ is the vector quantity

$$\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}.$$

In SI units, momentum is measured in kilogram-metres per second ($\mathrm{kg \cdot m \cdot s^{-1}}$). Because $\mathbf{v}$ is a vector, $\mathbf{p}$ carries both magnitude and direction. Newton's Second Law may be stated in its most general form as

$$\mathbf{F}_{\mathrm{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt},$$

which reduces to $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ when mass is constant. This momentum-based statement is the form Newton himself recorded, and it is the one that generalises most cleanly to other domains of mechanics.

**Worked Example.** A hockey puck of mass $m = 0.170\ \mathrm{kg}$ slides on frictionless ice at $\mathbf{v}_i = 6.0\ \hat{x}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. A player strikes it, and $12\ \mathrm{ms}$ later the puck moves at $\mathbf{v}_f = -4.0\ \hat{x}\ \mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. Find the average force exerted on the puck.

$$\Delta \mathbf{p} = m(\mathbf{v}_f - \mathbf{v}_i) = (0.170)\bigl((-4.0) - (6.0)\bigr)\hat{x} = -1.70\ \hat{x}\ \mathrm{kg \cdot m \cdot s^{-1}}.$$

$$\bar{\mathbf{F}} = \frac{\Delta \mathbf{p}}{\Delta t} = \frac{-1.70\ \hat{x}}{0.012} \approx -142\ \hat{x}\ \mathrm{N}.$$

The negative sign indicates the force acted opposite to the puck's initial direction of travel.

**Key Theorem (Conservation of Linear Momentum).** For an isolated system of $N$ particles — one on which the net external force vanishes — the total momentum

$$\mathbf{P} = \sum_{i=1}^{N} m_i \mathbf{v}_i$$

is constant in time. This follows directly from Newton's Third Law: internal forces between particles occur in equal-and-opposite pairs and therefore cancel pairwise in the sum $d\mathbf{P}/dt$. The result is independent of the nature of the internal interaction, so it holds whether or not kinetic energy is conserved.

**Lab Exercise.** Using a low-friction air track, record the masses and velocities of two carts before and after a head-on collision with a spring bumper. Compute $\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{before}}$ and $\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{after}}$, then determine the fractional change $|\Delta\mathbf{P}|/|\mathbf{P}_{\mathrm{before}}|$. Repeat for a perfectly inelastic collision (joined carts). Identify which physical sources of non-conservation (friction, air resistance) account for any discrepancy, and estimate their magnitudes from a force-sensor reading taken during impact.

### potential_energy (26.1s)

## Potential Energy

**Definition.** In classical mechanics, the *potential energy* of a system is a scalar function $U(\mathbf{r})$ that encodes the work a conservative force $\mathbf{F}$ can perform as the system moves between configurations. Formally, for a conservative force, there exists a scalar field $U$ such that

$$\mathbf{F} = -\nabla U(\mathbf{r}).$$

In one dimension this reduces to $F = -dU/dx$. Potential energy is defined only up to an additive constant; only differences $\Delta U = U(\mathbf{r}_2) - U(\mathbf{r}_1)$ are physically meaningful, and the reference level is chosen for convenience.

**Worked Example.** Consider a block of mass $m = 2\,\text{kg}$ released from rest at height $h = 5\,\text{m}$ above a table, which is itself $H = 3\,\text{m}$ above the floor. Setting the zero of gravitational potential energy at the floor, the block's initial potential energy is

$$U_i = mg(h + H) = (2)(9.8)(8) = 156.8\,\text{J}.$$

As the block reaches the table surface, $U_f = mgH = (2)(9.8)(3) = 58.8\,\text{J}$. The decrease in potential energy,

$$\Delta U = U_i - U_f = 98.0\,\text{J},$$

equals the work done by gravity, confirming $W_{\text{grav}} = mg\Delta h$. If the reference is instead placed at the table, $U_i = mgh = 98.0\,\text{J}$ and $U_f = 0$, giving the same $\Delta U$—the physics is reference-independent.

**Key Theorem (Conservation of Mechanical Energy).** When only conservative forces act on a system, the total mechanical energy $E = K + U$ is constant along any trajectory:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(K + U) = 0 \implies K_1 + U_1 = K_2 + U_2.$$

*Proof sketch.* The work–energy theorem gives $dK/dt = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{v}$. For a conservative force, $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla U$, so $\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{v} = -\nabla U \cdot \dot{\mathbf{r}} = -dU/dt$. Therefore $d(K+U)/dt = 0$. $\square$

This result extends directly to spring systems, where $U = \tfrac{1}{2}kx^2$, and to planetary orbits, where $U = -GMm/r$.

(a) Compute the elastic potential energy stored in the spring. (b) Using energy conservation, find the cart's speed at the moment of release. (c) If the track rises to a height $h = 0.15\,\text{m}$, determine whether the cart reaches that point.

### angular_momentum (54.2s)

## Angular Momentum

**Definition.** For a particle of mass $m$ moving with velocity $\mathbf{v}$ at position $\mathbf{r}$ relative to a chosen origin, the *angular momentum* about that origin is

$$\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{p} = \mathbf{r} \times m\mathbf{v},$$

where $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$ is the linear momentum introduced in the previous chapter. The magnitude is $L = mvr\sin\theta$, with $\theta$ the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. Angular momentum is a pseudovector with SI units $\mathrm{kg\cdot m^2\cdot s^{-1}}$.

**Worked Example.** A particle of mass $m = 2\,\mathrm{kg}$ moves in the $xy$-plane with constant velocity $\mathbf{v} = 3\,\hat{x}\,\mathrm{m\,s^{-1}}$. At time $t$, its position is $\mathbf{r} = (3t)\,\hat{x} + 4\,\hat{y}\,\mathrm{m}$. Compute $\mathbf{L}$ about the origin.

$$\mathbf{L} = m(\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{v}) = 2\bigl[(3t\,\hat{x}+4\,\hat{y})\times(3\,\hat{x})\bigr].$$

Using $\hat{x}\times\hat{x}=\mathbf{0}$ and $\hat{y}\times\hat{x}=-\hat{z}$:

$$\mathbf{L} = 2\bigl[9t(\hat{x}\times\hat{x}) + 12(\hat{y}\times\hat{x})\bigr] = 2(0 - 12\,\hat{z}) = -24\,\hat{z}\,\mathrm{kg\cdot m^2\cdot s^{-1}}.$$

The result is constant in time, reflecting the straight-line trajectory and constant perpendicular distance $d = 4\,\mathrm{m}$ from the origin.

**Key Theorem (Newton's Second Law for Rotation).** The net torque $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{net}}$ about a fixed origin equals the time rate of change of angular momentum:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{net}} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}.$$

*Proof sketch.* Differentiate $\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r}\times m\mathbf{v}$:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} = \dot{\mathbf{r}}\times m\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{r}\times m\dot{\mathbf{v}} = m(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{v}) + \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} + \boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{net}}.$$

**Corollary (Conservation).** When $\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\mathrm{net}} = \mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{L}$ is constant—a result central to planetary orbits and gyroscopic stability.

**Lab Exercise.** Attach a mass $m$ to a string of length $R$ and swing it in a horizontal circle on a frictionless surface. (a) Express $\mathbf{L}$ in terms of $m$, $R$, and angular speed $\omega$. (b) If the string is shortened to $R/2$ while the surface remains frictionless, use conservation of angular momentum to find the new $\omega'$. (c) Verify that the kinetic energy changes and identify the agent responsible for the work done.

### energy_conservation (14.0s)

## Energy Conservation

**Definition.** The *mechanical energy* of a particle is the scalar sum of its kinetic and potential energies:

$$E = K + U = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + U(\mathbf{r}).$$

A force $\mathbf{F}$ is *conservative* if it can be written as the negative gradient of a scalar potential, $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla U$. For a system subject exclusively to conservative forces, the total mechanical energy $E$ is constant throughout the motion — a statement known as the **principle of conservation of mechanical energy**.

**Proof sketch.** Begin with Newton's second law, $m\dot{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{F} = -\nabla U$. Take the dot product of both sides with the velocity $\mathbf{v}$:

$$m\mathbf{v}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{v}} = -\nabla U \cdot \mathbf{v}.$$

The left side is $\frac{d}{dt}\!\left(\frac{1}{2}mv^2\right) = \dot{K}$, and the right side equals $-\frac{dU}{dt}$ by the chain rule. Therefore $\dot{K} + \dot{U} = 0$, which integrates immediately to $E = K + U = \text{const}$.

**Worked Example.** A block of mass $m = 2\,\text{kg}$ is released from rest at height $h = 5\,\text{m}$ on a frictionless inclined plane. Find the speed at the base.

Setting the base as the reference level $U = 0$:

$$E_i = mgh + 0 = (2)(9.8)(5) = 98\,\text{J}.$$

At the base all energy is kinetic:

$$E_f = \frac{1}{2}mv_f^2.$$

Equating $E_i = E_f$:

$$v_f = \sqrt{2gh} = \sqrt{2(9.8)(5)} \approx 9.9\,\text{m/s}.$$

Note the result is independent of the incline angle, depending only on the vertical drop $h$.

**Key Theorem (Work–Energy Theorem for Conservative Systems).** The work done by all forces along any path connecting two points equals the decrease in potential energy:

$$W = \Delta K = -\Delta U \implies \Delta E = 0.$$

**Lab Exercise.** Mount a spring (spring constant $k$) vertically and attach a mass $m$. Compress the spring by $x_0$ and release. Using energy conservation, derive the maximum height $h$ the mass rises above the release point:

$$h = \frac{kx_0^2}{2mg}.$$

Measure $x_0$, $m$, and $k$ experimentally; compare the predicted $h$ with direct observation and compute the percentage discrepancy attributable to non-conservative effects such as air resistance and internal spring damping.

### orbital_mechanics (70.5s)

## Orbital Mechanics

**Definition.** Orbital mechanics is the branch of classical mechanics governing the motion of bodies under gravitational influence. For a two-body system consisting of masses $M$ and $m$ (with $M \gg m$), Newton's law of universal gravitation yields the central-force equation

$$\ddot{\mathbf{r}} = -\frac{GM}{r^2}\hat{\mathbf{r}},$$

where $\mathbf{r}$ is the position vector of $m$ relative to $M$, $r = |\mathbf{r}|$, and $G = 6.674 \times 10^{-11}\ \mathrm{N\,m^2\,kg^{-2}}$. Because the force is purely radial, the specific angular momentum

$$\mathbf{L} = \mathbf{r} \times \dot{\mathbf{r}}$$

is conserved: $\dot{\mathbf{L}} = \mathbf{r} \times \ddot{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{0}$. Conservation of $\mathbf{L}$ confines the motion to a fixed plane perpendicular to $\mathbf{L}$, and a standard integration of the radial equation shows that the resulting trajectories are conic sections (circles, ellipses, parabolas, or hyperbolas), with the massive body $M$ at one focus.

**Worked Example.** Consider the Earth–Moon system. Taking $M_\oplus = 5.972 \times 10^{24}\ \mathrm{kg}$ and the Moon's mean orbital radius $a = 3.844 \times 10^{8}\ \mathrm{m}$, the orbital speed follows from the circular-orbit condition (centripetal acceleration equals gravitational acceleration):

$$v_c = \sqrt{\frac{GM_\oplus}{a}} = \sqrt{\frac{(6.674\times10^{-11})(5.972\times10^{24})}{3.844\times10^{8}}} \approx 1.018\ \mathrm{km\,s^{-1}}.$$

The corresponding period is $T = 2\pi a/v_c \approx 2.37 \times 10^6\ \mathrm{s} \approx 27.4\ \mathrm{days}$, consistent with the observed synodic month.

**Key Theorem (Kepler's Third Law).** For any elliptical orbit with semi-major axis $a$, the square of the orbital period $T$ satisfies

$$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM}\,a^3.$$

*Proof sketch.* Conservation of $\mathbf{L}$ implies the radius vector sweeps area at the constant rate $dA/dt = L/2$. Integrating over one full period gives the total ellipse area $\pi ab$, where $b = a\sqrt{1-e^2}$ is the semi-minor axis and $e$ the orbital eccentricity. Substituting $L = \sqrt{GMa(1-e^2)}$ and simplifying yields the stated relation, independent of $e$.
