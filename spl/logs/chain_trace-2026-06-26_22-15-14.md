### displacement (19.1s)

## Displacement

Imagine you walk three blocks east and then turn around and walk one block west, ending up two blocks east of where you started. How far did you walk? You walked four blocks—that is your *distance*. But if I ask what your *displacement* was, I am only interested in the straight line from your starting point to your final position. Displacement is a fundamental concept in physics that allows us to define change in position using vectors.

Mathematically, displacement ($\Delta \vec{x}$) is defined as the vector difference between an object's final position ($\vec{r}_f$) and its initial position ($\vec{r}_i$). We write this relationship concisely as:
$$\Delta \vec{x} = \vec{r}_f - \vec{r}_i$$
Since displacement is a change in position, it must be a vector quantity—meaning it has both magnitude (size) and direction. It answers the question, "How far did you end up from where you began?"

Consider a car traveling along an axis. If its starting position is $x_i = 5$ meters and its final position is $x_f = -3$ meters, what was its displacement? We calculate:
$$\Delta x = (-3 \text{ m}) - (5 \text{ m}) = -8 \text{ m}$$
The negative sign indicates the direction of travel (e.g., 8 meters to the left). Even though the car traveled $8$ meters in total distance, its displacement was $-8$ meters.

## Average Acceleration

Imagine driving a car that needs to slow down drastically for an intersection. You might start at $20 \text{ m/s}$ and need to stop completely over the next $4$ seconds. Did you brake constantly, or did your braking force change? Even if your braking effort wasn't constant (meaning instantaneous acceleration was changing), we can calculate the *average* rate of deceleration over that entire time period. This average value provides a single number that summarizes the overall change in velocity, making it incredibly useful for analyzing motion over intervals where forces might not be uniform.

Mathematically, average acceleration ($\bar{a}$) is defined as the total change in an object's velocity ($\Delta \vec{v}$) divided by the time interval ($\Delta t$) over which that change occurs. It is fundamentally a rate of change: $\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}$. Since velocity is measured in meters per second ($\text{m/s}$), and time in seconds ($\text{s}$), the units for average acceleration are $\text{m/s}^2$. This simple definition shows that average acceleration does not depend on the path taken, only on the starting point, ending point, and the elapsed time.

Consider a baseball thrown up into the air, reaching its peak height at $3$ seconds. If it was initially launched from ground level ($v_i = 0 \text{ m/s}$), what is its average acceleration during those first three seconds? We know that gravity acts downwards, giving it an average acceleration due to gravity of approximately $-9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. Let's verify this using the definition: The final velocity at the peak is $v_f = 0 \text{ m/s}$.
$$\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta v}{\Delta t} = \frac{v_f - v_i}{t} = \frac{0 \text{ m/s} - v_i}{3 \text{ s}}$$
If we solve for $v_i$ by considering the full flight time, or simply recognize that $\bar{a}$ must equal $-g$, we calculate: $\bar{a} = -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$. This confirms that even though instantaneous acceleration changes (it's always $-g$), the calculation using initial and final states correctly captures the overall change in motion.

This formal definition ($\bar{a} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t}$) is robust because it relies only on kinematics, not forces. It allows us to predict the average rate of deceleration or acceleration over any measured period, regardless of how smoothly or violently that change occurred.

## Graphical Analysis Of Motion

Imagine tracking a car's journey using GPS data. Instead of just seeing the final distance, you can plot its entire movement over time—when it sped up, when it stopped, and how quickly it was moving at every second. This process is called graphical analysis of motion, and it is one of the most powerful tools in physics because it allows us to convert complex physical ideas (like velocity or acceleration) into simple geometric measurements (like slope and area).

Mathematically, we represent motion using graphs where time ($t$) is typically on the horizontal axis. The type of graph determines what physical quantity is being analyzed: a position-versus-time graph ($\Delta x$ vs. $t$), or a velocity-versus-time graph ($v$ vs. $t$).

The core principle rests on two relationships: **slope** and **area**. On an $x$ vs. $t$ graph, the slope represents instantaneous velocity, since slope is defined as $\text{rise}/\text{run} = \Delta x / \Delta t$. Conversely, on a $v$ vs. $t$ graph, the area under the curve calculates displacement ($\Delta x$), because displacement is the integral of velocity over time $\int v(t) dt$.

Consider a runner who starts at rest and accelerates uniformly to $10 \text{ m/s}$ over 4 seconds. We plot $v$ vs. $t$: this graph forms a straight line starting at $(0, 0)$ and ending at $(4 \text{ s}, 10 \text{ m/s})$. The total displacement of the runner is found by calculating the area under this triangle: $\Delta x = A = (1/2) \times \text{base} \times \text{height}$. Here, $A = (1/2)(4 \text{ s})(10 \text{ m/s}) = 20 \text{ m}$. This calculation confirms that the total displacement is independent of whether we calculate it using kinematics equations or geometry.

**Why this works:** The fundamental concept connecting these graphs is calculus. Slope ($\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}$) gives us velocity, and integrating velocity over time yields displacement ($\Delta x$). Graphically, calculating the area under a curve is simply the practical application of integration—it sums up the instantaneous values (velocity) across a continuous interval (time).
