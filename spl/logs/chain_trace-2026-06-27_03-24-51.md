### 人 (12.8s)

## 人

Have you ever watched someone walk away from you down a long street? Without thinking, you recognize the shape instantly — two legs, a body in motion. That silhouette is one of the oldest images in Chinese writing.

**Picture this.** The character 人 (pronounced *rén*) looks like a stick figure of a person seen from the side, mid-stride. Two strokes: one leaning left, one leaning right, propping each other up like two legs in balance:

$$\text{人}$$

Both strokes matter equally. Remove either one and the figure falls. This captures something poetic: a person is most stable when supported — by others, by community.

**Simple rule.** 人 means *person* or *human being*. It appears everywhere in Chinese. Whenever you see 人 inside or beside another character, it usually signals that the meaning involves a person or a human role. Think of it as a building block — a **radical** (the term for a character used as a component inside other characters).

**Try it yourself.** Look at these two characters:

- 大 (*dà*, big) — 人 with arms stretched wide, as if saying "this big!"
- 从 (*cóng*, to follow) — two 人 side by side, one person following another.

Can you spot the 人 hidden inside each one? Once you see it, you'll never unsee it — and that's exactly how learning characters works: one human figure at a time.

### 从 (12.6s)

## 从

**Everyday example.** Imagine you are walking to school and your little sibling follows right behind you — stepping where you step, turning where you turn. That image of one person trailing another is exactly what this character captures.

**Picture this.** Look at 从 carefully. Do you see two copies of 人 (rén), the character for *person*, placed side by side?

$$\text{人} + \text{人} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{从}$$

The first 人 walks ahead; the second 人 walks just behind. Together they draw a tiny scene: *following*. Chinese writing often works this way — stack or pair familiar shapes to paint a new idea.

**Simple rule.** When you see 从 (cóng), think **follow** or **from**. The character answers two questions at once: *Who comes after whom?* (one person follows another) and *Where did something start?* (coming *from* a place). So 从北京 means "from Beijing," and 跟从 means "to follow along."

**Try it yourself.** Cover the right half of 从 with your finger. What do you see? Now cover the left half. Both halves are identical — 人. Uncover both and watch the story appear: two people, one behind the other. Next time you read a sentence with 从, pause and picture that little procession. Ask yourself: who is following whom, or where is the journey starting *from*?

### 众 (13.5s)

## 众

**Everyday example.** Think about a busy market on a Saturday morning — shoppers everywhere, voices overlapping, energy buzzing. In Chinese, that feeling of *many people gathered together* is captured by a single character: **众** (*zhòng*), meaning "crowd" or "multitude."

**Picture this.** Look closely at 众. Do you see three copies of the character **人** (*rén*), which means "person"?

$$\text{人} + \text{人} + \text{人} = \text{众}$$

One person stands alone on top; two more stand side by side below. Visually, the group is growing — one leads, two follow, and together they form a crowd. Chinese writing is full of this kind of visual logic, where meaning is *built* from smaller pieces.

**Simple rule.** When a component is repeated three times in Chinese, it often signals *abundance* or *intensity*. Three 人 (people) → 众 (many people / the masses). This pattern — triple repetition equals amplification — appears throughout the writing system and is a handy shortcut once you notice it.

**Try it yourself.** Cover the character and reconstruct it from memory: draw one 人 on top, then two 人 side by side beneath it. Say *zhòng* aloud as you write. Now think of a sentence: *众人* (*zhòng rén*) means "everyone" — literally "crowd-person." Can you guess what 众多 (*zhòng duō*, "crowd-many") might mean? That's right: *numerous*.
