### position (19.0s)

## Position

**Definition.** In classical mechanics and analytic geometry, the *position* of a point particle is a vector that specifies its location relative to a chosen reference point called the *origin*. In an $n$-dimensional Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$, the position vector is written

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i(t)\,\hat{e}_i,$$

where $x_i(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ are the scalar coordinates and $\{\hat{e}_i\}$ is an orthonormal basis. In three dimensions this reduces to the familiar form $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\imath} + y(t)\,\hat{\jmath} + z(t)\,\hat{k}$. Position is a *state variable*: it is frame-dependent and changes with the choice of origin or basis, but the physical separation between two points is invariant.

**Worked Example.** A particle moves along the curve parametrised by

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \bigl(3t^2 - 1\bigr)\,\hat{\imath} + \bigl(2t^3\bigr)\,\hat{\jmath}, \quad t \in [0, 2].$$

At $t = 1$, the position is $\mathbf{r}(1) = 2\,\hat{\imath} + 2\,\hat{\jmath}$, a displacement of magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(1)| = \sqrt{4 + 4} = 2\sqrt{2}$ from the origin. The *displacement* between $t = 0$ and $t = 1$ is

$$\Delta\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(1) - \mathbf{r}(0) = (2 - (-1))\,\hat{\imath} + (2 - 0)\,\hat{\jmath} = 3\,\hat{\imath} + 2\,\hat{\jmath}.$$

Note that displacement $\Delta\mathbf{r}$ is *not* the arc length along the curve; the two coincide only for straight-line motion.

**Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Kinematics).** If a particle's velocity $\mathbf{v}(t) = \dot{\mathbf{r}}(t)$ is integrable on $[t_0,\, t]$, then position is recovered by

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t} \mathbf{v}(\tau)\,d\tau.$$

*Proof sketch.* This follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied component-wise: each scalar coordinate satisfies $x_i(t) - x_i(t_0) = \int_{t_0}^{t} \dot{x}_i(\tau)\,d\tau$, and assembling the components yields the vector statement. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Exercise.** A drone is observed to have velocity $\mathbf{v}(t) = (6t)\,\hat{\imath} + (4 - 2t)\,\hat{\jmath}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$ with initial position $\mathbf{r}(0) = (1, 0)\ \text{m}$.

1. Determine $\mathbf{r}(t)$ for all $t \geq 0$.
2. Find the time $t^*$ at which the $y$-coordinate is maximised, and compute $|\mathbf{r}(t^*)|$.
3. Sketch the trajectory in the $xy$-plane for $t \in [0, 4]$ and identify whether the path is a conic section.

### time (16.5s)

## Time

**Definition.** In classical mechanics and everyday analysis, *time* is a one-dimensional, totally ordered, continuous parameter $t \in \mathbb{R}$ that indexes the state of a system. Formally, a *time domain* is a connected subset $\mathcal{T} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, typically $[0, T]$ or $[0, \infty)$. A *trajectory* is a function $\mathbf{x} : \mathcal{T} \to \mathcal{S}$, where $\mathcal{S}$ is the state space. The *rate of change* of $\mathbf{x}$ at time $t$ is the derivative

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\mathbf{x}(t + \Delta t) - \mathbf{x}(t)}{\Delta t},$$

provided the limit exists. In special relativity, time is demoted from an absolute parameter to one coordinate of a four-dimensional spacetime manifold $(\mathbb{R}^{1,3}, \eta)$, where $\eta = \operatorname{diag}(-1, 1, 1, 1)$ is the Minkowski metric, and proper time $\tau$ satisfies $d\tau^2 = -\eta_{\mu\nu}\, dx^\mu dx^\nu / c^2$.

**Worked Example.** A particle moves under constant acceleration $a = 2\ \mathrm{m/s^2}$ starting from rest at $x_0 = 0$. Its trajectory is given by Newton's second law integrated twice:

$$v(t) = \int_0^t a\, dt' = at, \qquad x(t) = \int_0^t v(t')\, dt' = \tfrac{1}{2}at^2.$$

At $t = 3\ \mathrm{s}$, the position is $x(3) = \tfrac{1}{2}(2)(9) = 9\ \mathrm{m}$ and the velocity is $v(3) = 6\ \mathrm{m/s}$. This illustrates how specifying initial conditions at a reference time $t_0 = 0$ uniquely determines the future trajectory.

**Key Theorem (Uniqueness of Trajectories — Picard–Lindelöf).** Let $f : \mathcal{T} \times \mathcal{S} \to \mathcal{S}$ be continuous and Lipschitz continuous in its second argument with constant $L$. Then the initial value problem

$$\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t) = f(t, \mathbf{x}(t)), \quad \mathbf{x}(t_0) = \mathbf{x}_0,$$

has a *unique* solution on some interval $[t_0 - \epsilon,\, t_0 + \epsilon]$. This guarantees that causal, deterministic evolution is well-posed: a system's present state, together with the dynamical law, uniquely determines past and future trajectories.

**Lab Exercise.** Given the differential equation $\dot{x}(t) = -kx(t)$ with $k = 0.5\ \mathrm{s}^{-1}$ and $x(0) = 10$:
1. Solve analytically and express $x(t)$ in closed form.
2. Compute the *half-life* $t_{1/2}$ such that $x(t_{1/2}) = 5$.
3. Numerically approximate the solution using Euler's method with step size $\Delta t = 0.1\ \mathrm{s}$ over $[0, 4]$ and compare the accumulated error at $t = 4\ \mathrm{s}$ against the exact solution.
