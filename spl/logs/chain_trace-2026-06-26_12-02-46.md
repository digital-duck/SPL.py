### 木 (13.9s)

## 木

Think about the last time you sat under a tree. There was a thick trunk in the middle, branches stretching upward toward the light, and roots gripping the ground below. Now imagine someone thousands of years ago tried to capture that whole picture in a single brushstroke. That is exactly how the character 木 was born.

**Picture this.** Draw a vertical line — that is the trunk. Add a short horizontal stroke across the middle — those are the branches spreading left and right. Finally, two small diagonal lines at the bottom lean outward like roots anchoring the tree to the earth. Put it together and you have 木, pronounced *mù*, meaning *tree* or *wood*.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot 木 hiding inside a larger character, it almost always signals a connection to trees, plants, or wooden things. It is one of the most productive *radicals* — a building-block piece that hints at meaning — in the entire writing system.

**Try it yourself.** Look at these two characters and notice 木 on the left side of each:

- 桌 (*zhuō*) — a table (wood you work on)  
- 椅 (*yǐ*) — a chair (wood you sit on)

Both objects are traditionally made of wood, so both borrow 木 as their meaning-hint. You have just used one elemental shape to unlock two new words. That is the quiet power of 木: a single tree that grows an entire forest of characters.

### 女 (13.8s)

## 女

**Everyday example.** Think about the word "woman" in English — it appears inside dozens of other words: "woman," "womanly," "chairwoman." Chinese works the same way, and 女 (*nǚ*) is one of its most powerful building blocks, meaning **woman** or **female**.

**Picture this.** The ancient form of 女 shows a figure kneeling gracefully with arms crossed — a simple human silhouette. Over thousands of years, brush strokes smoothed it into three elegant lines: a sweeping curve on top, and two crossing strokes below. You are not memorizing an abstract shape; you are recognizing a person.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot 女 hiding inside a larger character, it carries a hint of femininity or relationship. For example:

$$\text{女} + \text{子} = \text{好} \quad (\text{hǎo, "good"})$$

A woman (女) beside a child (子) creates the character for *good* — a snapshot of warmth and care frozen into a single symbol.

**Try it yourself.** Write 女 three times, saying *nǚ* aloud each time. Then look up these characters: 妈 (mā, "mother"), 姐 (jiě, "elder sister"), and 她 (tā, "she"). Can you spot 女 hiding in each one? Once you can, you have unlocked a key that opens hundreds of Chinese characters — and you have only just begun.

### 人 (14.5s)

## 人

**Everyday example.** Imagine you are standing at a crosswalk, watching people pass by. Every single one of them — tall, short, young, old — shares one thing: they are all *human beings*. In Chinese, that single idea is captured by one character: **人** (*rén*), meaning *person* or *human*.

**Picture this.** Look at the character 人 and tilt your head slightly. Do you see it? It looks like a person walking — two diagonal strokes form two legs mid-stride. The left stroke is the body leaning forward; the right stroke is a leg pushing off the ground. This character is a *pictograph*, a tiny drawing that became a word. Ancient scribes literally sketched a walking figure, and over thousands of years it simplified into the elegant shape we see today.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot 人 inside a more complex character, it almost always carries the meaning *human* or *person*. Think of it as a building block — like the number $1$ in arithmetic, it is the fundamental unit from which bigger ideas are constructed. Just as $1 + 1 = 2$, combining 人 with other elements creates new meanings.

**Try it yourself.** Draw 人 slowly: start with the left stroke (top-center, sweeping down-left), then add the right stroke (starting slightly higher, sweeping down-right). Say *rén* aloud as you write it. Can you feel the walking figure taking shape under your pen?

### 子 (17.4s)

## 子

**Everyday example.** Think of a small child wrapped up snugly, arms reaching out to the sides — curious, dependent, full of potential. That living image is exactly what the character 子 (*zǐ*) was first drawn to capture.

**Picture this.** The ancient form of 子 looked like a swaddled infant: a round head on top, a compact body, and two little arms stretching outward. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the core shape survived. Today 子 is written in three strokes — a curved "arm" sweeping left, a short horizontal bar, and a descending hook — yet you can still sense the baby tucked inside.

**Simple rule.** 子 means *child* (or *son*). On its own it reads *zǐ*. But its real power comes from combination: 子 acts as a **building block** — a small, reusable piece — that slots into hundreds of other characters. Whenever you spot 子 inside a larger character, it often hints at ideas related to *offspring*, *smallness*, or *new beginnings*. For example, 孩 (*hái*, child) and 孙 (*sūn*, grandchild) both carry 子 as their structural heart.

**Try it yourself.** Write 子 slowly three times, saying *zǐ* aloud each time. Then look at the character 好 (*hǎo*, good): can you find 子 on the right side? The ancient scribes reasoned that a *woman* (女) beside a *child* (子) equals *goodness* — a family complete. You just decoded your first compound!

### 宀 (12.4s)

## 宀

**Everyday example.** Think about the feeling you get when you step inside on a rainy day — suddenly you are dry, warm, and protected. That sense of *being covered* is exactly what the component 宀 (pronounced *mián*) brings to Chinese characters.

**Picture this.** Draw a small triangle with a dot at the very top and two short feet hanging down at the bottom. That little shape is 宀. It looks almost exactly like a bird's-eye view of a pitched roof — a shelter over your head. In ancient Chinese writing, it was drawn even more like a house with walls.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 宀 sitting at the *top* of a character, that character almost always has something to do with **enclosed space, shelter, or home life**. It is a silent promise from the character: "something domestic or protected lives under this roof." For example:
- 家 (*jiā*) = home (a pig under a roof — livestock kept inside)
- 安 (*ān*) = peace (a woman under a roof — safe indoors)
- 宝 (*bǎo*) = treasure (valuables stored under a roof)

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 室 (*shì*, "room") and 宫 (*gōng*, "palace"). Can you spot 宀 crowning each one? Write the roof stroke first — one dot, one hat — and notice how it changes the whole character's meaning from "outside" to "inside."

### 马 (12.5s)

## 马

**Everyday example.** Think about the last time you saw a horse — maybe in a movie, a park, or a picture book. That powerful animal with flowing mane and four strong legs has inspired humans for thousands of years. In Chinese, the word for *horse* is **马** (pronounced *mǎ*, with a dipping-then-rising tone).

**Picture this.** The character 马 is one of Chinese writing's great treasures: it started as a *pictograph*, a tiny drawing of a real horse. Ancient versions, carved on oracle bones around 3,000 years ago, showed a horse with a head, mane, legs, and tail. Over centuries, artists simplified those strokes into the compact symbol we use today. If you squint at 马, you can still spot the horse's head at the top and its legs sweeping downward.

**Simple rule.** 马 belongs to a special group of characters called *radicals* — building blocks that appear inside hundreds of other characters. Whenever you see 马 hiding inside a more complex character, there is almost always a connection to horses, riding, or movement. For example, 骑 (*qí*) means *to ride*, and 马 lives right inside it.

**Try it yourself.** Trace 马 slowly with your finger: start with the head strokes, then the body, then the four short strokes at the bottom representing legs. Count them — exactly four, just like a real horse. How many horse-related words can you guess might contain 马?

### 从 (19.6s)

## 从

**Everyday example.** Imagine you are walking behind a friend who knows the way through a maze. You follow their every step — wherever they go, you go too. That relationship, *one person following another*, is exactly what this character captures.

**Picture this.** Look at 从 carefully. Do you see two human figures? The character 人 means *person*. Now place one 人 right behind another 人, and you get 从. The visual tells the whole story: a person trails a person. Ancient Chinese scribes drew characters to *look* like what they meant — a system called pictographic writing.

**Simple rule.** 从 (pronounced *cóng*) carries two connected meanings that both flow from the image of following:

- *to follow* or *to comply with* — you follow a rule, a leader, or a path
- *from* — you trace something back to its origin, the way you might follow a river upstream to its source

So when you see 从, ask: *who or what is being followed, and where does it lead?*

**Try it yourself.** Write 人 twice, the second one tucked just behind the first. Notice how the spacing creates a sense of motion — the second person is catching up. Now you have drawn 从 from memory. Say *cóng* aloud and think: *one follows one*. That tiny sentence is your anchor whenever this character appears.

### 口 (14.9s)

## 口

**Everyday example.** Every morning you wake up, yawn, and say "good morning." In that simple act, you use one of the most powerful tools you have: your mouth. In Chinese writing, that very idea — *mouth* — is captured in a single, unforgettable shape.

**Picture this.** Imagine looking straight at an open mouth from the front. You see four sides forming a small square opening: two lips on top and bottom, two corners on the sides. Now simplify that image into the fewest possible strokes. What you get is □ — a clean, four-sided box. That is 口 *(kǒu)*, the Chinese character for *mouth*.

**Simple rule.** 口 is a **pictograph** — a picture that became a word. Its meaning is direct: anything related to a mouth, an opening, or a hole. The stroke count is just **three strokes**: a left vertical stroke, a horizontal top stroke (written together as an "L" corner), a bottom stroke, and a right vertical stroke that closes the box. Because mouths are used for speaking, eating, and openings of all kinds, 口 appears inside hundreds of other characters as a building block called a **radical** (部首, *bùshǒu*) — a shared piece that hints at meaning.

**Try it yourself.** Look at these characters: 叫 (*jiào*, to shout), 吃 (*chī*, to eat), 喝 (*hē*, to drink). Can you spot 口 hiding on the left side of each one? That little square is whispering: *"something happens at the mouth here."*

### 日 (13.0s)

## 日

**Everyday example.** Think about watching a sunrise. The bright circle climbs above the horizon, floods the sky with light, and marks the start of a new day. Every culture on earth has a word for that glowing disk — in Chinese, it is **日** (pronounced *rì*).

**Picture this.** The character 日 is a tiny drawing. Thousands of years ago, ancient scribes drew a circle with a dot in the middle to represent the sun. Over centuries, the circle became a rectangle (easier to carve in stone and brush on silk), and the dot stretched into a horizontal line. What you see today — $\boxed{\text{日}}$ — is that same picture, just tidied up. Four strokes form an enclosure, and one stroke cuts across the middle.

**Simple rule.** 日 carries two closely linked meanings:
1. **The sun** — the star our planet orbits.
2. **A day** — one full sunrise-to-sunrise cycle, because the sun marks each day's rhythm.

So whenever you spot 日 inside another character, it almost always hints at *light*, *time*, or *the sky*.

**Try it yourself.** Trace 日 in the air with your finger: draw a tall rectangle, then add one horizontal bar across the middle. Now look at the word 明 (*míng*, meaning *bright*): can you spot 日 on its left side, paired with 月 (moon) on the right? Two sources of light — of course that means "bright."

### 月 (14.0s)

## 月

**Everyday example.** Every month you might circle a date on a calendar, count the weeks until a holiday, or notice the moon growing from a thin crescent to a full glowing disk. In Chinese, one small character captures all of this at once: **月** (yuè).

**Picture this.** Thousands of years ago, scribes drew 月 as a simple crescent — just the curved sliver of moon you see on a clear night. Over time the crescent was squared off into the two-stroke shape we write today. Think of the outer frame as the curved sides of the crescent, and the two horizontal lines inside as stylized shadows across its face.

**Simple rule.** 月 carries two related meanings that both flow from the moon:

- *Moon* — the celestial body itself ($\text{月亮}$, yuèliang).
- *Month* — because one lunar cycle, from new moon back to new moon, takes roughly $29.5$ days, early calendars counted time in "moons." So $1 \text{ 月} = \text{January}$, $2 \text{ 月} = \text{February}$, and so on up to $12 \text{ 月} = \text{December}$.

月 also acts as a **radical** — a building-block that appears inside larger characters, often hinting at connections to the body or to time.

**Try it yourself.** What month were you born in? Write it as a number followed by 月. If you were born in March, that is $3 \text{ 月}$. Can you guess what $6 \text{ 月}$ means?

### 林 (13.8s)

## 林

**Everyday example.** Think about a park where someone planted not just one tree, but a whole cluster of them side by side. That collection of trees has a different *feel* than a single tree standing alone — it's a grove, a small forest, a place where shade multiplies and birds gather.

**Picture this.** You already know 木 ($\text{mù}$), the character for "tree" — a trunk with branches above and roots below. Now imagine placing *two* of those trees right next to each other:

$$\text{木} + \text{木} = \text{林}$$

That's exactly how 林 ($\text{lín}$) is written. Two trees, shoulder to shoulder. The character *is* its meaning: more than one tree makes a grove.

**Simple rule.** In Chinese, doubling a character often intensifies or multiplies its base idea. One tree ($\text{木}$) → two trees side by side ($\text{林}$, grove) → three trees stacked together ($\text{森}$, dense forest). Each step adds more of the same element to grow the concept:

$$\text{木} \xrightarrow{\times 2} \text{林} \xrightarrow{+\text{木}} \text{森}$$

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 林 and trace it slowly — left tree first, then right tree. Ask yourself: which part of the stroke tells you it's a *tree*? Once you see the two hidden 木 shapes, you'll never unsee them. That's the magic of building meaning from simpler pieces.

### 水 (14.1s)

## 水

**Everyday example.** Turn on a faucet and watch the water flow. It finds its way around every rock, fills every hollow, and keeps moving — always downward, always outward. That restless, adaptable quality is exactly what the Chinese character 水 (*shuǐ*) is meant to capture.

**Picture this.** The character 水 looks like a river seen from above: a central current (the vertical stroke) flanked by two splashing ripples on each side. Early versions carved into oracle bones thousands of years ago looked even more like waves. Over centuries, the strokes were simplified, but the image of flowing water stayed alive inside the shape.

**Simple rule.** 水 is one of the most productive *radicals* in Chinese — a building-block that appears inside hundreds of other characters to signal a connection to liquids or flow. Whenever you see the three-drop shorthand 氵on the left side of a character, it is 水 in disguise, whispering *"something wet lives here."* For example, 河 (*hé*, river) and 海 (*hǎi*, sea) both carry 氵.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 泳 (*yǒng*, to swim). Can you spot the 氵radical on the left? What do you think the right-hand side might contribute to the meaning? Jot down your guess — you are already reading Chinese radicals like a detective.

### 休 (10.7s)

## 休

**Everyday example.** After a long hike, what do you do when you spot a shady tree? You lean against it and take a break. That moment of relief — pausing, breathing, resting — is exactly what the character 休 (*xiū*) captures.

**Picture this.** Look at the two parts side by side:

$$\text{休} = \text{人 (person)} + \text{木 (tree)}$$

On the left is 人, meaning *person* — a simplified drawing of someone standing upright. On the right is 木, meaning *tree* — a trunk with roots below and branches above. Put them together and you see a person leaning against a tree. The picture tells the story: a human finding rest in nature.

**Simple rule.** 休 means **rest** or **to take a break**. Whenever you see this character, think of someone stepping away from effort and recovering their energy. It appears in everyday words like 休息 (*xiūxi*, "to rest") and 休假 (*xiūjià*, "vacation").

**Try it yourself.** Trace 休 slowly with your finger. First write 人 on the left — two gentle strokes forming a leaning figure. Then add 木 on the right — a vertical line, a horizontal stroke, then two outward strokes for the roots. Can you *see* the person and the tree? Now close your eyes and imagine leaning against that tree. That feeling is 休.

### 众 (12.8s)

## 众

**Everyday example.** Imagine a school hallway the moment the bell rings — students streaming out of every door, shoulder to shoulder, filling every inch of space. That buzzing, crowded feeling is exactly what the character 众 captures.

**Picture this.** Look closely at 众. You will see the character 人 — meaning *one person* — written not once, but three times, stacked into a little triangle: one person on top, two on the bottom. In ancient Chinese writing, repeating a symbol was the natural way to say "many of these." Three people huddled together becomes the picture of a *crowd*.

$$\text{人} + \text{人} + \text{人} = \text{众}$$

**Simple rule.** In Chinese, the number three often stands for "many" rather than the literal count $3$. So 众 does not mean *exactly three people* — it means *a multitude, a throng, everybody*. You will see it in words like 众多 (*zhòng duō*, "numerous") and 观众 (*guān zhòng*, "audience" — literally "watching crowd").

**Try it yourself.** Trace 众 on paper: start with the lone 人 at the top, then draw the two 人 side by side beneath it. Notice how the single figure above seems to stand before the group below — like a speaker addressing an audience. What scene comes to your mind? A concert? A classroom? A marketplace? Every reading of this character invites a different crowd.

### 吗 (9.0s)

## 吗

**Everyday example:** Imagine you want to borrow a pencil from a friend. In English you might ask, "Do you have a pencil?" In Mandarin, one simple trick turns any statement into a question — just add a single word at the end.

**Picture this:** Think of 吗 (ma) as a tiny question mark you can *speak out loud*. Take the statement "你有铅笔" (nǐ yǒu qiānbǐ) — "You have a pencil." Now attach 吗 at the end: "你有铅笔**吗**？" Instantly, it becomes "Do you have a pencil?" No word order changes, no extra helping verbs — just one syllable does all the work.

**Simple rule:** To form a yes/no question in Mandarin, take any statement and place 吗 at the very end, before the question mark:

$$\text{Statement} + \text{吗} \rightarrow \text{Yes/No Question}$$

This works because 吗 is a *question particle* — a small grammar word whose only job is to signal "I'm asking, not telling."

**Try it yourself:** Start with "他是老师" (tā shì lǎoshī) — "He is a teacher." Can you turn it into a question? Just add 吗: "他是老师**吗**？" — "Is he a teacher?" Practice with three statements you already know, and you'll have three questions for free!
