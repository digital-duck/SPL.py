### stem (0.1s)

## Stem

Let's start with something familiar: a carrot.  Think about a carrot – you pull it out of the ground, right? The main part you eat is the stem. It’s what holds the leafy greens up and connects them to the roots underneath. That's essentially what a *stem* is in plants.

Picture this: You're building with LEGO bricks.  You need a strong base to build your tower on, right? The stem acts like that base for a plant – it provides support and anchors the plant firmly into the soil. It’s how the plant gets water and nutrients from the ground.

Simply put, a *stem* is the main stalk of a plant.  It’s what holds up the leaves, flowers, and fruits. In botanical terms, we can describe it as the part of a plant that connects the root system to aerial parts like leaves and branches. It's often green but can be other colors depending on the plant.

Try it yourself: Next time you see a flower or a vegetable, take a close look at its base. Is there a thick, supportive stalk? That’s likely the stem!  You could even draw a simple picture of a plant and label the part that connects the roots to the leaves – that's the stem.

### prefix (0.1s)

## Prefix

Let’s start with a really common thing: words! Think about all the different ways we use them to describe things. Sometimes, we add little bits to the beginning of a word to completely change its meaning. That's where prefixes come in.

**Everyday Example:** Let's take the word "unhappy." It describes a feeling – a negative one! Now think about “undo.” Both words relate to reversing something, but they do so in different ways.

**Picture This:** Imagine you’re building with LEGO bricks. You might start with a basic brick (like ‘block’). Then you add a prefix—a little modification—to change it into something new: maybe a "unblocker" that removes blocks from your creation!

**Simple Rule:** A *prefix* is simply a small group of letters added to the front of a word. It alters the word’s core meaning.  It's like giving a word a little nudge in a different direction. 

**Try it Yourself:** Let’s look at "preheat." What does “pre” mean?  It means "before.” So, “preheat” means to heat something *before* you need it hot – like preheating your oven before baking!  The prefix “pre-” tells us the action happens beforehand. Now you can start spotting prefixes in other words—like "predict," "prefer," or even "rewrite"!

### suffix (0.1s)

## Suffix

Let’s talk about suffixes. It sounds complicated, but it's actually a really simple idea! Think about words like “cat” and “cats.” What changes between them? The word "cat" is a basic building block, and adding "s" to it makes it “cats”.  That little “s” at the end of “cats” is what we’re focusing on – it's a *suffix*.

**Everyday Example:** Imagine you’re describing your pet. You might say, “I have a dog.” But if you want to talk about more than one dog, you say "dogs." The "-s" at the end of “dogs” is a suffix. It tells us there's more than one thing being described.

**Picture this:** Imagine building with LEGO bricks. You start with a basic brick – let’s call it a ‘block’. Now, you add another piece onto it that says "more".  The ‘more’ part attached to the 'block' is like a suffix. It changes what the whole thing represents.

**Simple rule:** A *suffix* is simply letters added to the end of a word. These letters change the meaning or function of the word. For example, in “quickly,” the "-ly" at the end turns it from an adjective (“quick”) into an adverb (“how quickly”). 

**Try it yourself:** Let’s add a suffix to the word "happy."  What happens if we add "-ing"? The new word is “happily.” Can you guess what that suffix does? It changes "happy" into an adverb, telling us *how* something is happy.


### -ion (0.2s)

## -Ion

Let’s talk about something really fundamental to how we describe things – the suffix "-ion". It's like a little helper that changes a word's meaning and often tells us *how* something is done.

**Everyday Example:** Think about the word “action”. What does it mean? It means doing something! Like when you *action* a task, you are performing an activity. 

**Picture this:** Imagine a painter carefully *applying* paint to a canvas. The word "apply" tells us *how* they're doing it – with paint. Now, let’s change it: “application”. This means the process of applying paint – not just the paint itself.

**Simple Rule:**  The suffix "-ion" is added to verbs (words that describe actions) to turn them into nouns (words that name things). It often indicates a *process* or an *act*. 

**Try it yourself:** Let's take the verb “create”. What new word do you get if you add "-ion"? That’s right: "creation"!  “Creation” is the act of making something new.  It’s all about taking an action and turning it into a thing we can describe. You can find countless examples of words with "-ion" – like “motion”, “vision”, and “erosion”. Each one describes a specific activity or state of being.

### in- (0.2s)

## In-

Let’s talk about “in.” It sounds simple, doesn't it? But when we start to think about it carefully, it turns out to be a surprisingly powerful idea.

**Everyday Example:** Think about your favorite mug. You drink *in* coffee from it. You put your hand *in* a warm bath. You listen *in* on a conversation. Each of these actions involves something going *inside* or being contained by something else.

**Picture This:** Imagine you’re building with LEGOs. You build a wall, and the bricks are *in* that wall. The wall is *in* your room. It's all about boundaries and what’s within them.

**Simple Rule:** "In" describes anything that is contained or enclosed by something else. It’s a way of showing where things exist relative to other things.  We use it constantly!

**Try It Yourself:** Let’s use the word “in” to describe this sentence: "The cat is *in* the box." What does “in” tell us about the location of the cat?  Can you think of another example where you might use the word “in”?  For instance, "I'm running *in* a circle.”

Essentially, "in" provides a simple framework for understanding spatial relationships and boundaries. It’s a fundamental building block for more complex ideas later on.

### port (0.1s)

## Port

Let’s start with a simple example: think about loading groceries from a delivery truck onto your kitchen counter. The truck is bringing something *to* you, right? A port does essentially the same thing – it's a place where things are brought *to* for storage or transfer. 

Picture this: imagine a bustling harbor filled with ships. These ships carry goods like computers, clothes, and food from all over the world. They need somewhere to unload their cargo before it can be distributed to shops and homes. That’s where a port comes in. 

A **port** is simply a location – often a harbor – designed for receiving and dispatching goods. It's like a giant waiting room for ships and trucks!  We use the word "port" in computers too, but it has a very specific meaning. 

The simple rule is this: A port is a connection point. Just as your kitchen counter is a connection point for groceries, a computer port is a connection point for data. It's an opening on a device that allows information to be sent and received. 

Try it yourself:  When you plug in your headphones to listen to music, you’re using a port – specifically the audio port on your device! You’re connecting your device to another device to exchange sound.

### spect (0.2s)

## Spect

Let’s talk about something really cool called a “spect.” It sounds complicated, but it's actually quite simple to understand. Think about how you see things – a red apple, a blue sky, a green leaf. Each of those colors is made up of tiny bits of light, and the spect helps us figure out *exactly* what that light looks like.

**Everyday Example:** Imagine you’re looking at a rainbow after it rains. You notice that the colors aren't just randomly arranged; they seem to flow smoothly from one shade to another. That smoothness is thanks to the spect.

**Picture this:**  Imagine a painter mixing paints. They carefully add small amounts of each color to create a specific tone, like a light lavender. A spect does something similar with light – it analyzes and breaks down the light into its individual colors.

**Simple rule:**  A spect is basically a tool that tells you *which* colors are in a beam of light, and *how much* of each color there is. It’s like having a precise recipe for light! This measurement is usually represented as a graph. The horizontal axis represents the different wavelengths of light (we'll talk about wavelength soon!), and the vertical axis shows how intense or bright that particular wavelength is.

**Try it yourself:**  Let’s say you shine a flashlight onto a white wall. You see all sorts of colors reflected back. A spect would show you exactly which colors are strongest in that light – likely red, green, and blue, as those are the primary colors found in most flashlights. It's a fascinating way to understand the world around us!


### -able (0.1s)

## -Able

Let’s start with something familiar: making toast. Think about it – you put bread into a toaster, and *out* comes toasted bread. You can do this action repeatedly, right? That's where the idea of "-able" begins.

**Everyday Example:** When we say someone is "readable," we mean they have words on paper that are easy to understand. It’s about something possessing a quality – in this case, being understandable.

**Picture This:** Imagine a muddy puddle. If you throw a stone into it, the water becomes “splashable.” You can *splash* with it! The puddle has a property that allows for splashing.

**Simple Rule:**  The suffix "-able" is added to words to show that something *can be done* or *possesses a quality*. It tells us there's a possibility of an action or characteristic being present. It’s like saying, “This thing can be made…” 

**Try it Yourself:** Let's add "-able" to the word "paint." What new word do we create?  Think about what you can do with paint. The answer is "paintable"! You can paint something *paintable*. This shows how easily a substance or object can be affected by an action.

### con- (0.1s)

## Con-

A **prefix** is simply a part of a word that comes *before* the main part, and it changes the meaning of that word. The "con-" prefix does something similar.

**Everyday Example:** Let's start with the word “connect.” You use this word when you link things together – like connecting your phone to the internet.  “Connect” means to join or make a connection. 

**Picture This:** Imagine you’re building with LEGO bricks. You might *construct* something, but you could also *connect* two separate sections to build a bigger structure. 

**Simple Rule:** The "con-" prefix generally means “together,” “with,” or “alongside.” It suggests there's a relationship between things.

**Try it yourself:** Let’s look at another word: “conference.” What do you think “conference” might mean? It probably has something to do with people coming together, right?  You’re correct! ### dict (0.1s)

## Dict

Let’s start with something incredibly useful: a dictionary. Think about it – when you don't know what a word means, you look it up in a dictionary, right? It gives you the word and then explains its meaning. That’s exactly what we’re going to explore today, but instead of paper pages, we’ll be using a special tool inside computers called a “dict” – short for *dictionary*.

**Everyday Example:** Imagine you come across the word "algorithm" while reading about computer science. You've heard it before, but you don’t quite understand what it means. A dictionary can tell you that an algorithm is a set of instructions for solving a problem. 

**Picture this:**  Imagine a recipe. It lists each step in order: “First, preheat the oven… Second, mix the flour and sugar…” Each step is clear and precise – that’s similar to how an algorithm works!

**Simple Rule:** A dict is like a collection of pairs. Each pair consists of a word (the *key*) and its definition or explanation (the *value*).  It's a way for the computer to quickly find information.

**Try it yourself:** Let’s say we want to store the words "apple" and "banana" along with their meanings in a simple dict:

```
my_dict = {
    "apple": "A sweet, round fruit.",
    "banana": "A long, yellow fruit."
}
```

This `my_dict` is now our own little dict, ready to store and retrieve information! We’ll build on this idea in the next section.

### ject (0.2s)

## Ject

Let’s talk about “ject”. It sounds a bit complicated, but it's actually a really simple idea that pops up everywhere you look – even if you don’t realize it!

**Everyday example:** Imagine you’re throwing a ball. You push it forward with your hand, and it flies through the air. What makes the ball move? *You* did! Your action – pushing the ball – is what caused it to change its state (from still to moving). 

**Picture this:** Think about a domino effect. When you knock over the first domino, it hits the next, which hits the next, and so on. Each domino’s fall *causes* the next one to fall.

**Simple rule:**  A “ject” is simply something that *makes* another thing happen. It's an action or a cause. More formally, we call it an *agent*. 

In our ball example, you were the agent. In the domino effect, the first domino was the agent. The key point is that one thing leads to another because of this “ject”. 

**Try it yourself:**  Think about when you turn on a light switch. What causes the light to come on? The action of flipping the switch *is* the ject – it’s what makes the electricity flow and illuminates the room!

That initial exploration is ‘pre’-learning.  The word "pre-" tells us this happens *before* the actual process of learning begins.

### struct (0.1s)

## Struct

Let’s start with something familiar: think about a real-life address. You know, like the one on your mailbox or a letter you send? It has several pieces of information – a street name, a building number, and a postal code. Each piece represents a specific detail that helps pinpoint exactly where something is located.

**Picture this:** Imagine you're organizing a treasure hunt. You don’t just tell people “Go to the park!” That’s too vague. Instead, you might say, "Go to 123 Main Street, Apartment 4B, Anytown, CA 91234." Each part of that sentence gives a clue about where to find the treasure.

Now, in computer programming, we have something similar called a **struct**.  A `struct` is simply a way to bundle together different pieces of data into one logical unit. It's like creating a container for related information.

**Simple rule:** A struct is like a box that holds several individual items (called *fields*) – each item has its own name and a specific type of data it stores.  For example, you might create a `struct` to represent a person: it could have fields for their Name, Age, and Email Address.

**Try it yourself:** Let's say we want to store information about a book. We can make a struct like this (in a simplified way):

```
struct Book {
  title: string;   // A piece of text
  author: string;  // Another piece of text
  pages: int;      // The number of pages
}
```

This `Book` struct allows us to group together the title, author, and page count into one manageable unit. As you learn more, you’ll see how structs are used to create complex data structures in various programming languages!

### -or (0.1s)

Let’s talk about something incredibly useful: portability. Simply put, **portable** means something can be easily moved from one place to another.
