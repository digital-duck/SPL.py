## Position

**Definition.** In classical mechanics and analytic geometry, the *position* of a point particle is a vector that specifies its location relative to a chosen reference point called the *origin*. In an $n$-dimensional Euclidean space $\mathbb{R}^n$, the position vector is written

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} x_i(t)\,\hat{e}_i,$$

where $x_i(t) \in \mathbb{R}$ are the scalar coordinates and $\{\hat{e}_i\}$ is an orthonormal basis. In three dimensions this reduces to the familiar form $\mathbf{r}(t) = x(t)\,\hat{\imath} + y(t)\,\hat{\jmath} + z(t)\,\hat{k}$. Position is a *state variable*: it is frame-dependent and changes with the choice of origin or basis, but the physical separation between two points is invariant.

**Worked Example.** A particle moves along the curve parametrised by

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \bigl(3t^2 - 1\bigr)\,\hat{\imath} + \bigl(2t^3\bigr)\,\hat{\jmath}, \quad t \in [0, 2].$$

At $t = 1$, the position is $\mathbf{r}(1) = 2\,\hat{\imath} + 2\,\hat{\jmath}$, a displacement of magnitude $|\mathbf{r}(1)| = \sqrt{4 + 4} = 2\sqrt{2}$ from the origin. The *displacement* between $t = 0$ and $t = 1$ is

$$\Delta\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}(1) - \mathbf{r}(0) = (2 - (-1))\,\hat{\imath} + (2 - 0)\,\hat{\jmath} = 3\,\hat{\imath} + 2\,\hat{\jmath}.$$

Note that displacement $\Delta\mathbf{r}$ is *not* the arc length along the curve; the two coincide only for straight-line motion.

**Key Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Kinematics).** If a particle's velocity $\mathbf{v}(t) = \dot{\mathbf{r}}(t)$ is integrable on $[t_0,\, t]$, then position is recovered by

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t} \mathbf{v}(\tau)\,d\tau.$$

*Proof sketch.* This follows directly from the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus applied component-wise: each scalar coordinate satisfies $x_i(t) - x_i(t_0) = \int_{t_0}^{t} \dot{x}_i(\tau)\,d\tau$, and assembling the components yields the vector statement. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Exercise.** A drone is observed to have velocity $\mathbf{v}(t) = (6t)\,\hat{\imath} + (4 - 2t)\,\hat{\jmath}\ \text{m s}^{-1}$ with initial position $\mathbf{r}(0) = (1, 0)\ \text{m}$.

1. Determine $\mathbf{r}(t)$ for all $t \geq 0$.
2. Find the time $t^*$ at which the $y$-coordinate is maximised, and compute $|\mathbf{r}(t^*)|$.
3. Sketch the trajectory in the $xy$-plane for $t \in [0, 4]$ and identify whether the path is a conic section.
