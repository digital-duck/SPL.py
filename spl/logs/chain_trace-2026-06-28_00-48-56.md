## 一

Have you ever started counting your fingers? The first one you point out is the number $\text{one}$. This concept, represented by the character $\text{一}$, is the absolute basic unit of quantity. It's the smallest building block from which all other numbers grow—just like how every complex sentence must start with a simple subject. Understanding 'one' means understanding the fundamental idea of singularity: being single, unique, or beginning something new.

Picture this concept as standing at the starting line of a race. The number $\text{one}$ is that initial moment before any action occurs; it represents pure potential and origin. It doesn't need anything else to exist. In mathematics, we call this unit element $1$. If you have an unknown variable $x$, and you set $x=1$, you are defining the simplest possible value for that variable.

The simple rule for 'one' is: it signifies identity. When something equals $\text{one}$, it means it exists as a single, indivisible whole. For example, if we multiply any number $N$ by $1$, the answer will always be $N$. This rule holds true because multiplying by one does not change the value—it merely confirms its existence.

Try it yourself: Think about grouping objects. If you have a collection of items and you can divide them into groups of $\text{one}$, you are counting their individual identities. Can you name three things around you that represent a single, unique item?

### phrase_守株待兔 (20.5s)

## Phrase 守株待兔

Have you ever heard someone say, "Why don't you just do what worked last time?" It might sound comforting, but sometimes relying too much on past success can be dangerous! Imagine a baker who only sells bread made with wheat. Even when local grain shifts to barley or rye, the baker insists on waiting by his old wheat-selling spot, hoping for more wheat and ignoring all the new opportunities around him.

This situation perfectly illustrates the concept behind **守株待兔** (Shǒu zhū dài tù). This phrase literally means "guarding a stump while waiting for rabbits," but what it actually describes is the mistake of expecting continued success or results simply because you succeeded once before, without adapting your methods to new realities. It’s about misplaced expectation and refusing to change your strategy.

The simple rule we learn from this idiom is: Success requires continuous adaptation. Just because something worked in the past does not guarantee it will work in the future. We must be flexible thinkers who are ready to adjust their approach when the circumstances—or the "stumps"—change.

To try it yourself, think about a skill you mastered last year. What specific habits or methods did you use? Now, imagine that same goal facing you next year with new resources or restrictions. How must you intentionally modify your old routine?
