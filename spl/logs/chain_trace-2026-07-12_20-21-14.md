### measurement (16.8s)

## Measurement

Every time a doctor reads a thermometer, a pilot checks altitude, or an engineer tests a bridge, they are doing the same fundamental thing: comparing an unknown quantity to an agreed-upon standard. Without that comparison — and without a record of how reliable it is — science cannot build on itself.

**Measurement** is the process of assigning a numerical value to a physical quantity by comparing it to a defined unit. A complete measurement always has two parts: a number and a unit. Writing that a room is "5 wide" is meaningless; writing that it is $5 \text{ m}$ wide is a fact others can verify and use.

**Worked example.** A student measures the length of a laboratory bench three times and records $1.52 \text{ m}$, $1.54 \text{ m}$, and $1.53 \text{ m}$. The best estimate of the true length is the mean:

$$\bar{L} = \frac{1.52 + 1.54 + 1.53}{3} \text{ m} = 1.530 \text{ m}.$$

The spread of values ($\pm 0.01 \text{ m}$) is the measurement *uncertainty* — not a mistake, but honest information about the precision of the instrument and technique.

**Why this matters.** No measurement is exact. Every instrument has a smallest readable division, and every experimenter introduces slight variability. Reporting uncertainty lets another scientist judge whether two results truly disagree or merely reflect normal scatter. The rule is simple: a result is only as trustworthy as its stated uncertainty. A value without uncertainty is incomplete data.

Precision (how repeatable a measurement is) and accuracy (how close it is to the true value) are distinct. A miscalibrated ruler can give highly precise but inaccurate results — a distinction that has derailed real engineering projects.

**Practice.** A stopwatch records four sprint times: $9.81 \text{ s}$, $9.78 \text{ s}$, $9.84 \text{ s}$, and $9.79 \text{ s}$. Calculate the mean time and the uncertainty. If the accepted world-record time is $9.58 \text{ s}$, is this measurement accurate? Is it precise? Explain the difference in one sentence.

### accuracy (14.6s)

## Accuracy

A sharpshooter fires five rounds and all five holes cluster tightly in the upper-left corner of the target — far from the bullseye. The grouping is impressive, but the aim is wrong. This everyday scenario captures the essence of a concept that underlies every measurement in physics: **accuracy**.

**Accuracy** describes how close a measured value is to the true or accepted value. It is distinct from precision, which concerns the reproducibility of repeated measurements. A single measurement can be accurate without being precise, and precise without being accurate — but physics demands both.

Quantitatively, accuracy is often expressed as **percent error**:

$$\% \text{ error} = \left| \frac{\text{measured value} - \text{accepted value}}{\text{accepted value}} \right| \times 100\%$$

### Worked Example

A student measures the acceleration due to gravity and obtains $g_{\text{meas}} = 9.65\ \text{m/s}^2$. The accepted value is $g_{\text{acc}} = 9.80\ \text{m/s}^2$.

$$\% \text{ error} = \left| \frac{9.65 - 9.80}{9.80} \right| \times 100\% = \left| \frac{-0.15}{9.80} \right| \times 100\% \approx 1.53\%$$

A percent error below $5\%$ is generally considered acceptable in an introductory lab context, so this result is reasonably accurate.

### Why This Matters

Accuracy is a statement about **systematic error** — a consistent bias that shifts every measurement in the same direction. Sources include miscalibrated instruments, flawed experimental design, or environmental factors not accounted for. Unlike random error, systematic error does not shrink when you average more trials; it must be identified and eliminated at the source. Recognizing this distinction is the first step toward designing experiments that produce trustworthy results.

### Practice Problem

A thermometer reads $98.4\ °\text{C}$ when placed in boiling water at sea level. The accepted boiling point is $100.0\ °\text{C}$. Calculate the percent error and identify whether this represents a systematic or random source of inaccuracy.

### precision (14.6s)

## Precision

A master watchmaker and a child with a ruler both measure the same table as "about one meter long." The child's result varies wildly between attempts; the watchmaker's repeated measurements cluster tightly around 1.003 m every single time. Both may be wrong about the true length — but only the watchmaker is *precise*.

**Precision** describes the degree of *repeatability* in a set of measurements: how closely multiple measurements of the same quantity agree with one another. It is a statement about the *spread* of results, not their accuracy. A measurement tool with fine graduations and a stable procedure yields high precision; inconsistent technique or coarse markings produces low precision.

Precision is often quantified by the **uncertainty** $\delta x$ associated with a measured value $x$, expressed as $x \pm \delta x$. The *percent uncertainty* makes comparison easier:

$$\text{Percent uncertainty} = \frac{\delta x}{x} \times 100\%$$

**Worked example.** A student measures the diameter of a coin five times with a digital caliper:

$$d = 24.26,\ 24.25,\ 24.26,\ 24.27,\ 24.26 \text{ mm}$$

The spread is only $0.02\ \text{mm}$, so we report $d = 24.26 \pm 0.01\ \text{mm}$. The percent uncertainty is:

$$\frac{0.01}{24.26} \times 100\% \approx 0.04\%$$

That is a highly precise measurement — the readings are tightly grouped regardless of whether 24.26 mm matches the coin's "true" diameter.

**Why does this matter?** Precision is limited by the *smallest division* readable on an instrument and by random fluctuations in the measurement process. Reducing random error — by averaging many trials or using a finer instrument — improves precision. Systematic error, by contrast, shifts every reading the same way and cannot be averaged out; that is an accuracy problem, not a precision problem.

**Practice problem.** A student records a spring constant as $k = 45\ \text{N/m}$ with an uncertainty of $\pm 2\ \text{N/m}$. What is the percent uncertainty, and should the result be reported to the nearest whole number or to one decimal place?

### uncertainty (14.9s)

## Uncertainty

Every measurement you make carries a built-in limitation. When you step on a bathroom scale and it reads 68 kg, that number isn't perfectly exact — the scale has a finest graduation, your posture shifts, and the spring inside has manufacturing tolerances. Physics doesn't pretend these limitations don't exist; it quantifies them through **uncertainty**.

**Uncertainty** is the range within which the true value of a measured quantity is expected to lie. If you report a length as $L = 2.35 \pm 0.02 \text{ m}$, you are saying the true value almost certainly falls between $2.33 \text{ m}$ and $2.37 \text{ m}$. The $\pm 0.02 \text{ m}$ term is the **absolute uncertainty**, $\delta L$.

When combining measurements, uncertainties propagate. For a sum or difference, absolute uncertainties add:

$$\delta(A + B) = \delta A + \delta B$$

For products and quotients, **relative uncertainties** (the ratio $\delta x / x$) add instead:

$$\frac{\delta(AB)}{AB} = \frac{\delta A}{A} + \frac{\delta B}{B}$$

**Worked example.** You measure a rectangular tabletop: length $L = 1.50 \pm 0.01 \text{ m}$, width $W = 0.80 \pm 0.01 \text{ m}$. What is the area with uncertainty?

$$A = LW = (1.50)(0.80) = 1.20 \text{ m}^2$$

Relative uncertainty of $L$: $0.01/1.50 \approx 0.0067$. Relative uncertainty of $W$: $0.01/0.80 = 0.0125$. Their sum gives $\delta A / A \approx 0.019$, so $\delta A \approx 0.02 \text{ m}^2$. Reported result: $A = 1.20 \pm 0.02 \text{ m}^2$.

**Why this works.** Each independent source of error can, in the worst case, push the result in the same direction, so the worst-case bound on the combined error is the sum of individual bounds. This is conservative but rigorous — it guarantees the true value stays inside the stated range.

**Practice.** A cyclist travels $d = 120 \pm 2 \text{ m}$ in $t = 8.0 \pm 0.2 \text{ s}$. Calculate the speed $v = d/t$ and its uncertainty.
