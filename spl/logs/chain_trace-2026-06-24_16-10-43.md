### scalar_multiplication (13.9s)

## Scalar Multiplication

**Everyday example.** Imagine you have a recipe that calls for 2 cups of flour and 3 cups of sugar. If you want to triple the recipe, you multiply every ingredient by 3, giving you 6 cups of flour and 9 cups of sugar. You didn't change *what* the recipe is — you just scaled it up.

**Picture this.** A vector is like that recipe: an ordered list of numbers. If $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix}$, think of it as an arrow pointing from the origin to the point $(2, 3)$. Now pick a single number — called a **scalar** because it "scales" things. Multiply every entry of $\mathbf{v}$ by that scalar $k = 3$:

$$k\,\mathbf{v} = 3 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \\ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 6 \\ 9 \end{bmatrix}$$

The arrow still points in the same direction, but it's now three times as long.

**Simple rule.** To multiply a scalar $k$ by a vector $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{bmatrix}$, multiply each component: $k\,\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} k\,v_1 \\ k\,v_2 \\ \vdots \\ k\,v_n \end{bmatrix}$. A negative scalar like $k = -1$ flips the arrow to point the opposite way. A scalar of $0$ shrinks any vector down to the **zero vector**.

**Try it yourself.** Let $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 \\ -1 \\ 5 \end{bmatrix}$. Compute $2\,\mathbf{u}$ and $-\mathbf{u}$. Sketch the original and scaled arrows — which way does each one point?

### vector_addition (13.3s)

## Vector Addition

**Everyday example.** Imagine you walk 3 blocks east, then 4 blocks north. Your total trip isn't 3 or 4 — it's a combination of both movements. That combination is exactly what vector addition does: it joins two movements (or forces, or any directed quantities) into one.

**Picture this.** Draw an arrow pointing right, 3 units long — call it $\vec{a}$. From the tip of that arrow, draw another arrow pointing up, 4 units long — call it $\vec{b}$. Now draw one arrow from the very start to the very end. That single arrow is the **sum** $\vec{a} + \vec{b}$, also called the **resultant**. This tip-to-tail method works for any two vectors, no matter their direction.

**Simple rule.** When vectors are written as numbers, addition is even easier — just add matching components:

$$\vec{a} + \vec{b} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \\ a_2 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \\ b_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 + b_1 \\ a_2 + b_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

For our walk: $\begin{pmatrix}3\\0\end{pmatrix}+\begin{pmatrix}0\\4\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}3\\4\end{pmatrix}$. Each direction is handled independently — east stays east, north stays north.

**Try it yourself.** A drone flies $\vec{p}=\begin{pmatrix}2\\5\end{pmatrix}$, then $\vec{q}=\begin{pmatrix}4\\-1\end{pmatrix}$. What is $\vec{p}+\vec{q}$? Sketch both arrows tip-to-tail, then check by adding the components.

### linear_combination (15.0s)

## Linear Combination

**Everyday example.** Imagine you're at a smoothie bar. You pick 2 scoops of strawberry and 3 scoops of banana. Your smoothie is a *blend* of those two base ingredients, each scaled by how many scoops you chose. A **linear combination** works the same way — you take several building blocks, multiply each one by a number, and add the results together.

**Picture this.** Suppose you have two vectors, $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. A linear combination picks a number (called a **scalar**) for each one — say $a$ and $b$ — and forms:

$$a\,\mathbf{u} + b\,\mathbf{v}$$

That's it. You *scale* each vector, then *add*. With $\mathbf{u} = [1, 0]$ and $\mathbf{v} = [0, 1]$, the combination $3\mathbf{u} + 2\mathbf{v} = [3, 2]$. Change the scalars and you reach a different point.

**Simple rule.** Given vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \dots, \mathbf{v}_k$ and scalars $c_1, c_2, \dots, c_k$, the expression

$$c_1\,\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\,\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k\,\mathbf{v}_k$$

is a **linear combination** of those vectors. "Linear" means you only multiply and add — no squaring, no square roots, no other operations.

**Try it yourself.** Let $\mathbf{u} = [1, 2]$ and $\mathbf{v} = [3, -1]$. Compute $2\mathbf{u} + 4\mathbf{v}$. Then find scalars $a$ and $b$ such that $a\mathbf{u} + b\mathbf{v} = [5, 3]$. Can you reach *every* point in the plane this way?
