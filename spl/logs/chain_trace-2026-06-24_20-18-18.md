### physical_quantity (14.2s)

## Physical Quantity

**Everyday example.** You step on a bathroom scale and it reads "70 kg." You glance at a thermometer and it says "22 °C." Each reading pairs a *number* with a *unit*. That pairing — number plus unit — is what scientists call a **physical quantity**.

**Picture this.** Imagine telling a friend, "The table is 1.2." They would immediately ask, "1.2 *what*? Meters? Feet?" A bare number is meaningless for describing the real world. A physical quantity always has two parts:

$$Q = \{n\} \times [u]$$

where $\{n\}$ is the numerical value and $[u]$ is the unit. For example, a length of three meters is written $L = 3\;\text{m}$. Change the unit to centimeters and the number changes too — $L = 300\;\text{cm}$ — but the physical quantity itself stays the same.

**Simple rule.** *A physical quantity is incomplete without its unit.* The number and the unit are inseparable partners: drop either one and you lose the meaning.

Some quantities you already use daily — length, mass, time, temperature — are called **base quantities** because they cannot be broken down further. Others, like speed ($\text{m/s}$), are built by combining base quantities and are called **derived quantities**.

**Try it yourself.** Pick three objects near you. For each one, write down one physical quantity (number + unit) that describes it — perhaps its mass, its length, or its temperature.

### precision (12.7s)

## Precision

**Everyday example.** Imagine you're a doctor screening patients for a rare disease. You flag 20 people as "likely positive." But when the lab results come back, only 12 of those 20 actually have the disease. The other 8 were false alarms. How much can you trust a positive flag? That's what **precision** measures.

**Picture this.** Think of an archer shooting at a target. Precision doesn't ask whether every bullseye on the board was hit — it asks, "Of the arrows I *did* shoot, how many landed on the bullseye?" If you shot 10 arrows and 7 hit the center, your precision is 7 out of 10.

**Simple rule.** Precision is the fraction of your positive predictions that turned out to be correct:

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{\text{True Positives}}{\text{True Positives} + \text{False Positives}}$$

A **true positive** is a case you flagged positive that really is positive. A **false positive** is a case you flagged positive that is actually negative — a false alarm. Precision runs from $0$ (every flag was wrong) to $1$ (every flag was right). High precision means you rarely cry wolf.

**Try it yourself.** A spam filter flags 50 emails as spam. You check and find 45 are truly spam, but 5 are legitimate messages. What is the filter's precision? (Answer: $\frac{45}{50} = 0.90$.)

### uncertainty (15.1s)

## Uncertainty

**Everyday example.** You check the weather app and it says "70 % chance of rain." That number captures something important: you don't *know* whether it will rain, but you have a feeling — backed by data — about how likely it is. That feeling-with-a-number is what we call **uncertainty**, the honest admission that an outcome is not guaranteed.

**Picture this.** Imagine tossing a fair coin. Before it lands you cannot say "heads" with full confidence. You might say the chance is $\frac{1}{2}$, or 50 %. Now imagine a loaded coin that lands heads 90 % of the time. You are still uncertain, but *less* uncertain — you have a stronger expectation. Uncertainty lives on a spectrum: from complete ignorance ("I have no idea") to near-certainty ("I'm almost sure").

**Simple rule.** We measure uncertainty with a number called **probability**, written $P$. It always satisfies

$$0 \le P(\text{event}) \le 1.$$

- $P = 0$ means the event is impossible.
- $P = 1$ means the event is certain.
- Anything in between means some degree of uncertainty remains.

The closer $P$ sits to $0$ or $1$, the less uncertain we are. When $P = 0.5$, uncertainty is at its maximum for a yes-or-no question — a true coin-flip situation.

**Try it yourself.** Pick three everyday events — say, "the sun rises tomorrow," "I roll a six on a die," and "it snows in July where I live." Rank them from least uncertain to most uncertain, then assign each a rough probability between $0$ and $1$. Notice how the number reflects your confidence.

### order_of_magnitude (13.0s)

## Order Of Magnitude

**Everyday example.** How far is it to the grocery store? Maybe 2 kilometres. How far is it to the Moon? About 384,000 kilometres. You don't need the exact numbers to feel the enormous gap between them — one is a short walk, the other is a space voyage. That "gap" is what scientists call a difference in **order of magnitude**.

**Picture this.** Imagine a ruler where each mark is ten times bigger than the last: $1, 10, 100, 1{,}000, 10{,}000, \dots$ Every time you step one mark to the right, you have jumped one order of magnitude. The distance to the Moon ($\approx 10^{5.6}$ km) is roughly five orders of magnitude larger than a trip to the store ($\approx 10^{0.3}$ km), because $10^{5.6} / 10^{0.3} \approx 10^{5}$.

**Simple rule.** To find the order of magnitude of a number, ask: *"What power of ten is closest?"* We write this as $10^n$. A number around $500$ is closest to $10^{2.7}$, so its order of magnitude is roughly $3$ (we often round to the nearest integer). Two quantities are "the same order of magnitude" when their ratio is less than $10$.

**Try it yourself.** A housefly weighs about $0.012$ grams and a house cat about $4{,}000$ grams. How many orders of magnitude apart are they? Hint: divide, then count the powers of ten.

### significant_figures (15.9s)

## Significant Figures

**Everyday example.** You step on a bathroom scale and it reads 68 kg. A laboratory balance reads 68.042 kg. Both describe your mass, but the lab number carries more *meaningful detail*. The digits we actually trust in a measurement are called **significant figures** (or **sig figs**).

**Picture this.** Imagine you measure a table with a ruler marked in centimetres. You can confidently read 74 cm and estimate one more digit — maybe 74.3 cm. That measurement has three significant figures: every digit you wrote down carries real information about the table's length.

**Simple rule.** Count every digit that is *not* just a placeholder zero:

- $0.0052$ has **2** sig figs — the leading zeros only show where the decimal point sits.
- $4.070$ has **4** sig figs — the trailing zero after 7 is intentional; it says "we measured to the thousandths place."
- $8200$ is ambiguous — it could be 2, 3, or 4 sig figs. Scientific notation removes the doubt: $8.20 \times 10^{3}$ clearly has **3**.

When you multiply or divide, your answer keeps the fewest sig figs of any input. For example:

$$3.2 \times 4.51 = 14.432 \;\longrightarrow\; 14$$

because $3.2$ has only two significant figures.

**Try it yourself.** How many significant figures are in $0.003060$? Which digits are placeholders, and which carry real measurement information?

### approximation (13.3s)

## Approximation

**Everyday example.** You glance at your watch and tell a friend, "It's about three o'clock," even though the time reads 2:57. You just made an **approximation** — a value that is close enough to the true answer for your purpose, even if it is not exact.

**Picture this.** Imagine measuring the length of a curved garden path with a straight ruler. You lay the ruler down several times, adding up short straight segments. Your total will never perfectly match every twist of the path, but with enough small segments the number you get is *very* close. That total is an approximation of the true length.

**Simple rule.** An approximation replaces something hard to compute or measure with something simpler that stays *close enough*. We write

$$x \approx a$$

to say "$x$ is approximately equal to $a$." The gap between them is called the **error**:

$$\text{error} = |x - a|$$

A good approximation keeps this error small relative to the quantity you care about. For instance, $\pi \approx 3.14$ has an error less than $0.002$, which is perfectly fine for most everyday calculations.

**Try it yourself.** Without a calculator, estimate $\sqrt{50}$. You know $7^{2} = 49$, so $\sqrt{50} \approx 7.07$. Check: $7.07^{2} = 49.9849$ — the error is only about $0.015$. That tiny gap is the power of approximation at work.
