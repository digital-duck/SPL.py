### function (13.8s)

## Function

**Definition.** A *function* $f$ from a set $A$ to a set $B$, written $f: A \to B$, is a rule that assigns to each element $x \in A$ exactly one element $f(x) \in B$. The set $A$ is called the *domain* of $f$, and $B$ is called the *codomain*. The *range* (or image) of $f$ is the subset $\{f(x) \mid x \in A\} \subseteq B$. The defining property is uniqueness: if $x = y$ then $f(x) = f(y)$. A relation that assigns more than one output to some input is not a function.

**Worked Example.** Let $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined by $f(x) = x^2 - 3x + 2$. To evaluate $f$ at $x = 4$:

$$f(4) = 4^2 - 3(4) + 2 = 16 - 12 + 2 = 6.$$

To find the range, complete the square: $f(x) = \left(x - \tfrac{3}{2}\right)^2 - \tfrac{1}{4}$. Since a square is always non-negative, $f(x) \geq -\tfrac{1}{4}$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$, and every value $y \geq -\tfrac{1}{4}$ is achieved. Thus the range is $\left[-\tfrac{1}{4}, \infty\right)$, which is strictly smaller than the codomain $\mathbb{R}$.

**Key Theorem (Composition and Injectivity).** Let $f: A \to B$ and $g: B \to C$. Their *composition* $g \circ f: A \to C$ is defined by $(g \circ f)(x) = g(f(x))$. A function $f$ is *injective* (one-to-one) if $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$ implies $x_1 = x_2$, and *surjective* (onto) if every element of $B$ is in the range of $f$. A function that is both injective and surjective is *bijective*, and bijections admit an inverse function $f^{-1}: B \to A$ satisfying $f^{-1}(f(x)) = x$ for all $x \in A$. Proof that injectivity is preserved under composition follows directly: if $g(f(x_1)) = g(f(x_2))$, injectivity of $g$ gives $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$, then injectivity of $f$ gives $x_1 = x_2$.

### limit (15.2s)

## Limit

**Definition.** Let $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function defined on a set $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, and let $a$ be an accumulation point of $D$. We say that the **limit of $f$ as $x$ approaches $a$** equals $L \in \mathbb{R}$, written

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L,$$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$ there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for all $x \in D$ satisfying $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, we have $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$. This is the classical **$\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition** due to Weierstrass. Crucially, $f$ need not be defined at $a$ itself; the limit describes the function's *tendency*, not its value.

**Worked Example.** We verify that $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 2}(3x - 1) = 5$.

Given $\varepsilon > 0$, we need $\delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$ implies $|(3x-1) - 5| < \varepsilon$. Simplifying the target inequality:

$$|(3x - 1) - 5| = |3x - 6| = 3|x - 2|.$$

We require $3|x - 2| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $|x - 2| < \varepsilon/3$. Choosing $\delta = \varepsilon/3$, we have: if $0 < |x - 2| < \delta$, then $|f(x) - 5| = 3|x - 2| < 3\delta = \varepsilon$. $\square$

**Key Theorem (Uniqueness of Limits).** *If $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = M$, then $L = M$.*

*Proof sketch.* Suppose $L \neq M$. Set $\varepsilon = |L - M|/2 > 0$. By hypothesis, there exist $\delta_1, \delta_2 > 0$ such that $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ and $|f(x) - M| < \varepsilon$ simultaneously for $x$ within $\min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$ of $a$. The triangle inequality then gives $|L - M| \leq |f(x) - L| + |f(x) - M| < 2\varepsilon = |L - M|$, a contradiction.

### infinity (81.1s)

## Infinity

**Definition.** In mathematics, *infinity* is not a real number but a concept describing quantities without bound. Set theory sharpens this intuition through *cardinality*: two sets $A$ and $B$ are **equinumerous** — written $|A| = |B|$ — if and only if there exists a bijection between them. The central primitive of this section is **countably infinite**: a set is countably infinite if it admits a bijection with the natural numbers $\mathbb{N}$. The cardinality of $\mathbb{N}$ is denoted $\aleph_0$ (aleph-null), and any countably infinite set likewise carries cardinality $\aleph_0$. This definition challenges finite intuition: a proper subset can be equinumerous with the whole, a phenomenon impossible among finite sets.

**Worked Example.** Define $f: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{Z}$ by
$$f(n) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{n}{2} & n \text{ even} \\[6pt] -\dfrac{n+1}{2} & n \text{ odd.} \end{cases}$$
The function $f$ is a bijection: it enumerates $\mathbb{Z}$ as $0, -1, 1, -2, 2, -3, 3, \ldots$, visiting every integer exactly once. Therefore $|\mathbb{Z}| = \aleph_0$, confirming that the integers are countably infinite. Intuitively one might expect $\mathbb{Z}$ to be "twice as large" as $\mathbb{N}$, but the bijection demonstrates they are equinumerous — size comparisons between infinite sets cannot rely on naive containment.

**Key Theorem (Cantor's Theorem).** For any set $A$, $|\mathcal{P}(A)| > |A|$. Applied to $\mathbb{N}$, this yields $|\mathcal{P}(\mathbb{N})| > \aleph_0$: there are infinite sets strictly larger than $\mathbb{N}$. The proof uses *diagonalization*: suppose $g: A \to \mathcal{P}(A)$ is a surjection; then the set $D = \{x \in A \mid x \notin g(x)\}$ belongs to $\mathcal{P}(A)$ but is absent from $\mathrm{ran}(g)$, a contradiction. This single argument generates an unlimited hierarchy of infinities, each strictly exceeding the last.

### real_number (16.5s)

## Real Number

**Definition.** A *real number* is any element of the unique complete ordered field $(\mathbb{R}, +, \cdot, \leq)$. Formally, $\mathbb{R}$ is characterized by three axiom families: the field axioms (closure, associativity, commutativity, distributivity, identity, and inverse elements for $+$ and $\cdot$), the order axioms (totality, transitivity, and compatibility with field operations), and the **completeness axiom** — every non-empty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a *least upper bound* (supremum) in $\mathbb{R}$. This last axiom distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$ and is the foundation of all limit-based analysis.

The real numbers encompass the integers $\mathbb{Z}$, the rationals $\mathbb{Q}$, and the irrationals. Irrationals such as $\sqrt{2}$ and $\pi$ are precisely those reals that cannot be expressed as $p/q$ with $p, q \in \mathbb{Z},\, q \neq 0$.

**Worked Example.** We show $\sqrt{2} \in \mathbb{R}$ by constructing its supremum. Let

$$S = \{ x \in \mathbb{Q} \mid x \geq 0 \text{ and } x^2 < 2 \}.$$

$S$ is non-empty ($1 \in S$) and bounded above ($2 \notin S$ implies $x < 2$ for all $x \in S$). By completeness, $\alpha = \sup S$ exists in $\mathbb{R}$. A standard trichotomy argument shows $\alpha^2 < 2$ and $\alpha^2 > 2$ both lead to contradictions, so $\alpha^2 = 2$, i.e., $\alpha = \sqrt{2}$.

**Key Theorem (Archimedean Property).** For every $x \in \mathbb{R}$, there exists $n \in \mathbb{N}$ such that $n > x$.

*Proof sketch.* Suppose not; then $\mathbb{N}$ is bounded above by $x$. By completeness, $\sup \mathbb{N} = M$ exists. Since $M - 1$ is not an upper bound, there is $m \in \mathbb{N}$ with $m > M - 1$, giving $m + 1 > M$ — contradicting $M = \sup \mathbb{N}$. $\square$

This property implies $\mathbb{Q}$ is dense in $\mathbb{R}$: between any two reals lies a rational.

### derivative (14.3s)

## Derivative

**Definition.** Let $f : (a, b) \to \mathbb{R}$ be a real-valued function and let $x_0 \in (a, b)$. The *derivative* of $f$ at $x_0$ is defined as the limit

$$f'(x_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0)}{h},$$

provided this limit exists and is finite. When $f'(x_0)$ exists, $f$ is said to be *differentiable* at $x_0$. Geometrically, $f'(x_0)$ is the slope of the tangent line to the graph of $f$ at the point $(x_0, f(x_0))$. Analytically, it measures the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ with respect to its argument.

**Worked Example.** Let $f(x) = x^3$. We compute $f'(2)$ directly from the definition:

$$f'(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(2+h)^3 - 2^3}{h}.$$

Expanding $(2+h)^3 = 8 + 12h + 6h^2 + h^3$ and subtracting $8$ gives

$$f'(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{12h + 6h^2 + h^3}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \left(12 + 6h + h^2\right) = 12.$$

Thus the tangent line to $y = x^3$ at $x = 2$ has slope $12$, with equation $y - 8 = 12(x - 2)$.

**Key Theorem (Differentiability Implies Continuity).** *If $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, then $f$ is continuous at $x_0$.*

*Proof sketch.* Write $f(x_0 + h) - f(x_0) = \dfrac{f(x_0+h)-f(x_0)}{h} \cdot h$. As $h \to 0$, the first factor converges to $f'(x_0)$ and the second factor converges to $0$, so the product converges to $0$, establishing continuity. The converse fails: $f(x) = |x|$ is continuous at $0$ but not differentiable there, since the left- and right-hand limits of the difference quotient are $-1$ and $1$, respectively.

### riemann_sum (15.5s)

## Riemann Sum

**Definition.** Let $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ be a bounded function. A *partition* of $[a, b]$ is a finite set $P = \{x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n\}$ with $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. For each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, choose a *sample point* $c_i \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$. The **Riemann sum** of $f$ with respect to $P$ and the choice of sample points is

$$S(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(c_i)\,\Delta x_i, \qquad \Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}.$$

Geometrically, each term $f(c_i)\,\Delta x_i$ is the signed area of a rectangle of width $\Delta x_i$ and height $f(c_i)$. The sum approximates the net area between the graph of $f$ and the $x$-axis. Common choices for $c_i$ yield the *left*, *right*, and *midpoint* Riemann sums.

**Worked Example.** Approximate $\int_0^1 x^2\,dx$ using a right Riemann sum with $n = 4$ equal subintervals.

Partition: $\Delta x = \tfrac{1}{4}$, so $x_i = \tfrac{i}{4}$ for $i = 0, 1, 2, 3, 4$. Right endpoints: $c_i = x_i$.

$$S = \sum_{i=1}^{4} \left(\frac{i}{4}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{64}\left(1^2 + 2^2 + 3^2 + 4^2\right) = \frac{1}{64}(1 + 4 + 9 + 16) = \frac{30}{64} = \frac{15}{32} = 0.46875.$$

The exact value is $\tfrac{1}{3} \approx 0.3333$, so the error is $\approx 0.136$. Increasing $n$ reduces this error.

**Key Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then for any sequence of partitions whose mesh $\|P\| = \max_i \Delta x_i \to 0$, the Riemann sums converge to the definite integral:

$$\lim_{\|P\| \to 0} S(f, P) = \int_a^b f(x)\,dx.$$

More generally, $f$ is Riemann integrable on $[a, b]$ if and only if it is bounded and continuous *almost everywhere* (Lebesgue's criterion).

### definite_integral (16.3s)

## Definite Integral

**Definition.** Let $f$ be a bounded function on a closed interval $[a, b]$. Partition $[a, b]$ into $n$ subintervals by choosing points $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. For each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ of width $\Delta x_i = x_i - x_{i-1}$, select a sample point $x_i^* \in [x_{i-1}, x_i]$. The **definite integral** of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ is defined as the limit of Riemann sums:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = \lim_{\|\mathcal{P}\| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*)\, \Delta x_i,$$

where $\|\mathcal{P}\| = \max_i \Delta x_i$ is the mesh of the partition. When this limit exists and is finite, $f$ is said to be **Riemann integrable** on $[a, b]$. Geometrically, the definite integral represents the **signed area** between the graph of $f$ and the $x$-axis: regions where $f > 0$ contribute positively, and regions where $f < 0$ contribute negatively.

**Worked Example.** Compute $\displaystyle\int_0^2 (3x^2)\, dx$ using the definition with a uniform partition of $n$ subintervals and right endpoints $x_i^* = \frac{2i}{n}$.

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} 3\!\left(\frac{2i}{n}\right)^2 \cdot \frac{2}{n} = \frac{24}{n^3}\sum_{i=1}^{n} i^2 = \frac{24}{n^3} \cdot \frac{n(n+1)(2n+1)}{6}.$$

Taking $n \to \infty$:

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{4(n+1)(2n+1)}{n^2} = \lim_{n\to\infty} 4\!\left(1+\tfrac{1}{n}\right)\!\left(2+\tfrac{1}{n}\right) = 8.$$

**Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (Part 2).** If $F$ is any antiderivative of $f$ on $[a,b]$—that is, $F'(x) = f(x)$—then:

$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx = F(b) - F(a).$$

This theorem bridges the apparently distinct operations of differentiation and integration. Applying it to the example above: $F(x) = x^3$, so $F(2) - F(0) = 8 - 0 = 8$, confirming the Riemann sum calculation.

### differential_equation (14.8s)

## Differential Equation

**Definition.** A *differential equation* (DE) is an equation that relates an unknown function to one or more of its derivatives. If the unknown depends on a single independent variable, the equation is called an *ordinary differential equation* (ODE); if it involves partial derivatives with respect to multiple variables, it is a *partial differential equation* (PDE). The *order* of a DE is the highest derivative that appears. A first-order ODE has the general form

$$F\!\left(x,\, y,\, \frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 0,$$

while a second-order ODE includes $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$. A DE is *linear* if $y$ and all its derivatives appear to the first power with no products among them; otherwise it is *nonlinear*.

**Worked Example.** Consider the first-order linear ODE

$$\frac{dy}{dx} - 2y = 0, \quad y(0) = 3.$$

Separating variables gives $\dfrac{dy}{y} = 2\,dx$. Integrating both sides yields $\ln|y| = 2x + C$, so $y = Ae^{2x}$ for an arbitrary constant $A$. Applying the initial condition $y(0) = 3$ forces $A = 3$, giving the unique solution

$$y(x) = 3e^{2x}.$$

One can verify by substitution: $y' = 6e^{2x} = 2 \cdot 3e^{2x} = 2y$. $\checkmark$

**Key Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness — Picard–Lindelöf).** Let $f(x, y)$ be continuous on an open rectangle $R$ containing $(x_0, y_0)$, and suppose $\partial f/\partial y$ is also continuous on $R$. Then the initial-value problem

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y), \quad y(x_0) = y_0$$

has a *unique* solution on some open interval containing $x_0$.

This theorem guarantees that under mild smoothness conditions, specifying the state of a system at one moment completely determines its future and past — a cornerstone of deterministic modeling in physics and engineering.

### physics_kinematics (16.5s)

## Physics Kinematics

**Definition.** Kinematics is the branch of classical mechanics that describes the motion of particles and rigid bodies without reference to the forces causing that motion. The fundamental kinematic quantities are position $\vec{r}(t)$, velocity $\vec{v}(t) = \dfrac{d\vec{r}}{dt}$, and acceleration $\vec{a}(t) = \dfrac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$. For one-dimensional motion under constant acceleration $a$, the kinematic equations are:

$$v = v_0 + at, \qquad x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2, \qquad v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0).$$

These three equations are not independent — any two imply the third — and they apply exclusively when $a$ is constant over the interval of interest.

**Worked Example.** A stone is dropped from rest at the edge of a cliff $h = 80\,\text{m}$ high. Taking downward as positive and $g = 10\,\text{m/s}^2$:

1. *Time to impact*: With $x_0 = 0$, $v_0 = 0$, $a = g$,
$$h = \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{2h}{g}} = \sqrt{\frac{2(80)}{10}} = 4\,\text{s}.$$

2. *Speed at impact*:
$$v = v_0 + gt = 0 + (10)(4) = 40\,\text{m/s}.$$

Equivalently, $v^2 = 2gh = 2(10)(80) = 1600\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}^2 \Rightarrow v = 40\,\text{m/s}$, confirming consistency between equations.

**Key Theorem (Kinematic Decomposition).** For projectile motion in two dimensions with constant gravitational acceleration $\vec{g} = -g\hat{j}$, the horizontal and vertical components of motion are *kinematically independent*:

$$x(t) = x_0 + v_{0x}\,t, \qquad y(t) = y_0 + v_{0y}\,t - \tfrac{1}{2}g t^2.$$

*Proof sketch*: Because $\vec{a} = -g\hat{j}$ has no $x$-component, integrating $\ddot{x} = 0$ yields $\dot{x} = v_{0x}$ (constant), and integrating again gives the linear $x(t)$ relation. The $y$-equation follows from $\ddot{y} = -g$ by two successive integrations with boundary conditions $y(0) = y_0$, $\dot{y}(0) = v_{0y}$. $\square$
