Picture yourself drawing a very tall wall that needs no support; it stands perfectly straight from top to bottom. The key here is maintaining absolute verticality throughout the entire movement. We call this element a *stroke* (or nét), which simply means one single, unbroken line segment. When you make the $\text{丨}$, your goal is uniform pressure and consistent speed, ensuring that no part of the stroke wavers or bends outward.

The simple rule for mastering the vertical stroke is: Start at the top, move straight down, and stop decisively at the bottom with minimal deviation. Do not pause in the middle, and do not let your hand drift sideways. By focusing only on this one element—the perfect, strong $\text{丨}$—you are training your foundational muscle memory for all complex characters that follow.

### 十 (21.7s)

## 十

Imagine you are following a recipe for cookies that requires exactly 10 minutes in the oven. You pull them out at the perfect golden color—they look and smell ideal! But just because they are ready, you decide to leave them in for five more minutes, thinking "just a little bit won't hurt." When you finally take them out, they are dry, tough, and inedible. This is a common mistake: mistaking completion ($\text{十}$) for the absolute limit of effort.

Picture this moment: The number $\text{十}$ (ten) represents reaching a specific, necessary point—the perfect balance. It’s the spot where enough action has been taken to achieve the desired result, but no more is needed. Over-completion means adding extra steps or details that weren't originally required. These unnecessary additions are what make things worse, not better, because they violate the principle of sufficiency.

The simple rule here is: When you reach a clear stopping point—the $\text{十}$—you must trust your initial assessment and stop. Don't let the desire to *improve* something make it unnecessarily complicated or damaged. Perfection isn't found in adding more; it's found in knowing when to pause.

### 画 (21.5s)

## 画

Imagine you are helping a child draw a simple picture of a house. First, you guide their hand to sketch the basic rectangle—the main body of the house. This foundational step is what we mean by **画** (*huà*), which simply means 'to draw' or 'to sketch.' It is the initial act of bringing an image from your mind onto paper. When learning this concept, think of drawing as establishing a clear boundary; you are defining the core shape that everything else will build upon.

Picture this: Now, imagine taking a beautifully drawn portrait—a picture of a person—and notice how every line contributes to the overall beauty and completeness. The initial *act* of drawing must be careful and intentional. You aren't just scribbling; you are carefully outlining the features, ensuring that the structure is sound before any detail is added.

The simple rule here is: **Completion in stages.** When you draw something, focus on completing one distinct part at a time—the main form first, then the details second. The essence of *huà* is establishing defined boundaries and maintaining structural integrity throughout the creative process.

Try it yourself: Take a pencil and sketch an object near you (like a cup or key). Don't worry about making it perfect; just focus on creating the most basic, clear outline possible. Notice how defining that initial shape gives you a solid foundation to work from!
