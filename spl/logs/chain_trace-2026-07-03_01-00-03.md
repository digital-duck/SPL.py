### stem (26.2s)

## Stem

Think about the word **"plays."** Now think about **"played," "playing,"** and **"player."** Something stays the same across all of them — the core piece **"play."** That shared core has a name: the **stem**.

**Picture this.** Imagine a tree. The trunk holds everything up: branches grow out of it, but the trunk itself stays firm. A stem in language works exactly the same way. It is the stable core of a word that carries the main meaning. Small pieces can be added to the front or back to make new words or show things like time and number.

**Simple rule.** Strip away any small piece added to the front or back, and what you have left is the stem. For the word family built on **"play"**:

$$\underbrace{\text{play}}_{\text{stem}} + \underbrace{\text{-ed}}_{\text{ending}} = \text{played}$$

$$\underbrace{\text{play}}_{\text{stem}} + \underbrace{\text{-er}}_{\text{ending}} = \text{player}$$

The stem does not have to be a complete word on its own — **"happi-"** in *happiness* is a stem, even though we don't say "happi" alone — but it always carries the central idea.

**Try it yourself.** Look at these three words: *quickly, quicker, quickest.* What is the stem they all share? Write it down, then check: yes, it is **"quick"** — the meaning stays, only the ending changes.

### prefix (9.2s)

## Prefix

Have you ever noticed that the word **unhappy** contains the word **happy** inside it? The little piece stuck onto the front — **un-** — is called a **prefix**. A prefix is a small group of letters added to the *beginning* of a word to change its meaning.

**Picture this:** imagine a word like a train. The main car in the middle is the root word — the core meaning. A prefix is an extra car you hook onto the *front* of the train. It doesn't replace the engine; it steers the whole train in a new direction.

| Root word | Prefix | New word | New meaning |
|-----------|--------|----------|-------------|
| happy | un- | unhappy | not happy |
| write | re- | rewrite | write again |
| view | pre- | preview | view before |

**Simple rule:** A prefix never stands alone — it always attaches to a root. The prefix **un-** signals *not* or *opposite*, **re-** signals *again*, and **pre-** signals *before*. Learning what each prefix means unlocks the meaning of dozens of new words instantly.

**Try it yourself:** Look at the word *redo*. Can you spot the prefix? What does it tell you about the meaning? Now try *preview* — what does **pre-** add to the idea of *viewing* something? Write down two more words you know that start with **re-** or **un-** and explain what the prefix contributes.

### suffix (10.6s)

## Suffix

Have you ever noticed that *teach*, *teacher*, and *teaching* all share the same core? They all start with *teach*, but something has been added to the end to change the meaning or job of the word. That addition is called a **suffix** — a small piece that attaches to the *end* of a word to create a new word.

**Picture this:** imagine a word as a train engine. A suffix is like a new car you hook onto the back. The engine (*teach*) still pulls everything, but the extra car changes what the whole train can carry. Add *-er* and you get a person who does the action (*teacher*). Add *-ing* and you get the action in progress (*teaching*). Add *-able* to *read* and you get something that can be done (*readable*).

**Simple rule:** a suffix always comes *after* the base word (called the **root** or **stem**), and it never stands alone. We can write this as a tiny formula:

$$\text{new word} = \text{root} + \text{suffix}$$

So: $\text{teach} + \text{-er} = \text{teacher}$.

**Try it yourself:** Take the root *kind*. What word do you get when you add *-ness*? What about *-ly*? Write your answers down — you just built two brand-new words using suffixes! Notice how each suffix gave the root a different job in a sentence.

### in- (11.8s)

## In-

**Everyday example.** You arrive at a party and someone says the venue is *accessible*. Great — easy to get in. But then you hear the backup location is *inaccessible*. Suddenly a single three-letter addition flips the meaning completely upside down.

**Picture this.** Imagine a light switch. The prefix **in-** acts like a finger that flicks the switch from ON to OFF. Attach it to the front of a word, and you reverse that word's meaning. *Visible* means you can see it; *in*visible means you cannot. *Complete* means finished; *in*complete means not finished yet. The base word is still there — you can still see it inside the longer word — but *in-* signals "the opposite is true."

**Simple rule.** When *in-* appears at the start of an adjective or noun, it usually carries the meaning **"not"** or **"the absence of"**:

$$\text{in-} + \text{base word} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{"not" + base word}$$

So *in-* $+$ *formal* $=$ *informal* ("not formal"), and *in-* $+$ *justice* $=$ *injustice* ("absence of justice").

**Try it yourself.** Cover the list below, then guess the meaning of each word before peeking:

- *indecent*
- *inequality*
- *insecure*

Check: did you read each one as "not decent," "lack of equality," and "not secure"? If yes, you have already mastered the core power of *in-*.

### port (10.5s)

## Port

**Everyday example:** Think about the word *transport*. You use it when moving something — a bus *transports* passengers, a ship *transports* cargo. Now think about *portable* (easy to carry), *import* (carry goods *into* a country), and *export* (carry goods *out*). What do all these words share? The chunk **port**.

**Picture this:** Imagine a tiny building block — a LEGO piece — that means **"carry"** in Latin. Whenever you see this block snapped into a word, the word has something to do with moving or carrying things from one place to another.

**Simple rule:** **Port** is a *root* — the core meaning-piece of a word. A root is the smallest unit that carries real meaning. The root **port** always signals *carrying or movement*. Pieces added *before* it (called *prefixes*) tell you the direction:

$$\underbrace{\text{im}}_{\text{prefix: in}} + \underbrace{\text{port}}_{\text{root: carry}} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{import}$$

$$\underbrace{\text{ex}}_{\text{prefix: out}} + \underbrace{\text{port}}_{\text{root: carry}} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{export}$$

Pieces added *after* it (called *suffixes*) adjust the grammar: **port** + *-able* → *portable* (able to be carried).

**Try it yourself:** Look at the word *report*. The prefix *re-* means "back." Using the pattern above, can you guess what *report* originally meant? Write your answer and then check a dictionary — you may be surprised how close you were!

### spect (10.6s)

## Spect

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *inspect*. When a health inspector visits a restaurant, what do they do? They *look* carefully at everything — the kitchen, the food, the equipment. That act of looking is the heart of the word.

**Picture this.** Imagine a pair of eyes scanning a room. That image lives inside dozens of English words because they all share a tiny Latin root: **spect**, which comes from the Latin verb *spectare*, meaning *to look* or *to see*. English borrowed this root long ago, and it has been hiding in plain sight ever since.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see the letters **spect** (or its close cousins **spec**, **spic**, **spi**) inside an English word, there is almost always a meaning connected to *looking*, *seeing*, or *watching*. You can think of it like a little signal: *spect* = eyes on something.

| Word | Prefix/Suffix added | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| inspect | *in-* (into) | look into |
| respect | *re-* (again) | look again → regard highly |
| spectator | *-ator* (one who) | one who watches |
| spectacular | *-acular* (full of) | full of things to see |
| suspect | *sub-* (under) | look under → doubt |

**Try it yourself.** Write down three more words you know that contain *spect* or *spec*. For each one, see if you can guess how "looking" connects to its meaning. You may be surprised how often the connection clicks!

### -ion (11.3s)

## -Ion

**Everyday example.** You *collect* stamps, and the result of that collecting is your *collection*. You *act* in a play, and the whole process is called *action*. Something small changed at the end of those words — the suffix **-ion** appeared — and suddenly a doing-word (a verb) became a thing-word (a noun).

**Picture this.** Imagine a factory. Raw material goes in one end — a verb like *connect* — and a finished product rolls out the other end: *connection*. The suffix -ion is the machine that does the converting. It takes an action and packages it into a concept you can hold, count, or talk about as a thing.

**Simple rule.** Attach **-ion** (or its close cousins **-tion**, **-sion**, **-ation**) to a verb root and you get a noun that names the act, process, or result:

$$\text{verb root} + \{-ion,\,-tion,\,-sion,\,-ation\} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{noun}$$

Examples: $\text{act} \to \text{action}$, $\text{invent} \to \text{invention}$, $\text{discuss} \to \text{discussion}$, $\text{inform} \to \text{information}$.

**Try it yourself.** Start with the verb *educate*. Drop the final *-e*, add *-ion*, and see what noun you get. Now try *pollute* and *celebrate*. Notice any pattern in how the spelling shifts? Write your three new nouns and use each one in a sentence — you have just practiced morphology, the study of how words are built!

### inspect (9.1s)

## Inspect

**Everyday example:** Imagine you pick up a strange coin and turn it over slowly, looking at every edge, the writing, the tiny images stamped on it. You are not guessing — you are *inspecting* it.

**Picture this:** In English, the word *inspect* carries that same careful-looking idea inside it. It is built from two Latin pieces that were joined together long ago:

$$\text{inspect} = \underbrace{\text{in-}}_{\text{prefix: "into"}} + \underbrace{\text{spect}}_{\text{root: "look"}}$$

The **root** *spect* (sometimes spelled *spec*) means *to look*. A root is the core meaning-carrier of a word — the part you cannot remove without losing the heart of the idea. The **prefix** *in-* means *into* or *upon*. Together they say: "look into (something) closely."

**Simple rule:** Whenever you see *spect* or *spec* hiding inside an English word, there is a good chance that word involves seeing or looking. Think of *spectacle* (a sight worth seeing), *spectator* (someone who looks on), or *spectacular* (amazing to look at).

**Try it yourself:** Cover the list above and brainstorm two more everyday words that contain *spect* or *spec*. Write them down, then guess what the extra prefix or suffix adds to the core meaning "look." Check a dictionary afterward — you may be surprised how often your guess is right!

### con- (13.2s)

## Con-

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *connect*. When you connect two wires, you join them *together* so electricity can flow between them. Now look at *combine*, *collect*, and *construct*. Each one carries the same quiet idea: things coming together into one.

**Picture this.** Imagine a bundle of sticks. One stick alone snaps easily, but *con*-nect several sticks — hold them *with* each other — and suddenly you have something strong. The prefix **con-** is like that invisible tie binding the sticks. It comes from Latin and means **"with" or "together."**

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see **con-** at the start of a word, ask yourself: *who or what is being joined?* A *congress* is people coming together ($\text{con-} + \text{gress}$ "walk"). A *conclusion* is ideas walking together to one end. The spelling can shift slightly — **com-** before $b$, $m$, or $p$ (*combine*, *command*); **col-** before $l$ (*collect*); **cor-** before $r$ (*correct*) — but the meaning stays the same: *with, together.*

**Try it yourself.** Look at the three words below and write what is being "joined together" in each one:

1. *conflict*
2. *cooperate* (hint: **co-** is another form of **con-**)
3. *construct*

For each word, jot one sentence using it in a real situation from your own life. Notice how the sense of *togetherness* or *with-ness* quietly shapes the meaning every time.

### dict (12.8s)

## Dict

**Everyday example.** You look up a word in a *dictionary*, your teacher might *dictate* a sentence for you to write down, and a judge delivers a *verdict* in court. These three words feel very different — yet they all share a hidden family secret.

**Picture this.** Imagine a tiny word-brick stamped with the meaning "say" or "speak." In Latin, that brick was the root *dict-*. Word-builders in ancient Rome — and later in English — kept snapping other pieces onto this brick to make new words, the way you snap Lego pieces together to build bigger shapes.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot the letters **dict** inside an English word, ask yourself: *does this word have something to do with speaking or saying?*

$$\text{dict} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{"say / speak"}$$

So *dictate* means "to say aloud for someone to write," *predict* means "to say something before it happens" (the prefix *pre-* = before), and *verdict* means "a true saying" (*ver-* = true). The root carries its meaning into every word it touches.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the words below and guess what each one has to do with "saying":

- *contradict*
- *diction*
- *edict*

Write one sentence explaining each guess. You will find that once you know *dict*, dozens of new words suddenly make sense — and that is the magic of studying word roots.

### ject (13.3s)

## Ject

Have you ever tossed a ball to a friend? That simple, physical action — the act of **throwing** — is hiding inside dozens of English words you already use every day.

**Picture this.** Imagine you are standing in the middle of a room, holding a ball. You can throw it *forward* toward someone, throw it *back* at them, throw it *out* the window, or push it *into* a bag. Each direction gives you a different action — and a different word.

That is exactly what the Latin root **ject** (from *jacere*, meaning "to throw") does. It is a word-building block — we call it a **root** — that carries the core meaning of throwing. When English words attach smaller pieces (called **prefixes**) to the front of *ject*, they add a direction or twist to that basic throw.

**Simple rule.** Any time you see *ject* inside a word, think *throw*:

- **re**ject — throw *back* (to refuse something)
- **pro**ject — throw *forward* (to cast an image or plan ahead)
- **in**ject — throw *into* (to push liquid inside)
- **e**ject — throw *out* (to force something away)

The prefix steers the throw; *ject* supplies the action.

**Try it yourself.** Can you guess what **trajectory** means? The prefix *tra-* comes from Latin *trans*, meaning "across." So a trajectory is the path something takes as it is thrown *across* space — like the arc of that ball you tossed! What direction does the prefix **de-** add in the word *dejected*?

### pre- (11.2s)

## Pre-

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *preview*. Before you watch a movie, you see a short clip of what's coming — a *preview*. The tiny piece "pre-" just means **before**. It sneaks onto the front of a word and shifts its meaning in time.

**Picture this.** Imagine a timeline stretching left to right. You place an event somewhere on it. Now you stick "pre-" in front of the event's name, and suddenly you've moved to the *left* — to whatever happened **earlier**.

$$\underbrace{\text{pre-}}_{\text{before}} + \underbrace{\text{view}}_{\text{look}} = \underbrace{\text{preview}}_{\text{look before}}$$

**Simple rule.** *Pre-* is a **prefix** — a word-part attached to the *beginning* of a base word. The formula is:

$$\text{pre-} + \text{base word} \rightarrow \text{new word meaning "before [base word]"}$$

A few quick examples:

| Base word | With *pre-* | Meaning |
|-----------|------------|---------|
| school | preschool | before regular school |
| heat | preheat | heat before using |
| pay | prepay | pay before the service |

Notice that the base word keeps its original spelling; *pre-* simply clips onto the front.

**Try it yourself.** Can you build a word meaning "to arrange something *before* an event"? Start with the base word *arrange* and attach *pre-* to the front. Write your answer, then check: did you get *prearrange*?

### re- (9.3s)

## Re-

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *rewrite*. You wrote something once, and then you wrote it *again*. The little piece "re-" at the front is doing all the work — it signals "do this action one more time."

**Picture this.** Imagine a magic stamp you can press onto any action word. The stamp reads **RE-**. Press it onto *read* and you get *reread* (read again). Press it onto *build* and you get *rebuild* (build again). Press it onto *start* and you get *restart* (start again). The stamp never changes — only the word underneath it does.

**Simple rule.** In English morphology — the study of how words are built — **re-** is called a *prefix*. A prefix is a small unit of meaning attached to the *front* of a base word. The prefix **re-** carries one core meaning:

$$\text{re-} + \text{verb} \;\Longrightarrow\; \text{"do the verb again"}$$

Because **re-** attaches to the front without changing its own form, linguists call it *invariant*: it looks the same no matter which verb follows it.

**Try it yourself.** Take the verbs below and add **re-** to each one. Then write a sentence using your new word.

1. *load*
2. *open*
3. *name*

Notice how each new word means "do that action once more." That single prefix tripled your vocabulary in under a minute — and that is the quiet power of morphology.

### struct (13.0s)

## Struct

Have you ever watched a building go up, brick by brick? Someone had to **build** it — choose the materials, stack them in the right order, and hold them together. The Latin word for "to build" or "to pile up" is *struere*, and it gives us the morpheme **-struct-** (sometimes spelled **-stru-**).

**Picture this.** The morpheme *struct* is like a Lego brick that sits in the middle of a word. It carries the core idea of *building* wherever it goes. You can attach other word-parts around it:

- **con-struct** — *con-* means "together," so you build things together → to *construct*
- **in-struct** — *in-* means "into," so you build knowledge into someone → to *instruct*
- **de-struct** — *de-* means "down or apart," so you un-build something → to *destruct*
- **struct-ure** — *-ure* turns the root into a noun → a *structure*, the thing that was built

Notice that *struct* never stands alone in everyday English — it always needs a partner. Linguists call this a **bound morpheme**: a meaning-unit that must be attached to something else to form a real word.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see *struct* (or *stru*) inside a word, ask yourself: *what is being built, or un-built, or built into?* The prefix and suffix around it will give you the full picture.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the word *infrastructure*. Can you spot the prefix *infra-* (meaning "below") and the root *struct*? What do you think the word literally means?

### trans- (10.3s)

## Trans-

**Everyday example.** Think about a *transatlantic* flight — a plane that travels *across* the Atlantic Ocean from one continent to another. Or consider *translation*, which carries a message *across* the barrier between two languages. What do these words share? They both start with the same little piece: **trans-**.

**Picture this.** Imagine drawing a line on a piece of paper. Now imagine stepping *over* that line, moving from one side to the other. The prefix **trans-** (from Latin, meaning "across" or "beyond") captures exactly that movement — crossing a boundary, going through something, or reaching the other side.

**Simple rule.** When you see **trans-** at the beginning of a word, it signals that something moves *across*, passes *through*, or goes *beyond* a dividing point. The base word tells you *what* is being crossed:

$$\text{trans-} + \text{port} = \textit{transport} \quad (\text{carry across})$$

$$\text{trans-} + \text{form} = \textit{transform} \quad (\text{change beyond the original form})$$

$$\text{trans-} + \text{parent} = \textit{transparent} \quad (\text{light passes through})$$

**Try it yourself.** Look at the word *transcontinental*. The root *continent* refers to a large landmass. Using the rule above, what do you think *transcontinental* means? Write your guess in a sentence, then check a dictionary. Notice how knowing **trans-** alone gave you most of the answer before you even looked it up — that is the power of learning prefixes!

### -able (10.8s)

## -Able

**Everyday example:** Think about the word *wash*. You put a shirt in the machine, and out it comes — clean. That shirt can be washed. So we call it *washable*.

**Picture this:** Imagine a little stamp you can press onto the end of a verb (an action word). The stamp reads **-able**, and it means *"can be done."* Press it onto *read* and you get *readable* — something that can be read. Press it onto *break* and you get *breakable* — something that can be broken.

**Simple rule:** Take an action word (verb), add **-able** to the end, and you create a describing word (adjective) that means *"possible to [verb]."*

$$\text{verb} + \text{-able} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{adjective meaning "can be verbed"}$$

A few quick examples:

| Verb | + -able | Meaning |
|------|---------|---------|
| eat | *edible* | can be eaten |
| love | *lovable* | can be loved |
| print | *printable* | can be printed |

*(Notice that* eat *changes spelling slightly — English has a few of those quirks!)*

**Try it yourself:** Look around the room. Pick three objects. Ask: *Can this be moved? Can it be opened? Can it be recycled?* Now build the -able word for each action. Can you say *movable*, *openable*, *recyclable*? You just used one of English's most productive suffixes — a building block that creates hundreds of new words from ones you already know.

### construct (17.4s)

## Construct

**Everyday example:** Think about the word *unhappiness*. You already know *happy*. But at some point, someone *built* a longer word by snapping pieces onto it — slipping *un-* in front and *-ness* at the back. That finished, fully assembled word is called a **construct**: a word (or word-form) that has been deliberately put together from smaller building blocks called **morphemes** (the tiniest meaningful pieces of a word).

**Picture this:** Imagine a LEGO model. Each individual brick is one morpheme. When you click those bricks together into a finished model, the completed object is the construct. So *unhappiness* is the LEGO model, while *un-*, *happy*, and *-ness* are the three bricks. We can write that assembly like this:

$$\text{construct} = \underbrace{un\text{-}}_{\text{prefix}} + \underbrace{happy}_{\text{root}} + \underbrace{\text{-}ness}_{\text{suffix}}$$

**Simple rule:** A construct is any word produced by combining at least two morphemes. The simplest constructs join just a root plus one affix, like $re\text{-} + write = rewrite$. More complex constructs stack several morphemes in a fixed order.

**Try it yourself:** Look at the word *disconnected*. Can you identify the three morphemes that form this construct? Write them out as:

$$\text{prefix} + \text{root} + \text{suffix}$$

Hint: the root is a common action word you use every day.

### predict (14.1s)

## Predict

Have you ever said, "I *predict* it will rain today"? You were making a guess about what comes *before* it actually happens. That everyday word holds a secret about how English builds meaning — brick by brick.

**Picture this.** The word *predict* is not one solid chunk. It is built from two smaller pieces called **morphemes** — the smallest units of meaning in a word. Slice it open and you find:

$$\underbrace{\text{pre}}_{\text{prefix: "before"}} + \underbrace{\text{dict}}_{\text{root: "say / speak"}}$$

The **prefix** *pre-* means *before in time*. The **root** *dict* comes from Latin *dicere*, meaning *to say* or *to speak*. Put them together: *predict* literally means "to say before" — to speak about something before it occurs.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot the root *dict*, think "something about speaking or declaring." And whenever you see *pre-*, think "before." These building blocks travel across many words and carry their meanings with them every time.

**Try it yourself.** Can you find the same root in these words?

- *dictate* — to speak words aloud for someone to write down
- *dictionary* — a book that "declares" what words mean
- *contradict* — to speak *against* (*contra-*) what someone else said

Now you have a detective's tool. Instead of memorizing hundreds of words one by one, spotting morphemes lets you *predict* — there's that word again! — the meaning of words you have never seen before.

### reject (9.7s)

## Reject

**Everyday example.** Imagine you apply for a club, but the club says "no" — you are turned away. That turning-away is called a *rejection*. In English, the word that means "to turn away or refuse" is **reject**.

**Picture this.** The word *reject* is built from two smaller pieces called *morphemes* (the tiniest meaningful units in a word). Think of it like a sandwich:

$$\text{reject} = \underbrace{\text{re-}}_{\text{prefix}} + \underbrace{\text{ject}}_{\text{root}}$$

The *root* **ject** comes from Latin *jacere*, meaning "to throw." The *prefix* **re-** means "back" or "again." So *reject* literally means **"to throw back"** — just like tossing a ball back to someone who offered it to you.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see **re-** at the front of a word, ask yourself: does it mean *back* or *again*? Whenever you see **-ject** inside a word, think *throw*. Together they build the meaning.

| Piece | Meaning |
|-------|---------|
| re-   | back / again |
| ject  | throw |
| reject | throw back → refuse |

**Try it yourself.** Can you find the same root in these words: *eject*, *inject*, *project*? Write down what each prefix adds to the meaning of "throw." Notice how one small root, **ject**, carries its meaning across many different words.

### transport (13.6s)

## Transport

Think about the last time you took a bus across town. The bus *carried you from one place to another*. That idea — carrying something across a distance — is hiding right inside the word **transport** itself.

**Picture this.** The word *transport* is built from two smaller pieces called **morphemes** (the tiniest units of meaning in a word). The first piece is **trans-**, a prefix (a word-part that attaches to the front) borrowed from Latin meaning *across* or *beyond*. The second piece is **port**, a root (the core meaning-carrier) also from Latin, meaning *carry*. Put them together: *trans* + *port* = "carry across." Suddenly the spelling is not random at all — it is a little picture of meaning.

**Simple rule.** When you spot the root **port** in an English word, expect the idea of *carrying or moving something*. When you spot the prefix **trans-**, expect the idea of *crossing over*. Together or apart, these pieces keep their jobs.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the words below. Can you find **port** or **trans-** hiding inside each one, and guess the meaning?

- *portable* — something you can \_\_\_ with you
- *export* — to carry \_\_\_ of a country
- *transfer* — to move \_\_\_ from one place to another
- *import* — to carry goods \_\_\_ a country

Each blank is filled by the morpheme's core meaning. You are already reading the *architecture* of words!

### -or (12.9s)

## -Or

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *act*. It names something you do — you perform on stage, you move, you do something. Now add two letters to the end: *actor*. Suddenly the word points to a *person* — someone who acts. That tiny ending, **-or**, did all the work.

**Picture this.** Imagine a little badge that says "person who ___." Whenever you pin that badge onto a verb (an action word), you get a noun (a thing word) naming the person who performs that action. The badge is **-or**.

| Base verb | + -or | Meaning |
|-----------|-------|---------|
| edit | editor | one who edits |
| direct | director | one who directs |
| operate | operator | one who operates |
| invent | inventor | one who invents |

**Simple rule.** When you see a word ending in **-or**, ask: *"What action does the base describe?"* The **-or** word names whoever carries out that action. In notation, we can write:

$$\text{verb} + \textit{-or} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{noun (agent)}$$

An *agent* just means "the one doing the thing."

**Try it yourself.** Cover the right-hand column and guess each meaning:

1. *conduct* → conduct**or** = ?
2. *supervise* → supervis**or** = ?
3. *govern* → govern**or** = ?

Check: one who conducts, one who supervises, one who governs. You already know the pattern — **-or** turns actions into people!

### import (23.6s)

## Import

**Everyday example**: You've probably used the word *import* — "the country imports bananas from Ecuador." But why does this word mean "to bring something in from outside"? The answer is hiding right inside the word itself.

**Picture this**: *Import* is built from two pieces. The first is the **prefix** *im-* (a close cousin of *in-*, meaning "into"). The second is the **root** *port*, from Latin *portare*, meaning "to carry." Place them side by side:

$$\text{im-} + \text{port} = \text{"carry into"}$$

That is exactly what importing means — carrying goods into a country from somewhere else.

**Simple rule**: A prefix is a small word-part attached to the *front* of a root to nudge its meaning in a new direction. The prefix *im-* signals "into" or "inward." The root *port* signals "carry." Whenever you spot *port* buried in a word, think movement: *transport* (carry across), *export* (carry out), *portable* (able to be carried).

**Try it yourself**: Decode *export* the same way. The prefix *ex-* means "out of," and *port* still means "carry," so:

$$\text{ex-} + \text{port} = \text{"carry out"}$$

Now try *transport*: *trans-* means "across," giving $\text{trans-} + \text{port} = \text{"carry across."}$ Congratulations — you just used morphological analysis, the same tool linguists reach for every day!

### construction (10.9s)

## Construction

**Everyday example.** You already know that "un-" means *not* and "happy" means *joyful*. Put them together and you get *unhappy* — a new word with a new meaning. That combining act, plus the meaning it produces, is what linguists call a **construction**.

**Picture this.** Imagine a recipe card. A recipe lists ingredients *and* tells you what the finished dish tastes like. A construction works the same way: it is a pairing of a **form** (the ingredients — sounds, morphemes, words) and a **meaning** (what that combination conveys). Neither half exists without the other on the card.

Formally, we can write this pairing as:

$$\text{construction} = \langle \text{form},\ \text{meaning} \rangle$$

So *un- + happy* is a construction whose form is a prefix attached to an adjective, and whose meaning is *the opposite of that adjective*.

**Simple rule.** Whenever a combination of linguistic pieces — morphemes, words, or even sentence patterns — carries a meaning that is stored as a unit in your mind, that combination is a construction.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the word *replayable*. Can you spot three smaller constructions nested inside it? Write down each piece (*re-*, *play*, *-able*), state its meaning, and then describe the larger construction that combines all three into one word. Notice how each layer adds a new meaning on top of the last.

### inspection (10.4s)

## Inspection

**Everyday example.** You drop a package at the post office and the clerk looks it over — checking the address, the weight, the tape — before accepting it. That careful looking-over is an *inspection*.

**Picture this.** The word *inspect* comes from the Latin root **spect**, meaning "to look." You can see this same root hiding inside *spectator* (someone who looks at a show) and *spectacles* (glasses that help you look). When we add **in-** at the front — called a *prefix* — we get "look into." Then we attach **-ion** at the end — a *suffix* — to turn the action into a noun, a thing: the act of looking into.

**Simple rule.** English builds many nouns this way:

$$\underbrace{\text{in-}}_{\text{prefix}} + \underbrace{\text{spect}}_{\text{root}} + \underbrace{\text{-ion}}_{\text{suffix}} = \textit{inspection}$$

The suffix **-ion** (sometimes **-tion** or **-ation**) almost always signals: *"this is the name of an action or process."*

**Try it yourself.** Look at these words and see if you can spot the same root **spect**:

- *respect* — to look again at someone with regard
- *expect* — to look outward, waiting for something
- *perspective* — a way of looking through something

Can you think of one more word with **spect** hiding inside it? Write it down and try to guess what its prefix adds to the meaning of "look."

### inspector (10.7s)

## Inspector

Have you ever seen an official walk through a restaurant kitchen, checking every corner for cleanliness? That person is called an **inspector** — someone whose job is to look carefully at things. But have you wondered why this particular word means that? The answer is hiding right inside the word itself.

**Picture this:** The word *inspector* is built from three smaller pieces, called **morphemes** (the smallest meaningful building blocks of a word). Crack it open like this:

$$\text{inspector} = \underbrace{\textit{in-}}_{\text{prefix}} + \underbrace{\textit{spect}}_{\text{root}} + \underbrace{\textit{-or}}_{\text{suffix}}$$

The **root** *spect* comes from Latin and means *to look*. The **prefix** *in-* means *into* or *upon*. The **suffix** *-or* means *a person who does something*. Put it all together: an inspector is literally *a person who looks into* something.

**Simple rule:** When you see *-or* at the end of a word, it often signals "a person or thing that does the action." And whenever you spot *spect* inside a word, think "seeing" or "looking."

**Try it yourself:** Can you spot the *spect* root in these words — *spectator*, *inspect*, *spectacle*? What do you think each one might have to do with the idea of *looking*? Write down your guesses before checking a dictionary. You may be surprised how much you already know!

### portable (8.8s)

## Portable

**Everyday example.** Think about a suitcase. You can carry it from your bedroom to the car, from the car to the airport, and from the airport to a hotel across the world. The suitcase *travels with you*. That idea of "able to be carried" is exactly what the word **portable** means.

**Picture this.** The word *portable* is built from two smaller pieces:

- **port** — a Latin root meaning *to carry*
- **-able** — a suffix meaning *capable of being*

Put them together: *carry* + *capable of being* = **capable of being carried**.

You can see the same root **port** hiding in other English words: a *porter* carries your bags at a hotel; a *port* is a harbor where ships carry goods; to *transport* something means to carry it from one place to another.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you spot the root **port** in an English word, ask yourself: *is something being carried here?* The answer is almost always yes. The suffix **-able** then tells you the carrying is *possible*.

$$\underbrace{\text{port}}_{\text{carry}} + \underbrace{\text{-able}}_{\text{capable of}} = \text{portable}$$

**Try it yourself.** Look at the word *import*. The prefix *im-* means *into*. Using what you just learned about **port**, what do you think *import* means? Write your answer in one sentence, then check a dictionary to see how close you were.

### prediction (9.6s)

## Prediction

**Everyday example.** Every morning you hear a knock at the door and think, "That's probably the mail carrier." You haven't opened the door yet — you're *predicting* what you'll find based on past experience.

Language works the same way. When someone says "I am walk—", your brain immediately fills in the blank: *walking*. You predicted the ending before the speaker finished. This mental skill is called **prediction** — making an educated guess about what comes next based on patterns you already know.

**Picture this.** Imagine the word *jump* sitting alone. If someone adds the ending *-ed*, the word becomes *jumped*. If they add *-ing*, it becomes *jumping*. Your knowledge of English tells you which endings are *possible* and which ones fit the situation. Prediction is simply using those stored patterns to make the best guess.

**Simple rule.** A prediction in language is a reasoned expectation:

$$\text{known pattern} + \text{new context} \rightarrow \text{expected form}$$

You take what you already know (the pattern) and apply it to something new (the context) to arrive at the most likely outcome.

**Try it yourself.** Fill in the blank: "She walk\_\_\_ to school every day." Did you write *walks*? You just made a grammatical prediction! Notice how the word *she* and the phrase *every day* gave you clues. Write three more sentences with blanks and see if a friend predicts the same endings you had in mind.

### rejection (10.1s)

## Rejection

**Everyday example:** Imagine you hand your friend a sandwich and say, "Do you want this?" Your friend shakes their head and says, "No, I *reject* it." The word *reject* carries a clear action — pushing something away. But notice what happens when we add a small ending to that word: *reject* becomes **rejection**. Suddenly, instead of an action, we have a *thing* — the act of being turned down.

**Picture this:** Think of a word as a small building. The base of the building is called the **root** — the core meaning. In *rejection*, the root is *reject* (meaning "to push back"). The ending **-ion** is a **suffix** — a little piece added to the back of the root. The suffix **-ion** works like a magic switch: it turns *verbs* (action words) into *nouns* (name words).

$$\text{verb} + \text{-ion} \rightarrow \text{noun}$$

So: $\text{reject} + \text{-ion} = \text{rejection}$

Notice the spelling: the final letter of *reject* stays, but *-ion* smoothly attaches to give us **rejection**, not *rejecton*.

**Simple rule:** When you see **-ion** at the end of an English word, it almost always signals a noun built from a verb.

**Try it yourself:** Can you spot the same pattern in these words?
- *collect* → **collection**
- *protect* → **protection**
- *connect* → **connection**

What is the root verb hiding inside each noun?

### transportable (9.8s)

## Transportable

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *transport* — it means to carry something from one place to another. Now add the ending *-able*, and you get *transportable*: "able to be transported." That tiny addition completely changes how the word works in a sentence.

**Picture this.** Imagine a label maker. You have a root word — a base meaning — sitting on a shelf. Morphemes (the smallest meaningful pieces of language) are like label strips you can attach. The suffix *-able* is one such strip. Snap it onto a verb, and you convert it into an adjective describing possibility or capacity.

$$\text{transport} + \text{-able} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{transportable}$$

The root *trans-* means "across," and *port* comes from Latin *portare*, "to carry." So the word carries its history right inside it.

**Simple rule.** Adding *-able* (or its variant *-ible*) to a verb base produces an adjective meaning *capable of being \[verb\]-ed*. The base must typically describe an action that can be done *to* something.

$$\text{verb base} + \text{-able} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{adjective expressing possibility}$$

**Try it yourself.** Can you apply the same pattern? Fill in the blank:

- *wash* + *-able* = \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_
- *read* + *-able* = \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_
- *break* + *-able* = \_\_\_\_\_\_\_\_

Notice how each result describes whether the action *can* be performed on the object. That is the power of a single morpheme.

### allomorphy (11.5s)

## Allomorphy

**Everyday example.** Think about how you say "a" versus "an" in English. You say *a cat* but *an apple*. The meaning is identical — both are the indefinite article — yet the spoken form changes depending on what comes next. That switching behaviour has a name: **allomorphy**.

**Picture this.** Imagine a word is like a costume-wearing actor. The actor (the meaning) stays the same, but the costume (the sound or spelling) changes to fit the scene. Each different costume is called an **allomorph** — a variant form of the same underlying unit of meaning, which linguists call a **morpheme**.

The plural morpheme in English is a perfect example. We write it as *-s*, but listen carefully:

- *cats* → the suffix sounds like $[\text{s}]$
- *dogs* → the suffix sounds like $[\text{z}]$
- *buses* → the suffix sounds like $[\text{ɪz}]$

Three different pronunciations, one single morpheme $\{-\text{PL}\}$. The surrounding sounds — technically called the **phonological environment** — trigger which allomorph appears.

**Simple rule.** Allomorphy = one morpheme, multiple surface forms, each chosen by its environment.

$$\text{morpheme} \rightarrow \text{allomorph}_1 \mid \text{allomorph}_2 \mid \cdots$$

**Try it yourself.** Say these words aloud and circle the sound the past-tense suffix *-ed* makes: *walked*, *jogged*, *wanted*. You should hear $[\text{t}]$, $[\text{d}]$, and $[\text{ɪd}]$. Congratulations — you just found three allomorphs of the English past-tense morpheme!

### prefix_root_composition (10.7s)

## Prefix Root Composition

**Everyday example.** Think about the word *unhappy*. You already know what *happy* means — feeling good. But someone added *un-* to the front, and suddenly the meaning flipped. That tiny addition, *un-*, is called a **prefix** (a word-piece that attaches to the *front* of another word). The word it attaches to, *happy*, is called the **root** (the core word that carries the main meaning). Sticking a prefix onto a root is called **prefix-root composition**.

**Picture this.** Imagine a root word as a plain white t-shirt. A prefix is a colorful patch you iron onto the chest. The shirt is still a shirt, but the patch changes how people read it. *happy* → patch on front → *unhappy*. The root stays intact; the prefix modifies it.

**Simple rule.** In prefix-root composition, the new word's meaning is roughly:

$$\text{new word} = \text{prefix meaning} + \text{root meaning}$$

So *re-* ("again") $+$ *write* $=$ *rewrite* ("write again"). The prefix shifts or sharpens what the root already says.

**Try it yourself.** Take the root *connect*. What do you get when you add *dis-* (meaning "apart" or "not")? Write the new word and guess its meaning before checking a dictionary. Then try *re-* and *inter-* with the same root. Notice how each prefix steers the meaning in a completely different direction — the root stays the same, but the prefix is the pilot.

### suffix_derivation (10.3s)

## Suffix Derivation

**Everyday example.** You already know the word *kind*. But what about *kindness*, *kindly*, and *unkindly*? You didn't learn four separate words — you learned one root and a few small attachments. Those attachments stuck onto the end are called **suffixes** (from Latin *suffixus*, "fastened below"). Adding a suffix to change a word's meaning or grammatical role is called **suffix derivation**.

**Picture this.** Think of a word as a train. The engine at the front is the **root** — the core meaning. A suffix is a railcar you snap onto the back. Snap *-ness* onto *kind* and you get a noun: *kindness* (the state of being kind). Snap *-ly* instead and you get an adverb: *kindly* (in a kind manner). Same engine, different cars, different destinations.

**Simple rule.** A suffix derivation follows this pattern:

$$\text{root} + \text{suffix} \;\longrightarrow\; \text{new word (often a new word class)}$$

For example:

$$\textit{teach} + \textit{-er} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{teacher} \quad \text{(verb} \to \text{noun)}$$

$$\textit{happy} + \textit{-ness} \;\longrightarrow\; \textit{happiness} \quad \text{(adjective} \to \text{noun)}$$

Notice that the suffix often *changes the grammatical category* of the word, not just its meaning.

**Try it yourself.** Take the root *dark*. What word do you get by adding *-en*? What about *-ness*? Write both words down and label whether the result is a verb, noun, or adjective.

### morphological_decoding_principle (10.2s)

## Morphological Decoding Principle

**Everyday example.** Imagine you're reading and you stumble on the word *unbreakable*. You've never seen it before — but you probably understand it instantly. How? Because you recognize its parts: *un-* (not), *break* (split apart), *-able* (can be done). Your brain decoded a new word by reading its pieces.

**Picture this.** Think of words like LEGO bricks. Each brick is a **morpheme** — the smallest meaningful chunk of a word. Some bricks start words (*un-*, *re-*, *pre-*), some end them (*-ness*, *-tion*, *-able*), and some stand alone (*break*, *happy*, *run*). The **Morphological Decoding Principle** says: *to understand an unfamiliar word, identify its morphemes and combine their meanings.*

**Simple rule.** If a word $W$ is built from morphemes $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_n$, its meaning is approximately:

$$\text{meaning}(W) \approx \text{meaning}(m_1) \oplus \text{meaning}(m_2) \oplus \cdots \oplus \text{meaning}(m_n)$$

where $\oplus$ means "combine." The order and type of morphemes matter — a *prefix* like *un-* flips meaning, while a *suffix* like *-ness* changes the word's grammatical role (happy → happiness).

**Try it yourself.** Break these words into morphemes and guess the meaning:
- *unhappiness*
- *rewritable*
- *prehistoric*

For each one, ask: What does each piece mean? How do they combine? Check a dictionary afterward — you'll likely find you were right!
