## Exact Rational

**Definition 1 (Exact Rational Arithmetic).** Let $\mathbb{Z}$ denote the ring of integers. A *rational number* is an equivalence class $[p, q] \in \mathbb{Z} \times (\mathbb{Z} \setminus \{0\})$ under the relation $(p, q) \sim (p', q')$ iff $pq' = p'q$. The field of rationals $\mathbb{Q}$ carries the four ring operations with exact closure. A computational system is said to implement *exact rational arithmetic* if every intermediate result is represented as a canonical pair $(\hat{p}, \hat{q})$ where $\hat{p} = p/\gcd(p,q)$, $\hat{q} = q/\gcd(p,q) > 0$, and no rounding or truncation occurs at any stage.

**Theorem 1 (Canonicity and Decidability).** Every nonzero element of $\mathbb{Q}$ has a unique representation as a fraction $p/q$ with $p \in \mathbb{Z}$, $q \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$, and $\gcd(|p|, q) = 1$. Consequently, equality in $\mathbb{Q}$ is decidable in time $O(\log \min(|p|,q))$ via the Euclidean algorithm.

**Proof.** Existence: Given any $(a, b)$ with $b \neq 0$, set $d = \gcd(|a|, |b|)$, $\hat{p} = a \cdot \mathrm{sgn}(b) / d$, $\hat{q} = |b|/d$. Then $\gcd(|\hat{p}|, \hat{q}) = 1$ and $\hat{q} > 0$. Uniqueness: Suppose $p/q = p'/q'$ with both in canonical form. Then $pq' = p'q$. Since $\gcd(p,q)=\gcd(p',q')=1$, Euclid's lemma forces $q \mid q'$ and $q' \mid q$, hence $q = q'$ and $p = p'$. Decidability follows because $\gcd$ terminates in $O(\log \min)$ divisions. $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** Exact rational arithmetic is foundational in computer algebra systems (Mathematica, SageMath, GMP) and formal verification (Lean's `Mathlib.Data.Rat`). The principal limitation is *coefficient swell*: iterative algorithms such as Gaussian elimination over $\mathbb{Q}$ can produce numerators and denominators of bit-length $O(n^2 \log M)$ for an $n \times n$ matrix with entries bounded by $M$ (Hadamard bound). This motivates modular methods—computing over $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ and recovering $\mathbb{Q}$ results via the rational reconstruction theorem (Wang, 1981), which lifts a residue $r$ to $p/q$ whenever $|p|, q \leq \sqrt{N/2}$ and $N$ is the modulus. Exact rationals embed faithfully into the $p$-adic fields $\mathbb{Q}_p$, connecting computational exactness to analytic number theory.

## Parent Structure

**Definition 1 (Parent Structure).** Let $\mathcal{L} = \langle \mathcal{F}, \mathcal{R} \rangle$ be a first-order language with function symbols $\mathcal{F}$ and relation symbols $\mathcal{R}$. An $\mathcal{L}$-structure $\mathbf{A} = \langle A, \{f^{\mathbf{A}}\}_{f \in \mathcal{F}}, \{R^{\mathbf{A}}\}_{R \in \mathcal{R}} \rangle$ is called a *parent structure* of $\mathbf{B}$ if $\mathbf{B}$ is a substructure of $\mathbf{A}$; that is, $B \subseteq A$, every $f^{\mathbf{B}}$ is the restriction $f^{\mathbf{A}}|_{B^{\text{ar}(f)}}$, and $R^{\mathbf{B}} = R^{\mathbf{A}} \cap B^{\text{ar}(R)}$ for all $f \in \mathcal{F}$, $R \in \mathcal{R}$.

**Theorem 1 (Substructure Lattice).** Let $\mathbf{A}$ be an $\mathcal{L}$-structure. The collection $\mathrm{Sub}(\mathbf{A})$ of all substructures of $\mathbf{A}$, ordered by inclusion, forms a complete lattice whose greatest element is $\mathbf{A}$ itself.

*Proof.* Let $\{B_i\}_{i \in I} \subseteq \mathrm{Sub}(\mathbf{A})$. The intersection $B = \bigcap_{i \in I} B_i$ is closed under every $f \in \mathcal{F}$: for any $\bar{a} \in B^{\text{ar}(f)}$ and each $i$, we have $\bar{a} \in B_i^{\text{ar}(f)}$, so $f^{\mathbf{A}}(\bar{a}) \in B_i$; hence $f^{\mathbf{A}}(\bar{a}) \in B$. Thus $B \in \mathrm{Sub}(\mathbf{A})$, making $B$ the infimum. The supremum of $\{B_i\}$ is the substructure *generated* by $\bigcup_i B_i$, obtained by iterating all $f^{\mathbf{A}}$ until closure. Since $A$ itself is an upper bound, the process terminates within $A$. Hence $\mathrm{Sub}(\mathbf{A})$ is a complete lattice. $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** This result is classical in universal algebra; see Grätzer (*Universal Algebra*, 2008, Theorem 5 in §5). The parent structure mediates the fundamental isomorphism theorems: if $\varphi: \mathbf{A} \to \mathbf{C}$ is a homomorphism, then $\mathrm{Im}(\varphi) \in \mathrm{Sub}(\mathbf{C})$, with $\mathbf{A}$ as its parent, and $\mathbf{A}/\ker\varphi \cong \mathrm{Im}(\varphi)$. In category-theoretic terms, the parent structure corresponds to a *representing object* in the slice category $\mathcal{C}/\mathbf{A}$, generalising the construction to any category with a suitable notion of monomorphism.

## Algebraic Numbers

**Definition.** A complex number $\alpha \in \mathbb{C}$ is called *algebraic* (over $\mathbb{Q}$) if there exists a nonzero polynomial $f \in \mathbb{Q}[x]$ such that $f(\alpha) = 0$. The set of all algebraic numbers is denoted $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. Among all such polynomials, the unique monic polynomial of minimal degree with $f(\alpha) = 0$ and coefficients in $\mathbb{Q}$ is called the *minimal polynomial* of $\alpha$, written $\mathrm{minpoly}(\alpha, \mathbb{Q})$; it is necessarily irreducible over $\mathbb{Q}$.

**Theorem.** $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$ is an algebraically closed field, and $[\overline{\mathbb{Q}} : \mathbb{Q}] = \infty$.

**Proof.** We verify the field axioms. If $\alpha, \beta \in \overline{\mathbb{Q}}$ with minimal polynomials $f$ and $g$ of degrees $m$ and $n$ respectively, then $\alpha + \beta$ and $\alpha\beta$ lie in the field extension $\mathbb{Q}(\alpha, \beta)$, which satisfies $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha, \beta) : \mathbb{Q}] \leq mn < \infty$. Any element of a finite extension of $\mathbb{Q}$ is algebraic, so $\alpha + \beta, \alpha\beta \in \overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. Closure under inverses follows similarly. Algebraic closure holds because if $p(x) \in \overline{\mathbb{Q}}[x]$, its coefficients generate a finite extension $K/\mathbb{Q}$, and any root $\gamma$ of $p$ satisfies $[K(\gamma):K] \leq \deg p$, giving $[\mathbb{Q}(\gamma):\mathbb{Q}] \leq [K(\gamma):\mathbb{Q}] < \infty$, hence $\gamma \in \overline{\mathbb{Q}}$. Infinitude of degree follows from the existence of $2^{1/n}$ for all $n \geq 1$. $\blacksquare$

**Remark.** The degree $[\mathbb{Q}(\alpha):\mathbb{Q}] = \deg \mathrm{minpoly}(\alpha, \mathbb{Q})$ is the *algebraic degree* of $\alpha$; degree $1$ characterises $\mathbb{Q}$ itself. This framework generalises: for any field $F$, one defines algebraic elements over $F$ and constructs the algebraic closure $\bar{F}$, unique up to $F$-isomorphism (cf. Lang, *Algebra*, Ch. V). Liouville's 1844 theorem and Cantor's cardinality argument establish that transcendental numbers—those outside $\overline{\mathbb{Q}}$—constitute a set of full measure in $\mathbb{R}$.
