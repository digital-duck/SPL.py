### scalar_multiplication (0.1s)

## Scalar Multiplication

**Everyday example.** Imagine you have a recipe that calls for 2 cups of flour and 3 cups of sugar. If you want to triple the recipe, you multiply every ingredient by 3: now you need 6 cups of flour and 9 cups of sugar. You scaled the entire recipe by one single number.

**Picture this.** A vector is just a list of numbers, like an arrow pointing somewhere. Suppose you have the vector $\mathbf{v} = (2, 3)$. A **scalar** is a fancy word for a plain, ordinary number — say $k = 3$. **Scalar multiplication** means multiplying every entry in the vector by that scalar:

$$k \, \mathbf{v} = 3 \cdot (2,\, 3) = (3 \times 2,\; 3 \times 3) = (6,\, 9)$$

The arrow now points in the same direction but is three times longer. If $k$ is negative — for example $k = -1$ — the arrow flips to point the opposite way: $-1 \cdot (2, 3) = (-2, -3)$.

**Simple rule.** To multiply a scalar $k$ by a vector $(v_1, v_2, \dots, v_n)$, multiply each component:

$$k \, \mathbf{v} = (k \, v_1,\; k \, v_2,\; \dots,\; k \, v_n)$$

The direction stays the same (or reverses if $k < 0$), and the length scales by $|k|$.

**Try it yourself.** Let $\mathbf{u} = (4, -1, 5)$. Compute $2\,\mathbf{u}$ and $-0.5\,\mathbf{u}$. Which result points in the opposite direction from $\mathbf{u}$?

### vector_addition (0.2s)

## Vector Addition

**Everyday example.** Imagine you walk 3 blocks east and then 4 blocks north. Your total trip isn't 3 or 4 — it's a combination of both movements. That combined movement is exactly what vector addition gives you: a single arrow that captures the net effect of two separate pushes.

**Picture this.** A *vector* is simply a quantity that has both a size (how much) and a direction (which way). We write a vector in component form like $\vec{u} = (u_1, u_2)$, where $u_1$ is the horizontal part and $u_2$ is the vertical part. To add two vectors, you place them tip-to-tail — finish the first arrow, then start the second arrow right where the first one ended. The arrow from your starting point to your final destination is the *resultant* vector.

**Simple rule.** Given $\vec{u} = (u_1, u_2)$ and $\vec{v} = (v_1, v_2)$, their sum is:

$$\vec{u} + \vec{v} = (u_1 + v_1,\; u_2 + v_2)$$

You simply add the matching components. For our walk: $(3, 0) + (0, 4) = (3, 4)$.

**Try it yourself.** A canoe drifts with the current at $\vec{c} = (2, 1)$ while you paddle at $\vec{p} = (1, 3)$. What is your actual velocity $\vec{c} + \vec{p}$? Sketch both arrows tip-to-tail and confirm your answer matches the component-wise sum.

### linear_combination (0.2s)

## Linear Combination

**Everyday example.** Imagine you're at a smoothie bar. You ask for 2 scoops of strawberry and 3 scoops of banana. The final drink is a *blend* — a mix of ingredients, each scaled by the amount you chose. A **linear combination** works the same way: you pick some objects, decide how much of each to use, and add them all together.

**Picture this.** Suppose you have two vectors, $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. A linear combination of them looks like:

$$a\,\mathbf{u} + b\,\mathbf{v}$$

where $a$ and $b$ are ordinary numbers (called **scalars** — they "scale" each vector up or down). If $\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\0\end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}0\\1\end{bmatrix}$, then $3\mathbf{u} + 2\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\2\end{bmatrix}$. You've built a brand-new vector just by mixing the two you started with.

**Simple rule.** A linear combination is any expression of the form $c_1\mathbf{v}_1 + c_2\mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_n\mathbf{v}_n$, where every $c_i$ is a scalar and every $\mathbf{v}_i$ is a vector. There is no squaring, no multiplying vectors together — only scaling and adding.

**Try it yourself.** Let $\mathbf{a} = \begin{bmatrix}1\\2\end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix}3\\-1\end{bmatrix}$. Compute $4\mathbf{a} - 2\mathbf{b}$. Can you find scalars $c_1, c_2$ so that $c_1\mathbf{a} + c_2\mathbf{b} = \begin{bmatrix}5\\3\end{bmatrix}$?

### linear_map (15.4s)

## Linear Map

**Definition.** Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over a field $\mathbb{F}$. A function $T: V \to W$ is called a **linear map** (or linear transformation) if it satisfies two axioms for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in V$ and all $c \in \mathbb{F}$:

$$T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}) \quad \text{(additivity)}$$

$$T(c\,\mathbf{v}) = c\,T(\mathbf{v}) \quad \text{(homogeneity)}$$

These two conditions combine into the single criterion: $T(c\,\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = c\,T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v})$. An immediate consequence is $T(\mathbf{0}_V) = \mathbf{0}_W$, which provides a quick check to rule out non-linear candidates.

**Worked Example.** Define $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ by

$$T\!\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} x + y \\ 2x \\ y - x \end{pmatrix}.$$

Verify linearity. Take $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, u_2)^\top$, $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2)^\top$, and $c \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$T(c\,\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T\!\begin{pmatrix} cu_1 + v_1 \\ cu_2 + v_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} (cu_1+v_1)+(cu_2+v_2) \\ 2(cu_1+v_1) \\ (cu_2+v_2)-(cu_1+v_1) \end{pmatrix} = c\,T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}).$$

The standard matrix representation of $T$ is obtained by computing $T(\mathbf{e}_1)$ and $T(\mathbf{e}_2)$:

$$[T] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 \\ -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

**Key Theorem (Rank–Nullity).** If $T: V \to W$ is a linear map and $V$ is finite-dimensional, then

$$\dim(\ker T) + \dim(\operatorname{im} T) = \dim V.$$

The quantities $\dim(\ker T)$ and $\dim(\operatorname{im} T)$ are called the **nullity** and **rank** of $T$, respectively. A proof sketch: choose a basis $\{\mathbf{n}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{n}_k\}$ for $\ker T$, extend it to a basis of $V$, and show the images of the extension vectors form a basis for $\operatorname{im} T$.

**Lab Exercise.** Let $T: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be defined by $T(x,y,z)^\top = (x - z,\; 2y + z)^\top$.

1. Write the matrix $[T]$ with respect to the standard bases.
2. Compute $\ker T$ explicitly by solving $[T]\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
3. Verify the Rank–Nullity theorem by computing $\dim(\ker T)$ and $\dim(\operatorname{im} T)$.

### eigenpair (14.0s)

## Eigenpair

**Definition.** Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ be a square matrix. A scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ is called an *eigenvalue* of $A$ if there exists a nonzero vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{C}^n$ such that

$$A\mathbf{v} = \lambda \mathbf{v}.$$

The vector $\mathbf{v}$ is called an *eigenvector* corresponding to $\lambda$. The pair $(\lambda, \mathbf{v})$ is called an **eigenpair** of $A$. The condition that $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$ is essential: the zero vector satisfies $A\mathbf{0} = \lambda\mathbf{0}$ trivially for any $\lambda$ and is therefore excluded. Geometrically, an eigenvector is a direction that $A$ stretches or reflects but does not rotate; $\lambda$ is the factor by which it is scaled.

**Worked Example.** Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

To find eigenpairs, solve $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 - \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & 2 - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (3 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda) = 0,$$

giving eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 3$ and $\lambda_2 = 2$. For $\lambda_1 = 3$, solve $(A - 3I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

For $\lambda_2 = 2$, solve $(A - 2I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}.$$

The eigenpairs are $(3,\, \mathbf{v}_1)$ and $(2,\, \mathbf{v}_2)$.

**Key Theorem (Eigendecomposition).** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n$ with corresponding eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, then $A$ is diagonalizable:

$$A = P \Lambda P^{-1},$$

where $P = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_n]$ and $\Lambda = \operatorname{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$. This decomposition reduces matrix powers and exponentials to scalar operations: $A^k = P\Lambda^k P^{-1}$.

**Lab Exercise.** Given

$$B = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix},$$

compute both eigenpairs of $B$. Verify your answer by confirming $B\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$ for each pair, then construct $P$ and $\Lambda$ and check that $B = P\Lambda P^{-1}$ holds numerically.

### normal_modes (17.4s)

## Normal Modes

**Definition.** Consider a system of $n$ coupled linear oscillators described by the equation of motion

$$M\ddot{\mathbf{q}} + K\mathbf{q} = \mathbf{0},$$

where $\mathbf{q}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the generalized coordinate vector, $M$ is the positive-definite mass matrix, and $K$ is the positive-semidefinite stiffness matrix. A *normal mode* is a solution of the form $\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{u}\cos(\omega t + \phi)$, where every component of the system oscillates at the *same* frequency $\omega$ and in fixed phase relation. The scalar $\omega$ is the *natural frequency* and $\mathbf{u}$ is the *mode shape* (or eigenvector) of that mode.

**Worked Example.** Let two identical masses $m$ be connected by three identical springs of stiffness $k$ (wall–mass–spring–mass–wall). The equations of motion yield $M = mI_2$ and

$$K = k\begin{pmatrix}2 & -1\\-1 & 2\end{pmatrix}.$$

Seeking solutions $\mathbf{q}(t) = \mathbf{u}e^{i\omega t}$ reduces to the generalized eigenvalue problem $K\mathbf{u} = \omega^2 M\mathbf{u}$, i.e., $K\mathbf{u} = \lambda m\mathbf{u}$. The characteristic equation is

$$\det(K - \lambda m I) = 0 \implies \lambda^2 - 4(k/m)\lambda + 3(k/m)^2 = 0,$$

giving $\omega_1^2 = k/m$ with $\mathbf{u}_1 = (1,1)^T$ (symmetric, in-phase) and $\omega_2^2 = 3k/m$ with $\mathbf{u}_2 = (1,-1)^T$ (antisymmetric, out-of-phase). The general solution is a linear superposition of these two modes.

**Key Theorem (Modal Decomposition).** If $M$ is positive definite and $K$ is symmetric, then the $n$ normal modes $\{\mathbf{u}_k\}$ form an $M$-orthogonal basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{u}_j^T M\, \mathbf{u}_k = \delta_{jk}, \quad \mathbf{u}_j^T K\, \mathbf{u}_k = \omega_k^2\delta_{jk}.$$

Under the change of basis $\mathbf{q}(t) = \sum_k \eta_k(t)\,\mathbf{u}_k$, the coupled system decouples into $n$ independent scalar oscillators $\ddot{\eta}_k + \omega_k^2\eta_k = 0$.

**Lab Exercise.** Construct the mass and stiffness matrices for three equal masses coupled in a line by four equal springs. Compute all three normal frequencies and mode shapes numerically (e.g., `scipy.linalg.eigh`). Plot each mode shape as a bar chart. Verify orthogonality by computing $U^T M U$, where $U$ is the matrix of eigenvectors, and confirm it is diagonal to within floating-point tolerance.
