## 万

Imagine you are counting the number of grains of rice in a very large field—the amount might be so vast that simply counting them would take years! This is where the concept of $\text{万}$ (wàn) helps us manage huge numbers. If we use $\text{万}$, we aren't just counting; we are organizing and grouping. It’s an incredibly useful unit for giving a sense of scale or **magnitude**—which simply means how big something is in number terms.

Picture this: Instead of saying "we counted ten thousand individual beans," we can say, "there are five $\text{万}$ beans." By using the $\text{万}$ unit, we skip counting all those small pieces and jump straight to a manageable grouping. It's like replacing a giant stack of books with a single, labeled box that tells you exactly how many stacks there are.

The simple rule is this: one $\text{万}$ always represents ten thousand ($10,000$). When we write numbers using $\text{万}$, we are essentially grouping them into sets of four digits. If you see the number $520\text{万}$, it means 5 groups of $\text{万}$, plus an additional 2 hundred $\text{万}$, resulting in precisely $5,200,000$.

Try it yourself: If a small town has $8 \text{万}$ residents, and you find that this is really $80,000$ people, how many groups of ten thousand are there? Use the simple rule to quickly solve it!

### 刀 (22.2s)

## 刀

Have you ever sliced through a loaf of bread or cut open a box? You used something that concentrates force into a very fine line—a blade. A **blade** is simply a piece of material, usually metal, shaped with one or two sharp edges designed for cutting or separating things. When we talk about the *edge*, we mean the extremely thin border along the length of the blade; this edge is where all the power is focused.

Picture this: Imagine trying to cut thick cardboard with your fingers versus using a box cutter. The difference isn't just effort, but geometry. A successful cut happens because the entire pressure you apply (the force) is distributed over an incredibly small area—that tiny, sharp edge. This concentrated force allows the blade to overcome the natural resistance of the material being cut.

The simple rule governing blades is this: sharpness relies on focusing maximum force along minimum area. If the edge becomes dull, the force spreads out, and you lose your cutting power. The goal of any good tool is always to maintain that precise, sharp boundary.

To try it yourself, think about a pencil tip versus a blunt piece of wood. Even though both can make marks, only the highly refined point (the functional edge) allows for clean separation. Next time you use a slicing tool, remember that every millimeter counts—it is the focused line that makes all the difference!

### 角 (23.3s)

## 角

Imagine opening a pair of scissors: the space created between the two blades is an angle! An angle, simply put, is the measure of the amount of rotation or the space formed when two straight lines meet. The point where these two lines connect is called the **vertex**, which is our starting corner. Each line segment forming the angle is called a **ray**, and we use them to draw the boundary.

Picture this: Think about the hands on an analog clock. When the hour hand and minute hand are apart, they form an angle ($\angle$). This space is measured in units called **degrees** ($^\circ$), where a full circle equals $360^\circ$. Our simple rule for measuring angles is that we measure the amount of "turn" from one ray to the other. If $\overrightarrow{A}$ and $\overrightarrow{B}$ are two rays starting at vertex $O$, the angle formed, denoted $\angle AOB$, tells us exactly how far apart those two rays are.

To practice, look around your room. Can you identify an angle? Find a corner where two walls meet—that is a perfect example of a right angle, which measures exactly $90^\circ$. Try to visualize the space between your book and your desk; that gap is another measurable angle!
