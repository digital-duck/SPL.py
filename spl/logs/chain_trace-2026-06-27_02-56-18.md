## Suffix

Have you ever noticed how adding just a couple of letters to the end of a word can completely change it? Consider the word *happy*. It tells us about a feeling, but if we attach the suffix *-ness* (which simply means "the state of being"), we get *happiness*. This little ending piece is what we call a **suffix**. A suffix is a meaningful group of letters added to the end of a larger word. The original word that remains—like *happy* in our example—is called the **base** or root.

Picture this: Think of words like they are simple, sturdy building blocks. When you find a base block and attach a specific suffix block, you don't just make a slightly different version; you construct an entirely new concept! The suffix acts like a little linguistic machine that modifies the meaning or function of the original word.

The simple rule to remember is: If you can identify a core meaningful unit (the base) and then see that another piece has been added to its end, chances are the added piece is a suffix. It changes the grammatical role—for instance, turning an action (*run*) into the state of being acted upon (*running*).

## -Ion

Have you ever heard words like *action*, *creation*, or *solution*? These "-ion" endings are very common, and they usually tell us about an activity—the process of doing something. For example, if we talk about the word *act* (the deed), adding the suffix *-ion* turns it into **action**, which means the whole process or energy of acting out that deed. The suffix $-ion$ basically signals that we are naming a specific event or a continuous flow.

Picture this: Think of an electrical circuit. The core concept is electricity, but if you want to name the *flow* of that electricity through a wire, you might use the word **current**. That "-ent" ending describes the constant movement—the process itself. Similarly, $-ion$ allows us to label any action as a complete process. It takes something basic (like an idea or a concept) and names the journey it takes to become real.

The simple rule is this: If you can describe the word's root as a physical or mental *process*—an activity that happens over time—it probably ends in $-ion$. To figure out if a word belongs here, try asking yourself: "What is the name of this process?" The answer likely has an "-ion" ending.

Try it yourself! If you know how to calculate something (a process), what word do you get by adding $-ion$ to describe the *act* of calculating? Take a moment and see if the pattern holds up for other verbs, too!
