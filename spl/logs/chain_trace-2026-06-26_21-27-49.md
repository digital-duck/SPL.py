## Semitone

Have you ever noticed how certain keys on a piano seem physically closer together than others? This perception points to the fundamental unit of musical distance: the semitone. For any musician or theorist, understanding this smallest interval is key because it dictates the structure of scales and chords, allowing for precise control over pitch movement.

**Definition:** A semitone is defined as half the size of a whole tone. It represents the minimum measurable interval on the standard chromatic scale in Western music theory. While octave relationships define frequency ratios (like $2:1$), the semitone defines the smallest quantized step between adjacent notes, such as C to $\text{C}\sharp$.

**Worked Example:** Consider the interval starting at Middle C ($C_4$) and moving up to D ($D_4$). We can break this movement down into successive steps: $C \rightarrow \text{C}\sharp$ (1 semitone); $\text{C}\sharp \rightarrow D\flat$ (1 semitone); and finally, $D\flat \rightarrow D$ (1 semitone). Therefore, the total interval from $C_4$ to $D_4$ spans exactly 3 half-steps, or 3 semitones.

## Major Third

When analyzing harmony in music, we aren't just counting steps; we are measuring precise distances between notes on the pitch spectrum. Consider a simple major triad (like C-E-G). The interval spanning from the root to the third is crucial—it gives the chord its characteristic bright quality. This specific distance defines what we call the Major Third ($\text{M}3$). Understanding $\text{M}3$ allows us to move beyond mere naming and begin quantifying the intervallic relationships that build all Western harmony.

**Definition:** The major third is a consonant interval spanning four semitones (semitones, or half steps) from one pitch class to another. If we designate the root note as $R$, then an interval reaching $R + \text{M}3$ has a size of $\frac{4}{12}$ of an octave, which is exactly $400$ cents.

**Worked Example:** Let's take the key of C Major. If our starting pitch (the root) is C, moving up by a major third lands us precisely on E. We can verify this measurement: C $\to$ C\# $\to$ D $\to$ D\# $\to$ E. This path covers four distinct semitones.

**Why it's true:** The definition of the major scale itself provides the proof for this interval size. In any diatonic major scale, the third scale degree is constructed by climbing two whole steps (two whole steps $= 4$ semitones). Since a whole step contains two semitones, the distance from the root to the third must be $2 + 2 = 4$ semitones. This fixed interval size makes $\text{M}3$ one of the most fundamental building blocks for harmonic analysis.

## Octave

When you sing a note and then hear that same melody sung by your parent—the higher version—you have experienced an octave. This phenomenon is fundamental not just to music theory, but to understanding how physical waves scale in frequency. The concept of the octave represents the simplest and most powerful interval relationship: doubling the pitch.

Formally, an octave defines a specific frequency ratio. If we start with a base frequency $f$, the corresponding note one octave higher has a frequency exactly twice that amount, $2f$. Conversely, the note one octave lower is found at $\frac{1}{2}f$. This relationship means that octaves are defined multiplicatively; they represent powers of two in terms of frequency ratios. Since pitch perception is logarithmic, this ratio appears as a simple doubling on a linear scale.

Consider the common reference frequency $A_4$, tuned to 440 Hz. If we move up one octave from $A_4$ to $A'_5$, we are not simply adding some arbitrary number; we are multiplying by two. The resulting frequency is $A'_5 = 2 \times 440 \, \text{Hz} = 880 \, \text{Hz}$. This consistency allows scientists and engineers to predict the frequencies of nearly any related pitch across an entire scale structure.

The reason this ratio must be exactly two stems from the physics of wave propagation. For a given medium (like air) and constant speed $v$, frequency ($f$) and wavelength ($\lambda$) are linked by $v = f\lambda$. When you double the frequency, $f' = 2f$, the wavelength must also precisely double, $\lambda' = 2\lambda$, to maintain the same wave speed. This stable physical relationship is what grounds the musical definition of the octave in observable physics.

## Perfect Fifth

When composing music or analyzing intervals, certain ratios sound exceptionally stable and consonant. One of the most foundational and defining intervals in Western harmony is the perfect fifth ($\text{P}5$). It gives us a sense of structural completeness—like two elements fitting together perfectly to create a solid foundation. From Renaissance polyphony to modern jazz chords, understanding the $\text{P}5$ is key to grasping harmonic relationships.

**Definition:** The perfect fifth is an interval spanning exactly seven semitones (or $7/12$ of an octave) and has a precise frequency ratio of $3:2$. Because its ratio is derived from the simplest integer ratios found in nature (the relationship between the fundamental frequency and the third harmonic), it is considered mathematically pure and inherently stable.

**Worked Example:** Consider starting on Middle C ($C_4$). The note that forms a perfect fifth above it is G ($G_4$). We can confirm this by counting semitones: $C \to C\# \to D \to D\# \to E \to F \to F\# \to G$. This represents exactly seven steps, corresponding to the ratio $3:2$.

**Why It's True (Justification):** The stability of the $\text{P}5$ is rooted in harmonic physics. When a string vibrates at frequency $f$, its overtones (harmonics) appear at integer multiples of $f$: $1f, 2f, 3f, 4f, \dots$. If we pluck a note and listen for the second overtone ($2f$), that tone will sound exactly an octave above the fundamental. However, if we listen for the third overtone ($3f$), that tone corresponds precisely to the $\text{P}5$ ratio (since $3f / 1f = 3/1$; and dividing by $2f$ gives $3/2$). The appearance of this simple $3:2$ relationship in the natural overtones is what makes the perfect fifth so universally consonant.
