### 子 (15.3s)

## 子

**Everyday example.** Imagine a newborn baby, wrapped snugly in a blanket, tiny arms reaching out to either side. That image is exactly what this character captures.

**Picture this.** The character 子 (pronounced *zǐ*, rhyming with "sir") is one of the oldest Chinese pictographs — a drawing that became writing. Look at it closely: the curved top stroke represents the baby's round head, the short horizontal strokes suggest little outstretched arms, and the downward curve at the bottom shows a swaddled body. Ancient oracle-bone carvings from 3,000 years ago look even more like a tiny figure. Over centuries, the strokes were simplified into the neat symbol we use today.

**Simple rule.** 子 carries the core meaning of *child* or *offspring*. Whenever you see 子 inside another character, it often hints at ideas related to children, birth, smallness, or the next generation. It appears in hundreds of compound words: 孩子 (*háizi*, "child"), 儿子 (*érzi*, "son"), and even in the name of the great philosopher 孔子 (*Kǒngzǐ*, Confucius — literally "Master Kong").

As a standalone number concept, 子 also labels the *first* of the twelve Earthly Branches, the classical Chinese counting cycle, where it corresponds to the rat and to midnight — new beginnings.

**Try it yourself.** Write 子 three times, saying *zǐ* aloud each time. Can you spot the baby's head, arms, and bundled body in your own strokes?
