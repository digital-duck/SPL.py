### prefix (28.4s)

## Prefix

Have you ever noticed how adding just a few letters to the beginning of a word can totally change its meaning? Consider the word *happy*. Now, if we put the prefix $\text{un-}$ in front of it, we get *unhappy*. The root idea—the core concept of happiness—remains, but the entire meaning shifts. We didn't add letters to the end (that would be a suffix), nor did we change anything inside the word. Instead, we added something entirely new right at the start.

Picture this: Think of a base word like a sturdy box. A prefix is like a small sign or sticker that you tape onto the very front edge of that box. This sticker changes what the box represents without altering the box itself. In morphology (the study of word structure), we call these tiny meaning units *morphemes*. The core, standalone morpheme is called the *base* (like *happy*). A prefix is simply a full morpheme added to the beginning of that base.

The simple rule for prefixes is: $\text{Prefix} + \text{Base} = \text{New Word}$. Remember that the prefix itself might not be a word on its own, but it carries meaning (like *un-* meaning 'not'). To solidify this concept, look at the word *re-read*. The base is *read*, and the prefix $\text{re-}$ tells us to do the action again. Try identifying the prefix in words like *disagree* or *preheat*.

### stem (38.7s)

## Stem

Have you ever wondered how one simple word can become dozens of related words? Consider the word *read*. If we want to talk about the person who reads, we might use the word *reader*. Notice that the core meaning—the act of reading—is what remains when we strip away the ending *-er* (which is a type of suffix that often means "one who does"). That central chunk, *read*, is our **stem**. A stem is simply the root or base part of a word that carries its main definition.

Picture this: Think of words like LEGO structures. The stem is the sturdy foundation piece—it gives the structure its basic function. Everything attached to it (these attachments are called affixes, which include prefixes and suffixes) modifies that core meaning, building complexity on top. For instance, *un-* attaches to *happy*, creating *unhappy*. The core idea is still "happy," modified by the prefix *un-*.

The simple rule for identifying a stem is this: If you can find an attached piece (an affix) and remove it, and the remaining core part still makes sense, that core part is likely the stem. For example, in *unbelievable*, we see the prefix *un-* (meaning "not") attached to the stem *believe*. The whole word builds its meaning around that strong foundation.

Try it yourself: In the word *catastrophe* (a sudden disaster), what do you think the stem is? If you remove the ending, which core word do you feel remains as the central idea? You've got this—you are already thinking like a linguist!

## In-

Notice how just adding those two little letters, `in-`, completely changes the meaning from "able to be seen" to "not able to be seen." This tiny addition is an example of a **prefix**—a morpheme (or smallest meaningful unit) attached to the beginning of a word to modify its core meaning.

Picture this: Prefixes are like little signaling flags placed at the start of words. When we add `in-` before a base word, it often acts as a signal for negation, meaning "not" or "the opposite of." Instead of rewriting the whole word, we simply attach the prefix to flip its original idea. For example, if *complete* means finished, adding `in-` creates *incomplete*, which signals that something is *not* finished.

### port (14.8s)

## Port

Do you ever notice how many words seem to share a common core? Consider the word *transport*. It feels complex, but if we break it down, we find a small piece—the root **port**. This morpheme (a meaningful unit of language, like a single building block) relates to carrying or moving things. When you see 'port' in different words, it often suggests movement from one place to another.

Picture this: Imagine the entire English vocabulary as a giant train network. The morphemes are the actual cars on the train, and some car types link together to make long, complex trains (the words). The root **port** is like a specific type of coupling mechanism that connects ideas of motion—like linking *export* (to carry out) with *import* (to bring in).

The simple rule is this: If you see the morpheme $\text{port}$ within a word, it generally means 'to carry' or 'to move across.' It acts as a central pivot point for concepts involving transfer. Understanding its presence helps us predict that even complicated words are built from these smaller, predictable chunks.

Try it yourself! Can you think of another everyday word that uses $\text{port}$ to suggest movement? Perhaps the place where things are loaded onto ships—the actual physical location called a *port* itself is also derived from this core meaning!

### con- (22.4s)

## Con-

Have you ever noticed how sometimes adding a tiny piece to the front of a word completely changes its meaning? That little piece is called a **prefix**, and think of it like a linguistic turbo button! Today, we are looking at the prefix *con-*. When we add *con-* to a word base, it often suggests an action done together, or reaching a state of completion. For example, if you have the root word *nect* (related to tying), adding *con-* creates **connect**, meaning to tie things together.

Picture this: Imagine that words are like LEGO structures. The main part of the word is the base, and prefixes like *con-* are specialized connectors that help build a bigger idea. When we use *con-*, we are focusing on connection or joining forces. We see this in **container**, where *con-* emphasizes holding things together inside one boundary.

The simple rule here is: If you encounter *con-* at the start of an English word, pause and ask yourself if the meaning relates to *togetherness* or a *completion* of action. It helps us understand that we are connecting two separate ideas into one whole concept. Remember, prefixes don't always follow strict Latin rules; they often help illuminate the core idea!

Try it yourself: Take the word **confuse**. We know the root relates to 'fuse' or mix. By recognizing *con-* adds the sense of togetherness, you can understand that ‘confuse’ means mixing things together in a jumbled state.
