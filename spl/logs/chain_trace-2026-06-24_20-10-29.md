### physical_quantity (14.8s)

## Physical Quantity

**Everyday example.** When you step on a bathroom scale, the display might read "68 kg." That reading combines two pieces: a *number* (68) and a *unit* (kilograms). Together they form a **physical quantity** — a measurable property of something in the real world.

**Picture this.** Imagine you tell a friend, "The table is 120." They would immediately ask, "120 what — centimeters? inches? bananas?" A bare number is meaningless without a unit. A physical quantity always pairs the two:

$$Q = \{n\} \times [u]$$

where $\{n\}$ is the numerical value and $[u]$ is the unit. Change the unit and the number must change to keep $Q$ the same: $1.5 \text{ m} = 150 \text{ cm}$. The *quantity itself* — the actual length of that table — never changes.

**Simple rule.** A physical quantity = a number + a unit. Neither part alone is enough. "5 seconds" is a physical quantity; plain "5" is not.

Some quantities you already use every day: temperature ($37\,°\text{C}$), speed ($60\,\text{km/h}$), mass ($0.5\,\text{kg}$). Each one answers a *how much* or *how fast* question about nature.

**Try it yourself.** Pick three objects near you. For each one, write down one physical quantity you could measure — for example, "the mug's mass is about $0.3\,\text{kg}$." Notice how every answer needs both a number and a unit.

### precision (16.0s)

## Precision

**Everyday example.** Imagine you're a doctor running a test to screen patients for a rare disease. The test flags 20 people as positive, but when you double-check, only 12 of them actually have the disease. The other 8 were false alarms. Precision tells you how trustworthy those positive flags really are.

**Picture this.** Think of an archer shooting at a target. Precision isn't about hitting the bullseye — it's about how tightly the arrows cluster together. If every arrow lands in the same small area, the archer is precise, even if that area is off-center. In data science, precision asks: "Of all the times my model said *yes*, how often was it actually right?"

**Simple rule.** Precision is the fraction of positive predictions that turned out to be correct. If your model flags $P$ items as positive, and $TP$ of those are truly positive (called **true positives**), then:

$$\text{Precision} = \frac{TP}{TP + FP}$$

where $FP$ stands for **false positives** — the items incorrectly flagged. A precision of $1.0$ means every positive prediction was correct. A precision of $0.5$ means half were wrong.

**Try it yourself.** A spam filter marks 50 emails as spam. You check and find 40 are actually spam, while 10 are legitimate. What is the filter's precision? (Hint: $TP = 40$, $FP = 10$.)

### uncertainty (15.2s)

## Uncertainty

**Everyday example.** You check the weather app before a picnic. It says "70 % chance of rain." That number is not telling you *what will happen* — it is telling you *how sure we are* about what might happen. That feeling of "not completely knowing" is what we call **uncertainty**: a measure of how much we don't know about an outcome before it occurs.

**Picture this.** Imagine a jar filled with 10 marbles — 7 red and 3 blue. If you reach in without looking, you can't be certain which colour you'll grab. You know *something* (the mix of colours), but not *everything* (which marble your fingers will touch). Uncertainty lives in that gap between partial knowledge and complete knowledge.

**Simple rule.** We often express uncertainty as a number between $0$ and $1$. A value of $0$ means "impossible — it will definitely not happen," and $1$ means "certain — it will definitely happen." Everything in between reflects varying degrees of doubt:

$$0 \;\leq\; \text{uncertainty measure} \;\leq\; 1$$

The closer the value sits to $0.5$, the less confident we are about the outcome going one way or the other — like flipping a fair coin.

**Try it yourself.** Think of three events in your day: one you're almost sure will happen, one that's a coin-flip, and one that's very unlikely. Assign each a number between $0$ and $1$. Notice how the number captures your *degree of belief*, not a guarantee.

### order_of_magnitude (14.0s)

## Order Of Magnitude

**Everyday example.** How far is it from your house to the grocery store? Maybe 2 kilometres. How far is it to the Moon? About 384,000 kilometres. You don't need the exact numbers to see that the Moon trip is *wildly* bigger. An **order of magnitude** is a way to express that kind of enormous jump using powers of ten.

**Picture this.** Line up these distances on a number line, but instead of counting 1, 2, 3, you count by multiplying by 10 each step: $10^0 = 1$, $10^1 = 10$, $10^2 = 100$, $10^3 = 1{,}000$, and so on. The grocery store at 2 km sits near $10^0$ (a few kilometres). The Moon at 384,000 km sits near $10^5$. We say the Moon is **about five orders of magnitude** farther away — meaning roughly $10^5 = 100{,}000$ times farther.

**Simple rule.** To find the order of magnitude of any number, ask: *"Which power of ten is closest?"* For a number $n$, its order of magnitude is the integer $k$ such that $10^k$ is the nearest power of ten. In practice, just count the digits and subtract one: a number with 7 digits lives around $10^6$.

**Try it yourself.** The Earth's population is about 8,000,000,000. How many digits? Ten. So its order of magnitude is roughly $10^{9}$ — we say "on the order of billions." Now compare that to the number of stars in the Milky Way, about $10^{11}$. How many orders of magnitude separate them?

### significant_figures (14.5s)

## Significant Figures

**Everyday example.** You step on a bathroom scale and it reads 68 kg. A laboratory balance reads 68.042 kg. Both describe your mass, but the lab number carries more meaningful detail. The digits that actually carry reliable information are called **significant figures** (often shortened to "sig figs").

**Picture this.** Imagine you measure a table with a ruler marked in centimetres. You can confidently read 92 cm, and you might estimate one extra digit — 92.3 cm. That gives you three significant figures: every digit you trust, plus one you estimated. If someone then writes the length as 92.300000 cm, those trailing zeros pretend the ruler was far more precise than it really was.

**Simple rule.** Count every digit that is *not* just a placeholder zero. Here is the quick checklist:

- All non-zero digits count: $409$ has 3 sig figs.
- Zeros *between* non-zero digits count: $3{,}007$ has 4 sig figs.
- Leading zeros never count: $0.0052$ has 2 sig figs ($5$ and $2$).
- Trailing zeros after a decimal point count: $8.100$ has 4 sig figs.

When you multiply or divide, your answer keeps the fewest sig figs of any input. For example, $4.56 \times 1.4 = 6.384$, but $1.4$ has only 2 sig figs, so you round the result to $6.4$.

**Try it yourself.** How many significant figures are in $0.03070$? (Hint: the leading zeros are placeholders, but the trailing zero is intentional.)

### approximation (13.6s)

## Approximation

**Everyday example.** You glance at your watch and tell a friend, "It's about three o'clock," even though the exact time is 2:57. That rough answer is an *approximation*—a value that is close enough to the true answer to be useful, even though it is not perfectly exact.

**Picture this.** Imagine measuring the length of a curved garden path with a ruler. You can't bend the ruler, so you lay it along short straight pieces of the path and add them up. Each straight piece misses a tiny bit of the curve, but the more pieces you use, the closer your total gets to the real length. Your sum of straight segments is an approximation of the curved distance.

**Simple rule.** An approximation replaces something hard to compute or measure with something simpler that is "close enough." We write

$$x \approx a$$

to say "$x$ is approximately equal to $a$." The gap between them is called the *error*:

$$\text{error} = |x - a|$$

A good approximation keeps this error small enough for the task at hand. "About three o'clock" has an error of 3 minutes—fine for casual plans, not for catching a train.

**Try it yourself.** The value of $\pi$ is $3.14159\ldots$ Use the approximation $\pi \approx 3.14$ and calculate the circumference of a circle with radius $5$: $C \approx 2 \times 3.14 \times 5$. How far is your answer from the more precise value $C = 2\pi \times 5 = 31.4159\ldots$?
