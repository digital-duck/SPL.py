### angle (0.2s)

## Angle

每当你打开一扇门，门与门框之间就形成了一个张开的程度——这就是角。从时钟的指针到篮球的投篮弧线，角无处不在，它是几何学最基本的度量之一。

**定义。** 两条共享同一端点的射线所构成的图形称为**角**（angle）。共享的端点称为**顶点**（vertex），两条射线称为角的**边**（sides）。若射线 $\overrightarrow{OA}$ 与 $\overrightarrow{OB}$ 共享顶点 $O$，则所成的角记作 $\angle AOB$。角的大小度量一条边旋转到另一条边所经过的量，常用**度**（degree）或**弧度**（radian）表示：

$$360° = 2\pi \text{ rad}, \quad 1° = \frac{\pi}{180} \text{ rad}$$

按大小分类：锐角满足 $0° < \theta < 90°$，直角 $\theta = 90°$，钝角 $90° < \theta < 180°$，平角 $\theta = 180°$。

**例题。** 时钟在 3:00 时，时针指向 3，分针指向 12。钟面共 12 格，每格对应 $\frac{360°}{12} = 30°$。时针与分针相差 3 格，因此所成角为：

$$\theta = 3 \times 30° = 90°$$

这是一个直角。

**为什么弧度制成立？** 在半径为 $r$ 的圆中，弧度定义为弧长与半径之比：$\theta = \frac{s}{r}$。当弧长恰好等于半径时，$\theta = 1$ rad。绕整圆一周的弧长为 $2\pi r$，所以完整旋转对应 $\frac{2\pi r}{r} = 2\pi$ rad。这不是人为约定，而是圆的几何性质的自然结果——弧度制直接将角与弧长联系起来，使得微积分中的公式（如 $\frac{d}{d\theta}\sin\theta = \cos\theta$）不需要额外的转换系数。

**练习。** 将 $\theta = 150°$ 转换为弧度，并判断它属于哪一类角。

### distance (0.2s)

## Distance

When you check a map app for directions, the straight-line distance between two points is almost never the route you drive — yet that straight line is the most fundamental measurement in all of geometry. Understanding distance precisely lets us quantify closeness, define circles, and eventually build calculus.

**Definition.** Given two points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$ in the Euclidean plane, the *distance* between them is

$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$

**Worked example.** Find the distance between $A = (1, 2)$ and $B = (4, 6)$.

$$d(A, B) = \sqrt{(4 - 1)^2 + (6 - 2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$$

**Why it's true.** The formula is a direct application of the Pythagorean theorem. Draw a right triangle whose hypotenuse connects $A$ to $B$. The horizontal leg has length $|x_2 - x_1| = 3$ and the vertical leg has length $|y_2 - y_1| = 4$. By the Pythagorean theorem, the hypotenuse satisfies $c^2 = 3^2 + 4^2 = 25$, so $c = 5$. This argument works for any two points: the coordinate differences give the legs, and the theorem gives the hypotenuse — which is exactly the straight-line distance.

Notice that the formula always returns a non-negative number, and swapping the two points doesn't change the result — both facts follow from squaring the differences before taking the square root. These properties match our intuition: a distance is never negative, and the distance from $A$ to $B$ is the same as from $B$ to $A$.

Because the formula relies only on coordinate differences, it generalizes cleanly: in three dimensions you add a $(z_2 - z_1)^2$ term under the radical, in four dimensions another term, and so on. But the core idea — sum the squared differences, then take the square root — remains the Pythagorean theorem at work.

**Practice problem.** A triangle has vertices at $P = (0, 0)$, $Q = (3, 0)$, and $R = (0, 4)$. Compute all three side lengths and confirm that no single side is longer than the sum of the other two.

### angle_measure (0.2s)

## Angle Measure

**Definition.** An *angle* is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint, called the *vertex*. The *measure* of an angle quantifies the amount of rotation from one ray (the initial side) to the other (the terminal side). Two standard units of measure are in common use:

- **Degrees:** A full rotation is divided into $360$ equal parts, so one *degree* ($1^\circ$) is $\tfrac{1}{360}$ of a full rotation.
- **Radians:** The radian measure of an angle $\theta$ is defined as the ratio

$$\theta = \frac{s}{r},$$

where $s$ is the arc length subtended on a circle of radius $r$ centered at the vertex. Since a full circle has circumference $2\pi r$, a complete rotation equals $2\pi$ radians.

The conversion between the two systems is given by:

$$\theta_{\text{rad}} = \frac{\pi}{180}\,\theta_{\deg}, \qquad \theta_{\deg} = \frac{180}{\pi}\,\theta_{\text{rad}}.$$

**Worked Example.** Convert $135^\circ$ to radians and determine the arc length subtended on a circle of radius $r = 4$.

$$\theta = 135 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{3\pi}{4} \text{ radians.}$$

The corresponding arc length is:

$$s = r\theta = 4 \cdot \frac{3\pi}{4} = 3\pi \approx 9.42.$$

**Key Theorem (Radian Measure and Arc Length).** *Let a central angle of measure $\theta$ (in radians) be inscribed in a circle of radius $r > 0$. Then the arc length $s$ subtended by the angle satisfies:*

$$s = r\theta.$$

*Proof sketch.* The arc length scales linearly with the fraction $\tfrac{\theta}{2\pi}$ of the full circle, giving $s = 2\pi r \cdot \tfrac{\theta}{2\pi} = r\theta$. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
import sympy as sp

theta_deg = sp.Rational(135)
theta_rad = sp.pi * theta_deg / 180
r = 4

arc_length = r * theta_rad

print("Radians    :", theta_rad)          # 3*pi/4
print("Arc length :", arc_length)         # 3*pi
```

Running this cell confirms $s = 3\pi$, illustrating that radian measure makes the arc-length formula purely multiplicative — a central reason radians are the preferred unit in calculus and analysis. The sector area formula $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ follows by the same proportionality argument and will be taken up in the next section alongside the unit circle.

### line (0.2s)

## Line

想象一条笔直的公路，向两端无限延伸，没有起点，也没有终点。这就是几何中**直线**的直觉来源——它是空间中最基本的对象之一。

**定义。** 在欧几里得几何中，直线是由两个不同的点唯一确定的一维对象，沿两个方向无限延伸。给定平面上两点 $A(x_1, y_1)$ 和 $B(x_2, y_2)$（其中 $A \neq B$），通过它们的直线可以用参数方程表示：

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = (1-t)\,\mathbf{A} + t\,\mathbf{B}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

当 $x_1 \neq x_2$ 时，直线也可以写成斜截式 $y = mx + b$，其中斜率 $m = \dfrac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$。

**例题。** 求过点 $P(1, 2)$ 和 $Q(4, 8)$ 的直线方程。

先算斜率：

$$m = \frac{8 - 2}{4 - 1} = \frac{6}{3} = 2$$

代入点斜式 $y - y_1 = m(x - x_1)$：

$$y - 2 = 2(x - 1) \implies y = 2x$$

验证：当 $x = 4$ 时，$y = 8$，与 $Q$ 吻合。✓

**为什么两点确定一条直线？** 这是欧几里得第一公设的内容。直觉上，如果存在两条不同的直线都经过 $A$ 和 $B$，则它们之间的区域会产生"弯曲"，与直线的定义矛盾。在公理化体系中，这一性质被直接假定为基础真理——所有后续定理都建立在它之上。

直线的概念延伸至高维空间：在 $\mathbb{R}^n$ 中，过点 $\mathbf{p}$、方向为 $\mathbf{d} \neq \mathbf{0}$ 的直线为 $\{\mathbf{p} + t\,\mathbf{d} \mid t \in \mathbb{R}\}$。无论维度如何变化，"由一点和一个方向唯一确定"这一本质不变。

**练习。** 直线 $\ell$ 过点 $(−1, 5)$ 且斜率为 $-3$。求 $\ell$ 与 $x$ 轴的交点坐标。

### point (0.2s)

## Point

Every map begins with a single pin. Before you can draw a route, measure a distance, or outline a region, you need a way to say *where*. In geometry, that role belongs to the **point** — the simplest and most fundamental object in the entire subject.

**Definition.** A *point* is a location in space that has no size, no shape, and no dimension. It has position and nothing else. We label points with capital letters and represent them as dots on the page, but the dot is only a visual aid — a true point is infinitely small.

Formally, in a coordinate system, a point in the plane is an ordered pair $P = (x, y)$ where $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$. In three-dimensional space, a point is an ordered triple $P = (x, y, z)$.

**Worked Example.** Consider the point $A = (3, 5)$. This tells us $A$ sits exactly 3 units along the horizontal axis and 5 units along the vertical axis from the origin $O = (0, 0)$. The point $A$ occupies that single location — it contains no length, no area, nothing to measure except its coordinates.

Now consider $B = (3, 5)$ labeled differently. Despite the different letter, $A$ and $B$ are the *same point* because they share identical coordinates. A point's identity is its position: two points are equal if and only if their corresponding coordinates match. Conversely, $C = (5, 3)$ is a *different point* from $A$, because order matters in an ordered pair — $(3, 5) \neq (5, 3)$.

**Why it's true.** Euclid opens the *Elements* with: "A point is that which has no part." This is not a theorem but an axiom — an irreducible starting agreement. We accept it because every geometric object (lines, planes, solids) can be described as collections of points, yet no geometric object can be decomposed into anything simpler than a point. It is the atom of geometry.

**Practice Problem.** A point $P$ in the plane has coordinates $(a,\; 2a - 1)$. If $P$ is the same point as $Q = (4, 7)$, find $a$ and verify that both coordinates match.

### line_segment (0.2s)

## line_segment

**Definition.** A *line segment* is the convex hull of two distinct points in Euclidean space. Given points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the line segment $\overline{PQ}$ is the set

$$\overline{PQ} = \{ (1 - t)\,P + t\,Q \mid t \in [0, 1] \}.$$

The parameter $t$ traces the segment continuously from $P$ (at $t = 0$) to $Q$ (at $t = 1$). Each value of $t$ determines a unique interior or boundary point, so the parameterization is injective on $[0,1]$. The **length** of $\overline{PQ}$ is the Euclidean distance between its endpoints:

$$|\overline{PQ}| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$

This formula extends naturally to $\mathbb{R}^n$ by summing squared coordinate differences across all $n$ dimensions.

---

**Worked Example.** Let $P = (1, 2)$ and $Q = (4, 6)$.

- *Length:* $|\overline{PQ}| = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$
- *Point at* $t = 0.25$:

$$\point = (1 - 0.25)(1, 2) + 0.25(4, 6) = (0.75 + 1,\; 1.5 + 1.5) = (1.75, 3).$$

This confirms that interior points are uniquely recovered by specifying $t \in (0,1)$, and that the parameterization distributes proportionally along the segment — the point at $t = 0.25$ lies exactly one-quarter of the way from $P$ to $Q$.

---

**Key Theorem (Triangle Inequality).** For any three points $A, B, C \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$|\overline{AC}| \;\leq\; |\overline{AB}| + |\overline{BC}|,$$

with equality if and only if $B$ lies on the segment $\overline{AC}$. *Proof sketch:* Apply the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality to the vectors $\mathbf{u} = B - A$ and $\mathbf{v} = C - B$, noting that $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$.

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import Point, Segment, Rational

P = Point(1, 2)
Q = Point(4, 6)
seg = Segment(P, Q)

print("Length  :", seg.length)                              # 5
print("At t=0.25:", seg.arbitrary_point(Rational(1, 4)))   # Point2D(7/4, 3)
```

Running this cell reproduces each quantity derived above and verifies the parameterization symbolically, bridging geometric intuition with computational confirmation.

### polygon (0.2s)

## Polygon

**Definition.** A *polygon* is a closed plane figure formed by a finite sequence of line segments—called *sides* or *edges*—such that each segment shares exactly one endpoint with the next, and the last segment connects back to the first. Formally, a polygon $P$ with $n$ sides ($n \geq 3$) is determined by an ordered set of *vertices* $\{V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, with edges $\overline{V_1V_2},\, \overline{V_2V_3},\, \ldots,\, \overline{V_{n-1}V_n},\, \overline{V_nV_1}$. A polygon is called *simple* if no two non-adjacent edges intersect; all results in this section assume simplicity.

---

**Worked Example.** Consider the regular hexagon with center at the origin and circumradius $R = 2$. Its six vertices are given by

$$V_k = \left(2\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi(k-1)}{6}\right),\; 2\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi(k-1)}{6}\right)\right), \quad k = 1, 2, \ldots, 6.$$

Computing explicitly: $V_1 = (2, 0)$, $V_2 = (1, \sqrt{3})$, $V_3 = (-1, \sqrt{3})$, and so on. The side length equals $s = R = 2$, and the interior angle at each vertex is $\dfrac{(6-2)\cdot 180^\circ}{6} = 120^\circ$.

---

**Key Theorem (Interior Angle Sum).** For any simple polygon with $n$ vertices, the sum of interior angles is

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta_i = (n - 2)\cdot \pi \text{ radians} = (n-2)\cdot 180^\circ.$$

*Proof sketch.* Triangulate the polygon by drawing $n - 3$ non-crossing diagonals from a single vertex, producing exactly $n - 2$ triangles. Since each triangle's angles sum to $\pi$, and every triangle angle is an interior angle of $P$, the total is $(n-2)\pi$. $\square$

The area of a simple polygon whose vertices are listed in order is computed via the *Shoelace Formula*:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(x_i\, y_{i+1} - x_{i+1}\, y_i\bigr)\right|,$$

where indices are taken modulo $n$.

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import cos, sin, pi, simplify, Polygon, Point

# Regular hexagon with circumradius R = 2
R, n = 2, 6
vertices = [Point(R*cos(2*pi*k/n), R*sin(2*pi*k/n)) for k in range(n)]
hex_poly = Polygon(*vertices)

print("Area:              ", simplify(hex_poly.area))       # 6√3
print("Perimeter:         ", simplify(hex_poly.perimeter))  # 12
print("Interior angle sum:", (n - 2) * 180, "degrees")      # 720
```

Running this confirms $A = 6\sqrt{3} \approx 10.39$ and perimeter $= 12$, consistent with the formula $A = \tfrac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}s^2$ for a regular hexagon of side $s = 2$.

### rigid_motion (0.2s)

## rigid_motion

### Definition

A **rigid motion** (or *isometry*) of $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a map $T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ that preserves the Euclidean distance between every pair of points. Formally, $T$ is a rigid motion if and only if

$$
\|T(\mathbf{x}) - T(\mathbf{y})\| = \|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}\| \quad \forall\, \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n.
$$

Every rigid motion decomposes uniquely as the composition of an orthogonal linear map $R \in O(n)$ — a matrix satisfying $R^{\top}R = I$ — and a translation by a fixed vector $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$
T(\mathbf{x}) = R\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}.
$$

Here $R$ and $\mathbf{t}$ are assumed to be known building blocks; the content of this section is the rigid motion itself — the particular combination that guarantees distances are unchanged.

---

### Worked Example

Consider the rigid motion $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ defined by a rotation of $\theta = \frac{\pi}{3}$ about the origin followed by a translation $\mathbf{t} = (2,\,1)^\top$.

The rotation matrix is

$$
R = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\tfrac{\pi}{3} & -\sin\tfrac{\pi}{3} \\ \sin\tfrac{\pi}{3} & \cos\tfrac{\pi}{3} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{1}{2} & -\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \\ \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}.
$$

Applying $T$ to the point $\mathbf{x} = (2, 0)^\top$:

$$
T\!\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}\tfrac{1}{2}&-\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\\\tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}&\tfrac{1}{2}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2\\0\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\\\sqrt{3}\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}2\\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\\\sqrt{3}+1\end{pmatrix}.
$$

One verifies distance preservation by computing $\|T(\mathbf{x}) - T(\mathbf{0})\| = \|(3-2,\,\sqrt{3}+1-1)^\top\| = \|(1,\sqrt{3})^\top\| = 2 = \|\mathbf{x}\|$. $\checkmark$

---

### Key Theorem

**Theorem (Structure of Isometries).** Every isometry $T$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ has the form $T(\mathbf{x}) = R\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$ with $R \in O(n)$. Furthermore, the composition of two rigid motions is again a rigid motion, and every rigid motion has an inverse that is also a rigid motion.

*Proof sketch.* Distance-preservation implies that $T$ maps affine lines to affine lines and is therefore affine. Writing $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$ and expanding $\|T(\mathbf{x})-T(\mathbf{y})\|^2 = \|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}\|^2$ yields $A^\top A = I$, so $A \in O(n)$. Closure under composition follows by direct substitution: if $T_1(\mathbf{x}) = R_1\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{t}_1$ and $T_2(\mathbf{x}) = R_2\mathbf{x}+\mathbf{t}_2$, then $T_2 \circ T_1(\mathbf{x}) = R_2 R_1\mathbf{x} + R_2\mathbf{t}_1 + \mathbf{t}_2$, which has orthogonal part $R_2 R_1 \in O(n)$.

---

### Lab Cell (SymPy)

```python
import sympy as sp

theta = sp.pi / 3
R = sp.Matrix([
    [sp.cos(theta), -sp.sin(theta)],
    [sp.sin(theta),  sp.cos(theta)]
])
t = sp.Matrix([2, 1])
x = sp.Matrix([2, 0])

Tx = R @ x + t
print("T(x) =", Tx)                          # [3, sqrt(3)+1]
print("det(R) =", R.det())                    # 1
print("R^T R =", sp.simplify(R.T @ R))        # Identity
```

Running this cell confirms $R^\top R = I$, establishing that $T(\mathbf{x}) = R\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t}$ is a rigid motion: all distances from the origin are preserved after applying it.

### congruence (0.2s)

## Congruence

Clock faces share a quiet secret with number theory. When a clock reads 3:00 and you add 12 hours, the hands return to exactly 3:00. The extra 12 hours "vanish" because they're a full cycle. This is congruence in disguise.

**Definition.** Let $n$ be a positive integer called the *modulus*. Two integers $a$ and $b$ are **congruent modulo $n$**, written

$$a \equiv b \pmod{n},$$

if $n$ divides their difference, i.e., $n \mid (a - b)$. Equivalently, $a$ and $b$ leave the same remainder when divided by $n$.

**Worked example.** Is $38 \equiv 14 \pmod{12}$?

Compute the difference: $38 - 14 = 24 = 12 \times 2$. Since 12 divides 24, the answer is **yes**. Both 38 and 14 leave remainder 2 when divided by 12, just as 2:00 and 14:00 name the same clock position.

**Why it's true.** Congruence modulo $n$ is an *equivalence relation*: it is reflexive ($a \equiv a$), symmetric (if $a \equiv b$ then $b \equiv a$), and transitive (if $a \equiv b$ and $b \equiv c$ then $a \equiv c$). These follow immediately from properties of divisibility. More powerfully, congruence *respects arithmetic*: if $a \equiv b$ and $c \equiv d \pmod{n}$, then

$$a + c \equiv b + d \pmod{n} \quad \text{and} \quad ac \equiv bd \pmod{n}.$$

This means you can reduce large numbers before computing, keeping calculations small without losing information — the engine behind fast algorithms in cryptography and computer science.

**Practice problem.** Find the remainder when $7^{10}$ is divided by 5.

*Hint:* Start by computing $7 \equiv 2 \pmod{5}$, then repeatedly square and reduce: $2^2 \equiv ?, \; 2^4 \equiv ?, \; 2^8 \equiv ?$, and combine to get $2^{10} \pmod 5$.

### triangle (0.2s)

## Triangle

Look at a bicycle frame, a roof truss, or a slice of pizza — triangles appear wherever engineers and architects need rigid, load-bearing structure. A triangle cannot be deformed without changing the length of at least one side, a property no other polygon shares. That rigidity is not accidental; it is a consequence of the geometry itself.

**Definition.** A *triangle* is a polygon with exactly three vertices, three edges, and three interior angles. Given vertices $A$, $B$, $C$, the triangle is written $\triangle ABC$. Its three interior angles satisfy the *angle-sum property*:

$$\angle A + \angle B + \angle C = 180°.$$

Sides are conventionally labeled with lowercase letters matching the opposite vertex: side $a$ is opposite $\angle A$, and so on.

**Worked example.** Suppose $\angle A = 47°$ and $\angle B = 68°$. What is $\angle C$?

Apply the angle-sum property:

$$\angle C = 180° - 47° - 68° = 65°.$$

Since all three angles are strictly between $0°$ and $180°$ and their sum is $180°$, this is a valid (acute) triangle.

**Why the angle sum is $180°$.** Draw $\triangle ABC$ and construct a line $\ell$ through $C$ parallel to $AB$. By the *alternate interior angles* theorem, the angle on the left of $C$ along $\ell$ equals $\angle A$, and the angle on the right equals $\angle B$. Together with $\angle C$ itself, the three angles tile the straight angle at $C$, which measures exactly $180°$. No calculus required — just parallel lines and the definition of a straight angle.

**Practice problem.** In $\triangle PQR$, side $p = 7$, $\angle P = 30°$, and $\angle Q = 90°$. Find $\angle R$ and determine which side is the longest. (Hint: a right triangle's hypotenuse lies opposite the $90°$ angle.)

### area (0.2s)

## Area

**Definition.** Let $R$ be a bounded region in the plane $\mathbb{R}^2$. The *area* of $R$, denoted $A(R)$, is the limiting value of a sequence of Riemann sums formed by partitioning $R$ into infinitesimally small rectangles. More precisely, if $R$ is described by $a \le x \le b$ and $g_1(x) \le y \le g_2(x)$ for continuous functions $g_1, g_2$, then

$$A(R) = \int_a^b \bigl[g_2(x) - g_1(x)\bigr]\, dx.$$

Area is always non-negative: $A(R) \ge 0$, and $A(R) = 0$ only when $R$ has no interior — for instance, a single point or a curve.

---

**Worked Example.** Find the area enclosed between $f(x) = x^2$ and $g(x) = x + 2$.

*Step 1 — Find intersections.* Set $x^2 = x + 2$, giving $x^2 - x - 2 = 0$, so $(x-2)(x+1) = 0$. The curves meet at $x = -1$ and $x = 2$.

*Step 2 — Identify the upper curve.* On $[-1, 2]$, test $x = 0$: $g(0) = 2 > f(0) = 0$, so $g(x) = x+2$ lies above $f(x) = x^2$.

*Step 3 — Integrate.*
$$A = \int_{-1}^{2} \bigl[(x+2) - x^2\bigr]\, dx = \int_{-1}^{2} \bigl(-x^2 + x + 2\bigr)\, dx.$$
$$= \left[-\frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{x^2}{2} + 2x\right]_{-1}^{2} = \left(-\frac{8}{3} + 2 + 4\right) - \left(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} - 2\right) = \frac{9}{2}.$$

---

**Key Theorem (Additivity of Area).** If $R = R_1 \cup R_2$ with $R_1 \cap R_2$ having no interior, then $A(R) = A(R_1) + A(R_2)$. This additivity property is what allows complex regions to be decomposed into simpler sub-regions and integrated piecewise.

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**
```python
from sympy import symbols, integrate, solve

x = symbols('x')
f, g = x**2, x + 2
xs = solve(f - g, x)          # [-1, 2]
area = integrate(g - f, (x, xs[0], xs[1]))
print(area)                    # 9/2
```

### triangle_congruence_criteria (0.2s)

## Triangle Congruence Criteria

When a surveyor measures a plot of land, they don't carry every point of the terrain — they record just enough distances and angles to reconstruct the shape exactly. Triangle congruence criteria formalize this intuition: which minimal set of measurements forces two triangles to be identical?

**Definition.** Two triangles $\triangle ABC$ and $\triangle DEF$ are **congruent**, written $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$, if all three corresponding sides and angles are equal — six equalities in total. The key insight is that you rarely need all six: a carefully chosen subset forces the rest. This section introduces the first such subset.

**Side-Angle-Side (SAS).** If two sides and the *included* angle of one triangle equal the corresponding two sides and included angle of another, the triangles are congruent. (Three additional criteria — SSS, ASA, AAS — follow the same logic and each get their own section.)

**Worked Example.** Suppose $\triangle ABC$ has $AB = 5$, $\angle A = 40°$, and $AC = 7$, and $\triangle DEF$ has $DE = 5$, $\angle D = 40°$, $DF = 7$. The angle sits *between* the two known sides in each triangle, so SAS applies: $\triangle ABC \cong \triangle DEF$. Every remaining measurement — $BC$, $EF$, $\angle B$, $\angle C$, $\angle E$, $\angle F$ — is now forced to match.

**Why It's True.** Place $\triangle DEF$ so that $D$ coincides with $A$ and $DE$ lies along $AB$. Since $DE = AB$, point $E$ lands on $B$. Since $\angle D = \angle A$, ray $DF$ aligns with ray $AC$. Since $DF = AC$, point $F$ lands on $C$. The triangles now occupy the same set of points — congruence follows.

The requirement that the angle be *included* is essential: two sides and a non-included angle do not guarantee congruence, a failure known as the *ambiguous case* that will reappear later.

**Practice Problem.** In $\triangle PQR$ and $\triangle XYZ$, you know $PQ = 8$, $\angle P = 55°$, $PR = 11$, and the matching values $XY = 8$, $\angle X = 55°$, $XZ = 11$. Which criterion applies? Identify the included angle and write the congruence statement.

### pythagorean_theorem (0.3s)

## Pythagorean Theorem

A carpenter wants to ensure a door frame is perfectly square. Without any angle-measuring tools, she measures 3 feet along the bottom, 4 feet up the side, and checks whether the diagonal measures exactly 5 feet. If it does, she knows the corner is a right angle. This ancient trick—used by Egyptian rope-stretchers thousands of years ago—is the Pythagorean theorem in action.

**Definition.** In any right triangle, if $a$ and $b$ are the lengths of the two legs (the sides that form the right angle) and $c$ is the length of the hypotenuse (the side opposite the right angle), then

$$a^2 + b^2 = c^2.$$

**Worked Example.** A ladder leans against a wall. The base of the ladder sits $5\,\text{m}$ from the wall, and the ladder reaches $12\,\text{m}$ up the wall. How long is the ladder?

The wall, ground, and ladder form a right triangle with legs $a = 5$ and $b = 12$. Applying the theorem:

$$c^2 = 5^2 + 12^2 = 25 + 144 = 169 \implies c = \sqrt{169} = 13\,\text{m}.$$

The ladder is exactly $13\,\text{m}$ long.

**Why it's true.** One of the most elegant proofs uses area. Arrange four copies of the same right triangle inside a square of side $a + b$. The four triangles occupy total area $4 \cdot \tfrac{1}{2}ab = 2ab$. The remaining inner region is a square of side $c$, so its area is $c^2$. The outer square has area $(a+b)^2 = a^2 + 2ab + b^2$. Subtracting the triangle area: $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$. $\square$

### similarity (0.2s)

## Similarity

**Definition.** Two geometric figures are *similar* if one can be obtained from the other by a finite composition of isometries (rotations, reflections, translations) and a uniform scaling. Formally, figures $F$ and $F'$ are similar, written $F \sim F'$, if there exists a *similarity transformation* $T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ of the form

$$T(\mathbf{x}) = k R \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{t},$$

where $k > 0$ is the *scale factor*, $R$ is an orthogonal matrix ($R^T R = I$), and $\mathbf{t} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a translation vector. When $k = 1$ the transformation reduces to a rigid motion (congruence); similarity is strictly weaker than congruence.

For triangles, similarity is characterized by angle equality alone: $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ if and only if $\angle A = \angle D$, $\angle B = \angle E$, and $\angle C = \angle F$. The corresponding sides are then *proportional*:

$$\frac{AB}{DE} = \frac{BC}{EF} = \frac{CA}{FD} = k.$$

**Worked Example.** Suppose $\triangle ABC$ has sides $AB = 6$, $BC = 8$, $CA = 10$, and $\triangle DEF$ has sides $DE = 9$, $EF = 12$, $FD = 15$. Check whether the triangles are similar.

Compute the ratios:

$$\frac{DE}{AB} = \frac{9}{6} = \frac{3}{2}, \quad \frac{EF}{BC} = \frac{12}{8} = \frac{3}{2}, \quad \frac{FD}{CA} = \frac{15}{10} = \frac{3}{2}.$$

All three ratios equal $k = \tfrac{3}{2}$, so $\triangle ABC \sim \triangle DEF$ with scale factor $\tfrac{3}{2}$. Note also that $6^2 + 8^2 = 100 = 10^2$, confirming both triangles are right triangles, which independently guarantees AA similarity.

**Key Theorem (AA Criterion).** Two triangles are similar if and only if two pairs of corresponding angles are equal. *Proof sketch:* Given $\angle A = \angle D$ and $\angle B = \angle E$, the angle-sum identity forces $\angle C = \angle F$. Placing $D$ at $A$ and scaling $\triangle DEF$ by $AB/DE$ yields a congruent copy sharing two angles and one side with $\triangle ABC$; the triangles coincide by ASA congruence, establishing proportionality of all sides. $\square$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import symbols, Rational, simplify

AB, BC, CA = 6, 8, 10
DE, EF, FD = 9, 12, 15

ratios = [Rational(DE, AB), Rational(EF, BC), Rational(FD, CA)]
print("Ratios:", ratios)                        # [3/2, 3/2, 3/2]
print("Similar?", len(set(ratios)) == 1)        # True
print("Scale factor k =", ratios[0])            # 3/2
```

### architecture_construction (40.8s)

## Architecture Construction

Imagine assembling a circuit board: every component has a precise slot, and the board only works when every piece is placed correctly and in order. Mathematical structures are built the same way. **Architecture construction** is the process of assembling a formal system — its objects, operations, and governing laws — from the ground up, verifying at each step that the emerging structure satisfies every required property.

**Definition.** An *architecture* over a base set $S$ is a tuple $\mathcal{A} = (S,\, \mathcal{O},\, \mathcal{A}x)$, where $\mathcal{O}$ is a finite collection of operations on $S$ and $\mathcal{A}x$ is a set of axioms those operations must satisfy. A *construction* of $\mathcal{A}$ is a finite sequence of steps $s_1, s_2, \ldots, s_n$ such that each $s_i$ either (i) introduces an element of $S$, (ii) applies an operation in $\mathcal{O}$, or (iii) verifies one axiom in $\mathcal{A}x$.

**Worked Example.** Construct the architecture of a commutative monoid over $S = \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ with addition:

1. **Base set**: $S = \{0, 1, 2, \ldots\}$, defined via Peano's axioms.
2. **Operation**: $+\colon S \times S \to S$.
3. **Closure**: $a, b \in S \;\Rightarrow\; a + b \in S$. ✓
4. **Associativity**: $(a + b) + c = a + (b + c)$ for all $a, b, c \in S$. ✓
5. **Identity**: $a + 0 = a$ for all $a \in S$. ✓
6. **Commutativity**: $a + b = b + a$ for all $a, b \in S$. ✓

The construction is complete only after every axiom is confirmed — each step is a checkpoint, not a guess.

**Why it works.** The strength of architecture construction lies in its *locality*: each step is a small, independently checkable claim. If any step fails, the entire construction halts — there is no "approximately correct" architecture. This sequential verification guarantees that the final structure is logically airtight, not just intuitively plausible.

Identify the identity element, find the inverse of each element, and name the one axiom — beyond those of a commutative monoid — you must verify to complete the construction.
