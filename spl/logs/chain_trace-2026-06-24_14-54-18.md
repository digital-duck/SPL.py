### 子 (35.8s)

## 子

**Everyday example.** Think of a small child reaching up, arms waving. That lively image is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character **子** (pronounced *zǐ*). It originally pictured a baby with a large head and outstretched arms — a universal symbol everyone recognizes.

**Picture this.** In its oldest form, 子 looked like a stick figure of an infant: a round head on top, a body in the middle, and two little arms stretching sideways. Over centuries the brushstrokes simplified, but the meaning stayed anchored: *child, seed, offspring*.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 子 tucked inside a more complex character, it usually signals something related to *children*, *smallness*, *beginning*, or *growth*. You don't need to know the other parts yet — just spotting 子 already tells you part of the story. For example, the common character **好** (hǎo, "good") has 子 sitting on its right side. Even before you learn the left half, you can see the "child" ingredient and sense that the meaning connects to something positive and life-giving.

Because 子 appears in so many compounds, learning it early gives you a master key to dozens of other characters. We will meet several of those companions — like the left half of 好 — in their own sections later.

**Try it yourself.** Write 子 three times, paying attention to stroke order: first the curved top stroke, then the body, then the little hook. Now look at 好, 字, and 學 (xué, "to learn"). Can you spot where 子 is hiding in each one? Circle it.

### 女 (17.3s)

## 女

**Everyday example.** Think about how you might draw a person sitting gracefully with legs crossed. That simple, elegant posture is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they created the character 女 — meaning *woman* or *female*.

**Picture this.** Look at the character 女 and imagine a figure kneeling with arms crossed in front. The top stroke sweeps to the left like one arm, the middle strokes cross like folded hands, and the lower part shows a figure settled close to the ground. In oracle bone script (the earliest Chinese writing, carved on animal bones over 3,000 years ago), 女 looked even more like a person kneeling — the character has simplified over centuries but kept its core shape.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 女 appear on the *left side* of a character, it acts as a **radical** — a building block that hints at meaning. Characters with the 女 radical often relate to women, family relationships, or qualities historically associated with femininity. For example: 妈 (mā, mother), 姐 (jiě, older sister), and 好 (hǎo, good — literally a woman 女 with a child 子).

**Try it yourself.** Write 女 stroke by stroke: first the left-falling stroke, then the turning stroke that crosses it, and finally the horizontal line at the bottom. Now spot the 女 radical hiding inside these characters: 她, 姓, 妹. Can you guess which one means "younger sister"?

### 宀 (33.1s)

## 宀

**Everyday example.** Think about what makes a house feel like a house — before furniture, before walls, it's the **roof** overhead. A roof is the simplest promise of shelter: rain stays out, you stay dry. Across cultures, a single angled line above your head means "safe place."

**Picture this.** Look at the shape 宀. It's a small dot on top (the ridge beam) with two strokes sloping downward on either side — a miniature roof seen from the front. In Chinese writing, this little hat sits above other components to say: *everything underneath is sheltered, contained, or belongs to a household.*

**Simple rule.** When you spot 宀 crowning a character, the meaning almost always connects to **shelter, home, or enclosure**. For example:

- **家** (jiā) — "home": a roof with something living beneath it, because livestock under your roof meant a settled household.
- **安** (ān) — "peace": a roof with someone resting inside, evoking safety and calm within shelter.
- **字** (zì) — "character/word": a roof over new life, like nurturing ideas under one roof.

Don't worry about the components underneath — we'll meet them in later sections. For now, just notice the roof on top and how it steers each character toward the idea of shelter.

The radical 宀 is called **miánbǎo gài** (宝盖头), literally "treasure-cover top."

**Try it yourself.** Spot the 宀 roof in these characters: 宝, 室, 宁. Can you see how each one places something different under the same shelter?

### 好 (18.4s)

## 好

**Everyday example.**
You bite into a perfectly ripe peach and smile. In Chinese, the single word you need is **好** (hǎo) — "good." It is one of the first characters anyone learns, and you will meet it dozens of times a day: 好吃 (hǎo chī, "delicious"), 好看 (hǎo kàn, "good-looking"), 你好 (nǐ hǎo, "hello" — literally "you good").

**Picture this.**
Look at 好 closely and you will see it is made of two smaller pieces sitting side by side. On the left is **女** (nǚ, "woman"). On the right is **子** (zǐ, "child"). A woman beside her child — an image the ancient writers chose to represent something "good," a picture of harmony and completeness. Characters built by combining two meaningful pieces like this are called **compound ideographs** (会意字, huìyì zì): each half contributes an idea, and together they form a new meaning.

**Simple rule.**
When you see an unfamiliar character, ask: *Can I spot smaller pieces I already know?* With 好, once you recognize 女 and 子, the character stops being a mysterious squiggle and becomes a tiny story — mother and child, therefore *good*.

**Try it yourself.**
Cover the right half of 好. Can you write 女 from memory? Now cover the left half and write 子. Finally, combine them. Congratulations — you have just written your first compound ideograph.

### 字 (17.4s)

## 字

**Everyday example.**
Think about how letters combine to make words in English: *c-a-t* becomes "cat." Chinese works differently — instead of stringing letters in a line, you stack or nest small picture-pieces together to build a single square block. Each finished block is called a **字** (pronounced *zì*), meaning "character."

**Picture this.**
Look at 字 itself. On top sits 宀, a little roof shape — it means *shelter* or *house.* Underneath sits 子, which means *child.* A child growing up under a roof: that image captures the idea of something being *born* and *nurtured* inside a structure. A 字 is exactly that — a meaning that comes to life when smaller pieces are assembled inside one tidy frame.

**Simple rule.**
A 字 is the basic unit of written Chinese. Every 字 is built from one or more smaller components (we will call them **elementals** later). You never need to memorize a 字 as a random drawing; instead, you learn its building blocks and see how they fit together — roof + child = "character."

**Try it yourself.**
Find a piece of paper. Draw the roof shape 宀 first, then tuck the child shape 子 beneath it. You have just written 字 — the character that means *character.* Notice how the two pieces cooperate: neither one alone carries the full meaning, but together they tell a small story.

### vocabulary_compounds (34.9s)

## Vocabulary Compounds

**Everyday example.** Think about how English builds words from smaller parts: "sun" + "flower" = "sunflower," "book" + "shelf" = "bookshelf." Each piece carries meaning, and together they create something new yet logical. Chinese works the same way — but it does this *all the time*.

**Picture this.** You already know the character 火 (fire) and the character 山 (mountain). Place them side by side — 火山 — and you get "volcano." That's a **compound word** (复合词, *fùhécí*): two or more characters joined to express a single idea. The meaning often follows naturally from its parts: fire + mountain = a mountain that produces fire.

Now let's learn one new character: 车 (vehicle). Watch how it combines with characters you already know:

| Characters | Literal parts | Compound meaning |
|---|---|---|
| 火 + 车 | fire + vehicle | train |
| 山 + 火 | mountain + fire | wildfire |

Picture the first trains, powered by steam and fire — a fire vehicle!

**Simple rule.** Most Chinese vocabulary is built by compounding. Once you recognize individual characters, you can often *guess* a compound's meaning — and you'll be right surprisingly often.

**Try it yourself.** You know 水 (water) and now 车 (vehicle). What do you think 水车 means? Hint: imagine a vehicle that moves water. What word does that give you?
