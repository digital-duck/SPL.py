### 宀 (31.6s)

## 宀

**Everyday example.** Think about what a roof does — it covers and protects everything underneath it. When it rains, you step inside and the roof keeps you dry. That simple idea of *shelter from above* is exactly what 宀 captures.

**Picture this.** Look at the shape: 宀. A short horizontal dot on top, then two strokes slanting down and outward like the sloped sides of a roof. Imagine a tiny house drawn with just three quick lines — no walls, no door, just the protective cap sitting on top. In Chinese, this component is called *mián* (pronounced roughly "mee-EN"), and it means **roof** or **shelter**.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 宀 sitting on top of another character, it usually signals something related to **a building, a home, or being enclosed and protected**. You don't need to know the parts underneath yet — just spotting the roof shape on top is enough. For example:

- 家 (jiā) means "family/home" — notice 宀 on top providing shelter
- 安 (ān) means "peace" — again, 宀 on top suggesting safety and protection
- 室 (shì) means "room" — 宀 covers the space below like a ceiling

The roof on top is your visual clue: *something is sheltered here*.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 字 (zì, meaning "character/word"). Can you spot 宀 on top? Cover the roof with your finger — what remains is a separate component you'll meet later. For now, just practise recognizing that familiar roofline whenever it appears.

### 女 (30.7s)

## 女

**Everyday example.** Think about the word "mother," "sister," or "daughter." Each of these roles begins with the same idea: a woman. In Chinese, one simple character captures that core meaning — 女 (nǚ), meaning *woman* or *female*.

**Picture this.** Imagine a person kneeling gracefully, arms crossed in front of the body. That is exactly what the ancient form of 女 depicted — a figure in a poised, seated posture. Over thousands of years the strokes simplified, but the flowing, curved shape still carries an echo of that original image. Notice the sweeping stroke that crosses through the middle; it gives the character its distinctive elegance.

**Simple rule.** 女 is one of the most common *radicals* — a radical is a building-block piece that appears inside hundreds of other characters to hint at their meaning. Whenever you spot 女 tucked into the left side of a character, it usually signals that the word relates to women, family roles, or qualities traditionally associated with femininity. You do not need to know the other pieces yet; just recognizing 女 inside a larger character is enough to give you a helpful clue about its meaning.

**Try it yourself.** Write 女 stroke by stroke: first the left-falling curved stroke, then the flat horizontal, then the right-falling stroke that crosses it. Now look at these characters — 她, 姐, 妹 — and find 女 hiding inside each one. Can you guess which means *she*, *older sister*, and *younger sister*?

### 子 (17.7s)

## 子

**Everyday example.** Think of a toddler bundled in a winter coat, arms stretched wide for a hug. That simple image — a small person reaching out — is exactly what the ancient Chinese captured when they drew the character 子.

**Picture this.** In its oldest form, 子 showed a baby with a large head and two little arms. Over centuries the strokes simplified, but the meaning stayed: *child, son, offspring*. Today you write it in just three strokes — a short horizontal dash, a curving hook downward, and a horizontal line crossing through. The big head on top and the body below still echo that original baby picture.

**Simple rule.** 子 carries two jobs in Chinese. As a standalone word it means "child" or "son" (儿子, *érzi*). As a building block inside other characters, it often signals something small, young, or related to people — for example, 好 ("good") pairs 女 (woman) with 子 (child), painting the idea that a mother and child together mean "good." Whenever you spot 子 tucked inside a more complex character, ask yourself: *where is the child in this meaning?*

**Try it yourself.** Write 子 three times slowly, paying attention to the hook stroke. Then look at these characters that contain 子: 学 (learn), 字 (written character), 孩 (child). Can you find where 子 hides in each one?

### 字 (16.9s)

## 字

**Everyday example.** When you read a stop sign or a text message, you recognise individual symbols — letters, digits, punctuation. Chinese works the same way, except each symbol is called a **字** (pronounced *zì*). A 字 is the basic written unit of Chinese: one character, one compact block of strokes, one chunk of meaning.

**Picture this.** Look at the character 字 itself. It has two visible parts stacked vertically: a small roof shape 宀 on top and the character 子 (*zǐ*, "child") underneath. Imagine a child sheltered under a roof — something born indoors, carefully raised. That image is the original metaphor: just as a family gives birth to children, language gives birth to *characters*. A 字 is a "child of language."

**Simple rule.** Every 字 is built from smaller pieces. The roof 宀 and the child 子 are components — reusable building blocks that appear inside many different characters. Learning to spot these components is like learning an alphabet: once you know a handful of common pieces, you can start reading — and remembering — hundreds of characters.

**Try it yourself.** Find the 子 component hiding inside 字. Now look at these three characters: 好, 学, 孩. Can you spot 子 in each one? Each time, 子 contributes a hint about pronunciation or meaning — your first clue that characters are not random pictures but structured combinations.

### 安 (18.5s)

## 安

**Everyday example.** Think about the feeling you get when you finally step inside your home after a long, rainy walk. The door closes behind you, the noise fades, and you let out a relieved sigh. That feeling — safe, settled, at ease — is exactly what 安 (*ān*) captures.

**Picture this.** The character 安 is built from two pieces you may already recognize. On top sits 宀, a small roof — the same roof radical that appears in characters about houses and shelters. Underneath rests 女 (*nǚ*), meaning *woman*. Imagine the simple scene: a person sheltered peacefully beneath a roof. That image is the character's entire story — **shelter + person = peace**.

**Simple rule.** Whenever you see 安, think **"safe and calm."** It appears in dozens of common words:

- 安全 (*ānquán*) — safety, security
- 平安 (*píng'ān*) — safe and sound
- 安静 (*ānjìng*) — quiet, tranquil

Notice the pattern: 安 always contributes the idea of *being free from worry or danger*.

**Try it yourself.** Look at the character 安 and trace its two parts with your finger: first the roof 宀 on top, then the figure 女 below. Say *ān* aloud — the open, relaxed vowel even *sounds* calm. Now try using it: how would you wish a traveller well? 一路平安 — *yī lù píng'ān* — "peace the whole journey."

### cultural_etymology (13.4s)

## Cultural Etymology

Every word carries a hidden history. When you say *"salary"*, you're echoing ancient Roman soldiers who were paid in salt — *salarium* in Latin. The word traveled through centuries, shedding its literal meaning but keeping its shape. **Cultural etymology** is the study of where words come from and how the cultures that used them left fingerprints on their meaning.

**Picture this.** A river flows through several countries. Each village along its banks has its own name for the river, shaped by what matters to them — fishing, flooding, or worship. Words work the same way: they pass through cultures, and each culture reshapes the word to fit its world.

**The simple rule:** a word's current meaning is the sum of every culture that touched it. To understand a word deeply, trace it backward — language by language, era by era — and notice what each group added or dropped.

This matters beyond linguistics. In mathematics, the word *"calculus"* comes from the Latin for "small pebble," because Romans counted with stones. Knowing this reminds you that even the most abstract ideas began as something you could hold in your hand.

**Try it yourself.** Pick three everyday words — perhaps *"digital,"* *"computer,"* or *"algorithm."* Look up their origins. For each one, write down the original meaning and the culture it came from. Notice how far each word has traveled from its first use. What does that journey tell you about how people think?
