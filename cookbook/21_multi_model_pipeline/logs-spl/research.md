**1. The Science of Climate Change – The Basics**

* **The Greenhouse Effect:** The Earth's atmosphere naturally traps some of the sun's energy, warming the planet. This is essential for life. However, human activities, primarily burning fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas), are increasing the concentration of greenhouse gases – primarily carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O) – in the atmosphere.
* **Rising Temperatures:**  The Earth's average surface temperature has increased by approximately 1.1°C (2.0°F) since the late 19th century.  Almost all of this warming is attributable to human influence.
* **Key Gases & Concentrations:**
    * **CO2:**  Currently at 419 parts per million (ppm) – the highest level in at least 800,000 years. Concentrations are rising at an unprecedented rate.
    * **Methane:**  A more potent greenhouse gas than CO2 over shorter timescales. Levels are increasing due to agriculture, natural gas production, and thawing permafrost.
    * **Nitrous Oxide:** Primarily from agricultural activities and industrial processes.


**2. Observed Impacts & Statistics (As of 2023)**

* **Global Temperature Increases:** 2023 is on track to be the hottest year on record, with global average temperatures exceeding 1.4°C above pre-industrial levels.
* **Sea Level Rise:** The global mean sea level has risen by approximately 21-27 centimeters (8-11 inches) since 1880. This is primarily due to thermal expansion of water as it warms and melting of glaciers and ice sheets.
* **Melting Ice:**
    * **Greenland & Antarctic Ice Sheets:** Losing mass at an accelerating rate.  Greenland is losing mass at a rate of approximately 280-520 billion tonnes per year.
    * **Arctic Sea Ice:**  The extent of Arctic sea ice has declined by roughly 13% per decade since 1979. Summer sea ice is declining even faster.
    * **Glaciers:**  Global glacier mass has decreased by approximately 1.13m over the past century.
* **Extreme Weather Events:**  There’s increasing evidence linking climate change to:
    * **Heatwaves:**  More frequent, intense, and longer-lasting heatwaves.
    * **Heavy Precipitation:**  Increased frequency and intensity of heavy rainfall events leading to flooding.
    * **Droughts:**  Prolonged droughts in many regions, exacerbated by rising temperatures and altered precipitation patterns.
    * **Wildfires:**  Longer wildfire seasons and larger, more intense wildfires, particularly in the western US, Australia, and Mediterranean.
* **Ocean Changes:**
    * **Ocean Warming:** The upper 2,000 meters of the ocean have warmed significantly, impacting marine ecosystems.
    * **Ocean Acidification:**  The absorption of excess CO2 by the oceans is causing them to become more acidic, threatening marine life, especially shell-forming organisms.


**3. Recent Developments & Projections (2023)**

* **IPCC Sixth Assessment Report (2021/2023):** This report unequivocally states that human influence is the dominant cause of observed warming since the mid-20th century. It highlights the urgency of reducing greenhouse gas emissions to limit warming to 1.5°C or better.
* **Carbon Removal Technologies:**  Growing research and development in carbon capture and storage (CCS) and direct air capture (DAC) technologies, though currently expensive and not deployed at scale.
* **Net-Zero Targets:**  Over 190 countries have set net-zero emission targets by 2050 or earlier. However, the pace of emission reductions is currently insufficient.
* **Climate Models:**  Continued refinement of climate models, leading to more accurate projections of future warming and regional impacts. The models increasingly show that even with significant emissions reductions, some level of warming and associated impacts are unavoidable.
* **2023 NOAA Report:** Released in September 2023, confirms 2023 is set to be the warmest year on record.
