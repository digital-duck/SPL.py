**The Wage-Price Spiral – A Deeper Dive:**

The wage-price spiral isn’t just an abstract economic theory; it represents a self-reinforcing cycle frequently observed in inflationary environments.  Here’s how it works, step by step:

1. **Initial Inflationary Pressure:** Let's consider some initial inflationary pressure—perhaps due to surging demand (as we’ve discussed) or a cost-push shock like rising oil prices. This leads to an increase in the price of goods and services.

2. **Workers Demand Higher Wages:** Workers notice these rising prices impacting their purchasing power and realize that their wages haven't kept pace with inflation. Consequently, they demand higher pay raises from employers – essentially, compensation for this erosion of income.  This is a natural human response: people naturally want to maintain their standard of living.

3. **Businesses Grant Wage Increases:** To retain employees and avoid losing them to competitors offering higher wages, businesses often concede to worker demands and grant wage increases. They may also raise prices to offset these increased labor costs.

4. **Prices Rise Further (Reinforcing the Spiral):** The newly granted wage increases boost overall demand in the economy because workers now have more disposable income. This increased demand puts further upward pressure on prices, as businesses respond by raising their own prices to capitalize on this higher demand – this is where the “spiral” truly begins.

5. **Workers Demand Even Higher Wages (Again):** With prices rising even *faster*, workers recognize that their wages are still inadequate and demand even larger increases. This creates a vicious cycle.

6. **The Cycle Continues:** Businesses respond to these escalating demands by raising prices again, fueling further wage demands, and so on. The spiral can accelerate rapidly if expectations of inflation become firmly entrenched in the minds of both workers and businesses.

**Why it's Difficult to Break:**

The core challenge with the wage-price spiral is that it becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. It’s not simply about supply and demand; it’s fundamentally driven by *expectations* shaping behavior. Central banks attempting to combat inflation through interest rate hikes can inadvertently exacerbate the spiral if they aren't careful, as higher rates also dampen economic activity, potentially prompting businesses to reduce wages or raise prices in response.

**Breaking the Spiral:**

Successfully breaking this cycle requires credible policy interventions—primarily by a central bank—to restore confidence that inflation will be brought under control. This frequently involves:

*   **Clear Communication:** The central bank must clearly communicate its commitment to price stability and detail how it plans to achieve that goal.
*   **Inflation Targeting:** Explicitly targeting a specific inflation rate can help anchor expectations.
*   **Monetary Policy Action:** Raising interest rates (as previously mentioned) can cool down demand, but this needs careful calibration to avoid triggering a recession.

**Response to Input 3: What causes inflation?**

Inflation, broadly defined as a sustained increase in the general price level of goods and services in an economy, is caused by several factors, often interacting with each other. Here’s a breakdown of the key drivers:

*   **Demand-Pull Inflation:** This occurs when there's too much money chasing too few goods.  Increased consumer spending, business investment, or government spending can all drive up demand beyond what the economy can supply at current prices.
*   **Cost-Push Inflation:** This happens when the costs of production for businesses increase.  This could be driven by rising raw material prices (like oil), wage increases, or supply chain disruptions. Businesses then pass these increased costs on to consumers in the form of higher prices.
*   **Built-In Inflation:**  This is a more persistent type of inflation that arises from the wage-price spiral we've discussed extensively. It happens when workers demand higher wages to compensate for expected future inflation, and businesses respond by raising prices accordingly – creating a self-sustaining cycle.
*   **Money Supply Growth:** When there’s an excessive increase in the amount of money circulating in the economy (without a corresponding increase in goods and services), it can lead to inflationary pressures.
