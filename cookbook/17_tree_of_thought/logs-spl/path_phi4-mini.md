To decide between rewriting an entire software application versus performing incremental improvements, it's essential first and foremost to understand its current condition. This calls upon a comprehensive analysis that includes technical reviews of code quality (such as through static analyzers), performance benchmarks against industry standards if applicable for your domain.

**Detailed Steps:**

1. **Lifecycle Analysis - Technical Audit & Cost Projection**
   - Conduct an in-depth audit using tools like SonarQube or CodeClimate to identify areas with high complexity, low cohesion and lack of modularity.
   - Evaluate the software architecture by assessing its scalability (can it handle increased loads?), maintainability (ease for new developers), testability (how easy is it unit testing) and security. Tools such as Pentaho Spotter can help in identifying vulnerabilities or misconfigurations.

2. **Risk Assessment with a Systems Engineering Mindset - Technical & Organizational**
   - Perform Failure Modes, Effects, and Criticality Analysis to pinpoint failure risks associated specifically within the software's functionality.
   - Assess organizational risk by evaluating employee skill levels for new technologies required if rewriting versus their capacity in optimizing existing systems through refactoring.

3. **Technical Debt Evaluation Using Modern Methodologies** 
   - Incorporate a Technical Debts Ledger into your project management tools (Jira, Trello) to keep track of identified debts.
   - Conduct an Agile retrospective session with the development team focused on this analysis and determine what technical debt can be resolved incrementally versus needing complete elimination through rewriting.

4. **Value Stream Mapping (VSM)** 
   - Map out all software-related processes including requirements gathering, design, coding/testing/deploying cycles using VSM.
   - Quantify waste in terms of time spent on repetitive tasks or debugging due to outdated systems and compare this with the potential savings from each option.

5. **Stakeholder Impact Analysis**  
   - Conduct interviews/surveys/questionnaires among key stakeholders (developers, QA team members) for their perspectives about both options.
   - Consider using tools like User Story Mapping software which can help visualize how different stakeholder groups interact and are affected by the system's performance as it currently is or after potential improvements.

6. **Resource Availability & Constraints Assessment**
   - Use a resource management tool (like Microsoft Project, Toggl) to track current resources' allocation towards maintenance efforts.
   - Estimate future availability of human capital for major refactoring versus continuous incremental updates which might require less upfront planning but demand consistent attention and adjustments overtime depending on the changes introduced.

7. **Incremental vs Big Bang Decision-Making Model (IMBM)** 
   - Evaluate costs, risks involved in both approaches using IMBM as a guideline.
   - Conduct simulations or use project management software to test outcomes under different scenarios of refactoring versus rewriting – considering factors like time-to-market and potential impact on existing systems.

8. **Strategic Alignment Check**
   - Align the decision with your organization's strategic goals; if quick market responsiveness is crucial, incremental updates may be favored over a major rewrite.
   
By incorporating these detailed steps into an overarching framework based around principles from both software engineering practices (like Agile) and Lean Six Sigma methodologies like VSM for waste reduction analysis alongside systems thinking perspectives that emphasize lifecycle view of complex problems can lead to more informed decisions about whether or not it makes sense - either in terms of practical feasibility, technical viability as well organizational alignment- –to rewrite your legacy system fully-or incrementally refactor it.
