## Semitone

In music theory, an interval defines the distance between two pitches. The semitone is defined as the smallest standard interval unit in Western equal temperament tuning. Mathematically, understanding the semitone requires modeling pitch not linearly by frequency, but logarithmically, as pitch perception relates to ratios rather than absolute differences. An octave represents a doubling of frequency (a ratio of $2:1$). In the system of twelve-tone equal temperament, this octave is divided into exactly twelve discrete, acoustically equal steps, each step being one semitone.

The relationship between two frequencies, $f_1$ and $f_2$, separated by an interval corresponding to $k$ semitones ($k \in \mathbb{Z}$), must satisfy the condition that when $k=12$, the ratio $\frac{f_2}{f_1} = 2$. ## Middle C

The pitch of a note is traditionally measured by its perceived frequency in Hertz ($\text{Hz}$), though scientifically speaking, it is defined by the oscillation rate of the sound wave. Middle C, designated as $\text{C}_4$ in scientific pitch notation (where the subscript indicates the octave, with $\text{C}_0$ being the lowest standard reference point), represents a specific fundamental frequency within the Western musical scale. Establishing this frequency requires referencing an agreed-upon tuning standard. The modern international standard specifies that the note A above middle C ($\text{A}_4$) must have a frequency of $f_{\text{ref}} = 440$ Hz.

The relationship between the pitch ($P$) of any note and its reference frequency is governed by the principle of equal temperament, which models the twelve semitones of an octave as geometrically progressing steps in frequency. If $\Delta n$ represents the number of semitones separating a target note from the reference note ($\text{A}_4$), the frequency $f$ of the target note is given by:
$$f = f_{\text{ref}} \cdot 2^{\frac{\Delta n}{12}}$$

The calculation confirms that Middle C ($\text{C}_4$) is precisely 9 semitones lower than $\text{A}_4$. Thus, substituting $f_{\text{ref}}=440$ Hz and $\Delta n = -9$:
$$f_{\text{C}4} = 440 \cdot 2^{\frac{-9}{12}} = 440 \cdot 2^{-3/4}$$

This calculation yields an approximate frequency of $261.63$ Hz, which is the accepted standard fundamental frequency for $\text{C}_4$.

**Worked Example: Calculating Frequency Displacement**
Consider a note $\text{G}\sharp_5$, which is 10 semitones above $\text{A}_4$. We use the established formula to find its precise frequency $f_{\text{G}\sharp}$. Here, $\Delta n = +10$:
$$f_{\text{G}\sharp} = 440 \cdot 2^{\frac{10}{12}} = 440 \cdot 2^{5/6}$$
Evaluating this expression gives $f_{\text{G}\sharp} \approx 739.99$ Hz. This exponential scaling demonstrates that the frequency difference between successive notes is multiplicative, not additive, forming a geometric progression across the octave structure.
