### angle (0.1s)

## Angle

**Definition.** Let $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ be two nonzero vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The *angle* $\theta$ between them is the unique value in $[0, \pi]$ satisfying

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|},$$

where $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i$ is the Euclidean inner product and $\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u}}$ is the Euclidean norm. This definition extends the familiar geometric notion of angle to arbitrary finite dimensions.

**Worked Example.** Let $\mathbf{u} = (1, 2, 2)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (4, 0, -3)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Compute:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = (1)(4) + (2)(0) + (2)(-3) = 4 + 0 - 6 = -2,$$

$$\|\mathbf{u}\| = \sqrt{1 + 4 + 4} = 3, \qquad \|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{16 + 0 + 9} = 5.$$

Therefore,

$$\cos\theta = \frac{-2}{3 \cdot 5} = -\frac{2}{15}, \qquad \theta = \arccos\!\left(-\tfrac{2}{15}\right) \approx 97.7°.$$

Since $\cos\theta < 0$, the vectors form an obtuse angle, which is geometrically consistent with $\mathbf{v}$ having a component directly opposing $\mathbf{u}$.

**Key Theorem (Cauchy–Schwarz Inequality).** For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|,$$

with equality if and only if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent. This inequality guarantees that the ratio $\dfrac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\,\|\mathbf{v}\|}$ always lies in $[-1, 1]$, making the definition of $\theta$ via $\arccos$ well-posed in any dimension.

*Proof sketch.* Consider $f(t) = \|\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v}\|^2 \geq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Expanding yields a quadratic in $t$ whose discriminant must be non-positive, giving $(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \leq \|\mathbf{u}\|^2\|\mathbf{v}\|^2$. $\square$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
import sympy as sp

u = sp.Matrix([1, 2, 2])
v = sp.Matrix([4, 0, -3])

cos_theta = u.dot(v) / (u.norm() * v.norm())
theta = sp.acos(cos_theta)
print(sp.simplify(theta))          # exact form
print(float(theta.evalf()) * 180 / float(sp.pi))  # degrees
```

### distance (0.3s)

## Distance

**Definition.** Let $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n)$ be two points in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The **(Euclidean) distance** between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is defined by

$$
d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^{n}(u_i - v_i)^2}.
$$

This quantity generalises the familiar ruler measurement on a number line to any finite dimension and arises naturally from the inner product: $d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v},\, \mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\rangle}$. Non-negativity ($d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) \geq 0$, with equality if and only if $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}$) and symmetry ($d(\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}) = d(\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u})$) follow immediately from the definition; both are left as short exercises.

**Worked Example.** Compute the distance between $\mathbf{u} = (1, 3, -2)$ and $\mathbf{v} = (4, -1, 2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

$$
\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v} = (1-4,\; 3-(-1),\; -2-2) = (-3,\; 4,\; -4).
$$

$$
d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \sqrt{(-3)^2 + 4^2 + (-4)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16 + 16} = \sqrt{41} \approx 6.403.
$$

**Key Theorem (Triangle Inequality).** For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$
d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w}) \leq d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) + d(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}).
$$

Geometrically, no side of a triangle is longer than the sum of the other two. This is the non-trivial metric axiom and the one with genuine content to prove.

*Proof sketch.* By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality, $|\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\rangle| \leq \|\mathbf{x}\|\,\|\mathbf{y}\|$. Set $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}$. Expanding $\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{w}\|^2 = \|(\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v})+(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w})\|^2$ and applying the inequality to the cross term yields $\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{w}\|^2 \leq \bigl(\|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}\|+\|\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{w}\|\bigr)^2$; taking square roots gives the result. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import Matrix, sqrt, simplify

u = Matrix([1, 3, -2])
v = Matrix([4, -1, 2])

diff = u - v
dist = sqrt(diff.dot(diff))
print("Distance:", simplify(dist))   # sqrt(41)
```

Running this cell confirms $d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \sqrt{41}$. Experiment by replacing the coordinates to build intuition for how each component contributes quadratically to the total separation.

### line (0.1s)

## Line

**Definition.** A *line* in the Euclidean plane $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a one-dimensional affine subspace. Formally, given a point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$ and a nonzero direction vector $\mathbf{d} = (d_1, d_2)$, the line $\ell$ is the set

$$\ell = \bigl\{(x_0 + t\,d_1,\; y_0 + t\,d_2) : t \in \mathbb{R}\bigr\}.$$

This is the **parametric form**. Eliminating $t$ by solving the first component for $t$ and substituting into the second yields the equivalent **implicit (Cartesian) form**

$$ax + by = c, \qquad (a,b) \neq (0,0),$$

where $a = d_2$, $b = -d_1$, and $c = d_2 x_0 - d_1 y_0$. The implicit form is often more convenient for checking whether a given point lies on the line: substitute the coordinates and verify the equality.

---

**Worked Example.** Find the equation of the line passing through $A = (1, 3)$ and $B = (4, 9)$.

*Direction vector:* $\mathbf{d} = B - A = (3, 6)$, so we may take $(d_1, d_2) = (1, 2)$.

*Parametric form* anchored at $A$:

$$(x, y) = (1 + t,\; 3 + 2t), \quad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$

*Implicit form:* $a = 2$, $b = -1$, $c = 2(1) - 1(3) = -1$, giving $2x - y = -1$, or equivalently $y = 2x + 1$.

Verification: $y(4) = 2(4) + 1 = 9$ ✓.

---

**Key Theorem (Unique Line Through Two Points).** *For any two distinct points $P, Q \in \mathbb{R}^2$, there exists exactly one line containing both.*

*Proof sketch.* Existence: take $\mathbf{d} = Q - P$ and use the parametric form above. Uniqueness: if two lines $\ell_1, \ell_2$ both contain $P$ and $Q$, their direction vectors are both parallel to $Q - P$, hence they share a point and a direction, so $\ell_1 = \ell_2$. $\square$

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy)**

```python
from sympy import symbols, solve, Rational

x, y = symbols('x y')

# Two points
x0, y0 = 1, 3
x1, y1 = 4, 9

# Direction vector components
d1, d2 = x1 - x0, y1 - y0          # (3, 6), simplified to (1, 2)

# Implicit form: a*x + b*y = c
a, b = d2, -d1
c = d2 * x0 - d1 * y0

implicit_eq = a*x + b*y - c
print(f"{a}*x + ({b})*y = {c}")     # 6*x + (-3)*y = -3, i.e. 2x - y = -1

# Solve for x given y = 7
sol = solve(implicit_eq.subs(y, 7), x)
print(f"x when y=7: {sol[0]}")      # x = 3
```

### point (0.1s)

## Point

**Definition.** A *point* is the most primitive object in Euclidean geometry: it has no dimension, no length, no area, and no volume. Formally, in $\mathbb{R}^n$, a point is an ordered $n$-tuple

$$P = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \in \mathbb{R}^n,$$

where each $x_i \in \mathbb{R}$ is called a *coordinate* of $P$ along the $i$-th axis. In the plane ($n = 2$) we write $P = (x, y)$; in space ($n = 3$) we write $P = (x, y, z)$. A point is location without extent — it satisfies Euclid's first postulate: *"A point is that which has no part."*

**Worked Example.** Let $P = (3, -1)$ and $Q = (7, 2)$ be two points in $\mathbb{R}^2$. The *distance* between them is given by the Euclidean metric

$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(7 - 3)^2 + (2 - (-1))^2} = \sqrt{16 + 9} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$$

Observe that $d(P, Q) = d(Q, P)$, and that $d(P, Q) = 0$ if and only if $P$ and $Q$ are the same point — properties that make the Euclidean metric a proper *metric* in the sense of analysis. The distance of 5 units here is exact; no rounding occurs because the radicand is a perfect square.

**Key Theorem (Distance Formula).** For any two points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$,

$$d(P, Q) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$

*Proof sketch.* Drop a perpendicular from $Q$ to the horizontal line through $P$, forming a right triangle with legs $|x_2 - x_1|$ and $|y_2 - y_1|$. The hypotenuse equals $d(P, Q)$; the result follows directly from the Pythagorean theorem. The formula extends to $\mathbb{R}^n$ by induction, adding one squared-difference term per additional coordinate. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import sqrt

x1, y1, x2, y2 = 3, -1, 7, 2
distance = sqrt((x2 - x1)**2 + (y2 - y1)**2)

print("Distance :", distance)   # 5
```

Running this cell confirms the computation symbolically, illustrating how SymPy handles exact arithmetic — returning the integer $5$ rather than the floating-point approximation $5.0$. This distinction matters whenever subsequent steps require algebraic manipulation of the result.

### angle_measure (0.1s)

## Angle Measure

**Definition.** An *angle* is formed by two rays sharing a common endpoint, called the *vertex*. The *measure* of an angle quantifies the amount of rotation from one ray (the initial side) to the other (the terminal side). Two standard units of measure are in common use:

- **Degrees:** A full rotation is divided into $360$ equal parts, so one *degree* ($1^\circ$) is $\tfrac{1}{360}$ of a full rotation.
- **Radians:** The radian measure of an angle $\theta$ is defined as the ratio

$$\theta = \frac{s}{r},$$

where $s$ is the arc length subtended on a circle of radius $r$ centered at the vertex. Since a full circle has circumference $2\pi r$, a complete rotation equals $2\pi$ radians.

The conversion between the two systems is given by:

$$\theta_{\text{rad}} = \frac{\pi}{180}\,\theta_{\deg}, \qquad \theta_{\deg} = \frac{180}{\pi}\,\theta_{\text{rad}}.$$

**Worked Example.** Convert $135^\circ$ to radians and determine the arc length subtended on a circle of radius $r = 4$.

$$\theta = 135 \times \frac{\pi}{180} = \frac{3\pi}{4} \text{ radians.}$$

The corresponding arc length is:

$$s = r\theta = 4 \cdot \frac{3\pi}{4} = 3\pi \approx 9.42.$$

**Key Theorem (Radian Measure and Arc Length).** *Let a central angle of measure $\theta$ (in radians) be inscribed in a circle of radius $r > 0$. Then the arc length $s$ subtended by the angle satisfies:*

$$s = r\theta.$$

*Proof sketch.* The arc length scales linearly with the fraction $\tfrac{\theta}{2\pi}$ of the full circle, giving $s = 2\pi r \cdot \tfrac{\theta}{2\pi} = r\theta$. $\blacksquare$

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
import sympy as sp

theta_deg = sp.Rational(135)
theta_rad = sp.pi * theta_deg / 180
r = 4

arc_length = r * theta_rad

print("Radians    :", theta_rad)          # 3*pi/4
print("Arc length :", arc_length)         # 3*pi
```

Running this cell confirms $s = 3\pi$, illustrating that radian measure makes the arc-length formula purely multiplicative — a central reason radians are the preferred unit in calculus and analysis. The sector area formula $A = \tfrac{1}{2}r^2\theta$ follows by the same proportionality argument and will be taken up in the next section alongside the unit circle.

### line_segment (0.1s)

## line_segment

**Definition.** A *line segment* is the convex hull of two distinct points in Euclidean space. Given points $P = (x_1, y_1)$ and $Q = (x_2, y_2)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the line segment $\overline{PQ}$ is the set

$$\overline{PQ} = \{ (1 - t)\,P + t\,Q \mid t \in [0, 1] \}.$$

The parameter $t$ traces the segment continuously from $P$ (at $t = 0$) to $Q$ (at $t = 1$). Each value of $t$ determines a unique interior or boundary point, so the parameterization is injective on $[0,1]$. The **length** of $\overline{PQ}$ is the Euclidean distance between its endpoints:

$$|\overline{PQ}| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}.$$

This formula extends naturally to $\mathbb{R}^n$ by summing squared coordinate differences across all $n$ dimensions.

---

**Worked Example.** Let $P = (1, 2)$ and $Q = (4, 6)$.

- *Length:* $|\overline{PQ}| = \sqrt{(4-1)^2 + (6-2)^2} = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5.$
- *Point at* $t = 0.25$:

$$\point = (1 - 0.25)(1, 2) + 0.25(4, 6) = (0.75 + 1,\; 1.5 + 1.5) = (1.75, 3).$$

This confirms that interior points are uniquely recovered by specifying $t \in (0,1)$, and that the parameterization distributes proportionally along the segment — the point at $t = 0.25$ lies exactly one-quarter of the way from $P$ to $Q$.

---

**Key Theorem (Triangle Inequality).** For any three points $A, B, C \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$|\overline{AC}| \;\leq\; |\overline{AB}| + |\overline{BC}|,$$

with equality if and only if $B$ lies on the segment $\overline{AC}$. *Proof sketch:* Apply the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality to the vectors $\mathbf{u} = B - A$ and $\mathbf{v} = C - B$, noting that $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$.

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import Point, Segment, Rational

P = Point(1, 2)
Q = Point(4, 6)
seg = Segment(P, Q)

print("Length  :", seg.length)                              # 5
print("At t=0.25:", seg.arbitrary_point(Rational(1, 4)))   # Point2D(7/4, 3)
```

Running this cell reproduces each quantity derived above and verifies the parameterization symbolically, bridging geometric intuition with computational confirmation.

### polygon (0.1s)

## Polygon

**Definition.** A *polygon* is a closed plane figure formed by a finite sequence of line segments—called *sides* or *edges*—such that each segment shares exactly one endpoint with the next, and the last segment connects back to the first. Formally, a polygon $P$ with $n$ sides ($n \geq 3$) is determined by an ordered set of *vertices* $\{V_1, V_2, \ldots, V_n\} \subset \mathbb{R}^2$, with edges $\overline{V_1V_2},\, \overline{V_2V_3},\, \ldots,\, \overline{V_{n-1}V_n},\, \overline{V_nV_1}$. A polygon is called *simple* if no two non-adjacent edges intersect; all results in this section assume simplicity.

---

**Worked Example.** Consider the regular hexagon with center at the origin and circumradius $R = 2$. Its six vertices are given by

$$V_k = \left(2\cos\!\left(\frac{2\pi(k-1)}{6}\right),\; 2\sin\!\left(\frac{2\pi(k-1)}{6}\right)\right), \quad k = 1, 2, \ldots, 6.$$

Computing explicitly: $V_1 = (2, 0)$, $V_2 = (1, \sqrt{3})$, $V_3 = (-1, \sqrt{3})$, and so on. The side length equals $s = R = 2$, and the interior angle at each vertex is $\dfrac{(6-2)\cdot 180^\circ}{6} = 120^\circ$.

---

**Key Theorem (Interior Angle Sum).** For any simple polygon with $n$ vertices, the sum of interior angles is

$$\sum_{i=1}^{n} \theta_i = (n - 2)\cdot \pi \text{ radians} = (n-2)\cdot 180^\circ.$$

*Proof sketch.* Triangulate the polygon by drawing $n - 3$ non-crossing diagonals from a single vertex, producing exactly $n - 2$ triangles. Since each triangle's angles sum to $\pi$, and every triangle angle is an interior angle of $P$, the total is $(n-2)\pi$. $\square$

The area of a simple polygon whose vertices are listed in order is computed via the *Shoelace Formula*:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^{n}\bigl(x_i\, y_{i+1} - x_{i+1}\, y_i\bigr)\right|,$$

where indices are taken modulo $n$.

---

**Lab Cell (SymPy).**

```python
from sympy import cos, sin, pi, simplify, Polygon, Point

# Regular hexagon with circumradius R = 2
R, n = 2, 6
vertices = [Point(R*cos(2*pi*k/n), R*sin(2*pi*k/n)) for k in range(n)]
hex_poly = Polygon(*vertices)

print("Area:              ", simplify(hex_poly.area))       # 6√3
print("Perimeter:         ", simplify(hex_poly.perimeter))  # 12
print("Interior angle sum:", (n - 2) * 180, "degrees")      # 720
```

Running this confirms $A = 6\sqrt{3} \approx 10.39$ and perimeter $= 12$, consistent with the formula $A = \tfrac{3\sqrt{3}}{2}s^2$ for a regular hexagon of side $s = 2$.
