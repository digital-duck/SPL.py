Allomorphy

Allomorphy refers to a branch of syntax that studies the distribution and patterning of allomorphs, which are forms of words or morphemes that differ from one another in their phonological or orthographic realizations.

Definition

An allomorph is a form of a morpheme (a unit of meaning) that differs from others in its form, often due to changes in pronunciation, spelling, or both. Allomorphy can be observed in various languages, including English, where it is manifested through the use of different forms of words such as "bit" (the past tense of "bite") and "beat" (a verb meaning to strike or hit).

Worked Example

Consider the English word "go". In its base form, the morpheme /g/ has two allomorphic variants: [ɡ] (as in "gold") and [ɔː] (as in "go"). The first variant is used before vowels, while the second variant is used before consonants. Using this information, we can sketch a possible proof of the allomorphy of /g/, which would involve demonstrating that there exists a non-constant function mapping the set of vowels onto the real numbers such that for each vowel, the corresponding value in the image of this function differs from the value in the image of another vowel.

Key Theorem

The Law of Allomorphy states that if a morpheme has allomorphic variants, then there exists an allomorphical hierarchy, where each level of the hierarchy corresponds to a particular set of allomorphs. This theorem provides a foundation for understanding the distribution and patterning of allomorphs in languages.
