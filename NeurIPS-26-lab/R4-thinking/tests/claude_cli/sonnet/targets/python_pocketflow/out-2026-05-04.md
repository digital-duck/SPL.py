The Fibonacci sequence (1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 21, ...) is important for several interconnected reasons. First, it appears naturally in biological growth patterns — the spirals of sunflower seeds, pinecone scales, and nautilus shells follow Fibonacci numbers. This suggests the sequence encodes an efficient packing and growth strategy that evolution has converged on. Second, the ratio of consecutive Fibonacci numbers converges to the golden ratio (φ ≈ 1.618), which has deep connections to geometry, aesthetics, and self-similar structures. Third, in mathematics, Fibonacci numbers appear in combinatorics (counting tilings, paths in graphs), number theory (every positive integer is a sum of distinct Fibonacci numbers — Zeckendorf's theorem), and linear algebra (the recurrence has a closed-form solution via matrix exponentiation). Fourth, in computer science, Fibonacci heaps, Fibonacci search, and the sequence itself serve as a canonical benchmark for recursion, dynamic programming, and memoization pedagogy. Fifth, in financial markets, Fibonacci retracement levels are widely used in technical analysis, reflecting human pattern-seeking behavior even if the mathematical basis is debated.
