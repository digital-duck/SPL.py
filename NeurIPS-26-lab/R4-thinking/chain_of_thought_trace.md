  SHEEP ANSWER (confirmed): The farmer has 9 sheep left. "All but 9 die" = all except 9 die = 9 survive. The trick is linguistic, not mathematical.

  COMPOUND INTEREST — STEP-BY-STEP:

  Step 1 — The Core Mechanic:
  Simple interest pays you on your principal only. If you deposit $1,000 at 8%, you earn $80/year forever — a flat line.
  Compound interest pays you on your principal PLUS all previously earned interest. In year 1 you earn $80. In year 2 you earn interest on $1,080, so $86.40. In year 3, on $1,166.40, earning $93.31. Each period's base is larger than the last. This is exponential growth.

  Step 2 — Numeric Example ($1,000 at 8% for 30 years):
  - Simple interest: $1,000 + (1,000 × 0.08 × 30) = $1,000 + $2,400 = $3,400
  - Compound (annual, formula A = P(1+r)^t): $1,000 × (1.08)^30 = $1,000 × 10.0627 ≈ $10,063
  - Difference: $10,063 vs $3,400 — compounding yields nearly 3× more. The "extra" $6,663 came from earning returns on returns.

  Step 3 — Rule of 72 (practical shortcut):
  Divide 72 by the annual interest rate to estimate how many years it takes to double your money.
  - At 8%: 72 / 8 = 9 years to double.
  - At 6%: 72 / 6 = 12 years.
  - At 12%: 72 / 12 = 6 years.
  This rule works because ln(2) ≈ 0.693, and 72 is a convenient close approximation that also has many integer divisors. It lets investors quickly compare investment options without a calculator.

  Step 4 — Compounding Frequency Matters:
  The formula A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) shows that more frequent compounding increases returns.
  - Annual (n=1): $1,000 × (1.08)^30 = $10,063
  - Monthly (n=12): $1,000 × (1 + 0.08/12)^360 = $1,000 × (1.006667)^360 ≈ $10,935
  - Daily (n=365): ≈ $11,020
  The difference between monthly and annual compounding over 30 years is ~$872 on $1,000 — meaningful but not transformative. The bigger lever is always time and rate, not frequency alone.

  Step 5 — Why It Matters for Long-Term Investing:
  (a) Time is the dominant variable. Compare two investors, both contributing $5,000/year at 8%:
    - Investor A starts at 25, stops at 35 (10 years, $50,000 total), then lets it ride to 65.
    - Investor B starts at 35, contributes every year to 65 (30 years, $150,000 total).
    Investor A ends with ~$615,000. Investor B ends with ~$566,000. A wins despite contributing 1/3 as much — because time in the market compounds more powerfully than contribution size.
  (b) Reinvesting dividends: stocks that pay dividends, when reinvested, buy more shares that generate more dividends — a compounding loop on top of price appreciation.
  (c) Tax-advantaged accounts (401k, IRA, Roth IRA): by deferring or eliminating taxes on gains, the full amount compounds uninterrupted. Paying taxes annually on gains removes capital that would otherwise compound — a hidden drag on returns.
  (d) Inflation note: compounding works against you too when you owe money (debt) or when inflation erodes purchasing power. The same mechanism that builds wealth also destroys it if you're on the wrong side.

  KEY INSIGHT: The exponential curve starts flat and ends steep. Most of the wealth is created in the final years. In the $1,000/8%/30-year example, the last 10 years generate more money than the first 20 combined. This is why "start early, don't stop" dominates all other investing advice — you cannot buy back time, and the cost of delay is not linear, it's exponential.
